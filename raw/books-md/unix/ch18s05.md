<div>

## <span id="id3002482"></span>DocBook

</div>

</div>

A great many major open-source projects are converging on DocBook as a
standard format for their documentation. The advocates of XML-based
markup seem to have won the theoretical argument against
presentation-level and for structural-level markup, and an effective
XML-DocBook toolchain is available in open source.

Nevertheless, a lot of confusion still surrounds DocBook and the
programs that support it. Its devotees speak an argot that is dense and
forbidding even by computer-science standards, slinging around acronyms
that have no obvious relationship to the things you need to do to write
markup and make HTML or PostScript from it. XML standards and technical
papers are notoriously obscure. In the rest of this section, we'll try
to dispel the fog of jargon.

<div class="sect2" lang="en">

<div class="titlepage">

<div>

### <span id="id3006015"></span>Document Type Definitions

</div>

</div>

(Note: to keep the explanation simple, most of this section tells some
lies, mainly by omitting a lot of history. Truthfulness will be fully
restored in a following section.)

DocBook is a structural-level markup language. Specifically, it is a
dialect of XML. A DocBook document is a piece of XML that uses XML tags
for structural markup.

For a document formatter to apply a stylesheet to your document and make
it look good, it needs to know things about the overall structure of
your document. For example, in order to physically format chapter
headers properly, it needs to know that a book manuscript normally
consists of front matter, a sequence of chapters, and back matter. In
order for it to know this sort of thing, you need to give it a *Document
Type Definition*<span id="id3006049" class="indexterm"></span> or DTD.
The DTD tells your formatter what sorts of elements can be in the
document structure, and in what order they can appear.

What we mean by calling DocBook a ‘dialect’ of XML is actually that
DocBook is a DTD — a rather large DTD, with somewhere around 400 tags in
it.<sup>\[<a href="ch18s05.html#ftn.id3006067" id="id3006067">152</a>\]</sup>

Lurking behind DocBook is a kind of program called a *validating
parser*<span id="id3006082" class="indexterm"></span>. When you format a
DocBook document, the first step is to pass it through a validating
parser (the front end of the DocBook formatter). This program checks
your document against the DocBook DTD to make sure you aren't breaking
any of the DTD's structural rules (otherwise the back end of the
formatter, the part that applies your stylesheet, might become quite
confused).

The validating parser will either throw an error, giving you messages
about places where the document structure is broken, or translate the
document into a stream of XML elements and text that the parser back end
combines with the information in your stylesheet to produce formatted
output.

diagrams the whole process.

<div>

### <span id="id3006182"></span>Other DTDs

</div>

</div>

A brief digression into other DTDs may help clarify what parts of the
previous section are specific to DocBook and what parts are general to
all structural-markup languages.

<a href="http://www.tei-c.org/" target="_top">TEI</a> (Text Encoding
Initiative) is a large, elaborate DTD used primarily in academia for
computer transcription of literary texts. TEI's Unix-based toolchains
use many of the same tools that are involved with DocBook, but with
different stylesheets and (of course) a different DTD.

XHTML, the latest version of HTML, is also an XML application described
by a DTD, which explains the family resemblance between XHTML and
DocBook tags. The XHTML toolchain consists of Web browsers that can
format HTML as flat ASCII, together with any of a number of ad-hoc
HTML-to-print utilities.

Many other XML DTDs are maintained to help people exchange structured
information in fields as diverse as bioinformatics and banking. You can
look at a
<a href="http://www.xml.com/pub/rg/DTD_Repositories" target="_top">list
of repositories</a> to get some idea of the variety available.

</div>

<div>

### <span id="db_toolchain"></span>The DocBook Toolchain

</div>

</div>

<span id="id3006245" class="indexterm"></span>

Normally, what you'll do to make XHTML from your DocBook sources is use
the xmlto(1) front end. Your commands will look like this:

``` screen
bash$ xmlto xhtml foo.xml
bash$ ls *.html
ar01s02.html ar01s03.html ar01s04.html index.html
```

In this example, you converted an XML-DocBook document named `foo.xml`
with three top-level sections into an index page and two parts. Making
one big page is just as easy:

``` screen
bash$ xmlto xhtml-nochunks foo.xml
bash$ ls *.html
foo.html
```

Finally, here is how you make PostScript for printing:

``` screen
bash$ xmlto ps foo.xml       # To make PostScript
bash$ ls *.ps
foo.ps
```

To turn your documents into HTML or PostScript, you need an engine that
can apply the combination of DocBook DTD and a suitable stylesheet to
your document.
[Figure 18.2](ch18s05.html#docfig2 "Figure 18.2. Present-day XML-DocBook toolchain.")
illustrates how the open-source tools for doing this fit together.

<div class="figure">

<span id="docfig2"></span>

**Figure 18.2. Present-day XML-DocBook toolchain.**

<div class="mediaobject">

![Present-day XML-DocBook toolchain.](graphics/docflow2.png)

</div>

</div>

Parsing your document and applying the stylesheet transformation will be
handled by one of three programs. The most likely one is
*xsltproc*<span id="id3006388" class="indexterm"></span>, the parser
that ships with Red Hat Linux<span id="id3006397"
class="indexterm"></span>. The other possibilities are two
Java<span id="id3006409" class="indexterm"></span> programs, *Saxon* and
*Xalan*.

It is relatively easy to generate high-quality XHTML from either
DocBook; the fact that XHTML is simply another XML DTD helps a lot.
Translation to HTML is done by applying a rather simple stylesheet, and
that's the end of the story. RTF is also simple to generate in this way,
and from XHTML or RTF it's easy to generate a flat ASCII text
approximation in a pinch.

The awkward case is print. Generating high-quality printed output —
which means, in practice, Adobe's PDF<span id="id3006449"
class="indexterm"></span> (Portable Document Format) — is difficult.
Doing it right requires algorithmically duplicating the delicate
judgments of a human typesetter moving from content to presentation
level.

So, first, a stylesheet translates DocBook's structural markup into
another dialect of XML — FO<span id="id3006466"
class="indexterm"></span> (Formatting Objects). FO markup is very much
presentation-level; you can think of it as a sort of XML functional
equivalent of *troff*. It has to be translated to PostScript for
packaging in a PDF.

In the toolchain shipped with Red Hat Linux<span id="id3006490"
class="indexterm"></span>, this job is handled by a *TeX* macro package
called *PassiveTeX*<span id="id3006519" class="indexterm"></span>. It
translates the formatting objects generated by **xsltproc** into Donald
Knuth's<span id="id3006535" class="indexterm"></span> *TeX* language.
*TeX*'s output, known as DVI<span id="id3006563"
class="indexterm"></span> (DeVice Independent) format, is then massaged
into PDF<span id="id3006573" class="indexterm"></span>.

If you think this bucket chain of XML to *TeX* macros to DVI to PDF
sounds like an awkward kludge, you're right. It clanks, it wheezes, and
it has ugly warts. Fonts are a significant problem, since XML and *TeX*
and PDF have very different models of how fonts work; also, handling
internationalization and localization is a nightmare. About the only
thing this code path has going for it is that it works.

The elegant way will be FOP<span id="id3006615"
class="indexterm"></span>, a direct FO-to-PostScript translator being
developed by the Apache<span id="id3006625" class="indexterm"></span>
project. With FOP, the internationalization problem is, if not solved,
at least well confined; XML tools handle Unicode all the way through to
FOP. The mapping from Unicode glyphs to Postscript font is also strictly
FOP's problem. The only trouble with this approach is that it doesn't
work — yet. As of mid-2003, FOP is in an unfinished alpha state —
usable, but with rough edges and missing features.

[Figure 18.3](ch18s05.html#docfig3 "Figure 18.3. Future XML-DocBook toolchain with FOP.")
illustrates what the FOP toolchain looks like.

<div class="figure">

<span id="docfig3"></span>

**Figure 18.3. Future XML-DocBook toolchain with FOP.**

<div class="mediaobject">

![Future XML-DocBook toolchain with FOP.](graphics/docflow3.png)

</div>

</div>

FOP has competition. Another project called *xsl-fo-proc* aims to do the
same things as FOP, but in C++<span id="id3006712"
class="indexterm"></span> (and therefore both faster than
Java<span id="id3006722" class="indexterm"></span> and not relying on
the Java environment). As of mid-2003, *xsl-fo-proc* is in an unfinished
alpha state, not as far along as FOP.

</div>

<div>

### <span id="id3006742"></span>Migration Tools

</div>

</div>

The second biggest problem with DocBook is the effort needed to convert
old-style presentation markup to DocBook markup. Human beings can
usually parse the presentation of a document into logical structure
automatically, because (for example) they can tell from context when an
italic font means ‘emphasis’ and when it means something else such as
‘this is a foreign phrase’.

Somehow, in converting documents to DocBook, those sorts of distinctions
need to be made explicit. Sometimes they're present in the old markup;
often they are not, and the missing structural information has to be
either deduced by clever heuristics or added by a human.

Here is a summary of the state of conversion tools from various other
formats. None of these do a completely perfect job; inspection and
perhaps a bit of hand-editing by a human being will be needed after
conversion.

<div class="variablelist">

<span class="term">GNU Texinfo</span>  
The Free Software Foundation<span id="id3006792"
class="indexterm"></span> intends to support DocBook as an interchange
format. Texinfo has enough structure to make reasonably good automatic
conversion possible (human editing is still needed afterwards, but not
much of it), and the 4.x versions of *makeinfo* feature a `--docbook`
switch that generates DocBook. More at the
<a href="http://www.gnu.org/directory/texinfo.html"
target="_top"><em>makeinfo</em> project page</a>.

<span class="term">POD</span>  
A <a href="http://www.cpan.org/modules/by-module/Pod/"
target="_top">POD::DocBook</a> module translates Plain Old Documentation
markup to DocBook. It claims to translate every POD tag except the L\<\>
italic tag. The man page also says “Nested =over/=back lists are not
supported within DocBook”, but notes that the module has been heavily
tested.

<span class="term">*LaTeX*</span>  
A project called <a
href="http://www.lrz-muenchen.de/services/software/sonstiges/tex4ht/mn.html"
target="_top">TeX4ht</a> can, according to the author of Passive*TeX*,
generate DocBook from *LaTeX*.

<span class="term">man pages and other *troff*-based markups</span>  
These are generally considered the biggest and nastiest conversion
problems. And indeed, the basic troff(1) markup is at too low a
presentation level for automatic conversion tools to do much of any
good. However, the gloom in the picture lightens significantly if we
consider translation from sources of documents written in macro packages
like man(7). These have enough structural features for automatic
translation to get some traction.

I wrote a tool to do troff-to-DocBook myself, because I couldn't find
anything else that did a tolerable job of it. It's called
<a href="http://www.catb.org/~esr/doclifter/"
target="_top">doclifter</a>. It will translate to either SGML or XML
DocBook from man(7), mdoc(7), ms(7), or me(7) macros. See the
documentation for details.

</div>

</div>

<div>

### <span id="id3007022"></span>Editing Tools

</div>

</div>

One thing we do not have in mid-2003 is a good open-source structure
editor for SGML/XML documents.

<a href="http://www.lyx.org/" target="_top">LyX</a> is a GUI word
processor that uses *LaTeX* for printing and supports structural editing
of *LaTeX* markup. There is a *LaTeX* package that generates DocBook,
and a
<a href="http://bgu.chez.tiscali.fr/doc/db4lyx/" target="_top">how-to
document</a> describing how to write SGML and XML in the LyX GUI.

<a href="http://www.math.u-psud.fr/~anh/TeXmacs/TeXmacs.html"
target="_top">GNU <em>TeX</em>Macs</a> is a project aimed at producing
an editor that is good for technical and mathematical material,
including displayed formulas. 1.0 was released in April 2002. The
developers plan XML support in the future, but it's not there yet.

Most people still hack DocBook tags by hand using either *vi* or
*emacs*.

</div>

### <span id="id3007143"></span>Related Standards and Practices

</div>

</div>

The tools are coming together, if slowly, to edit and format DocBook
markup. But DocBook itself is a means, not an end. We'll need other
standards besides DocBook itself to accomplish the
searchable-documentation-database objective. There are two big issues:
document cataloging and metadata.

The <a href="http://scrollkeeper.sourceforge.net/"
target="_top">ScrollKeeper</a> project aims directly to meet this need.
It provides a simple set of script hooks that can be used by package
install and uninstall productions to register and unregister their
documentation.

ScrollKeeper uses the
<a href="http://www.ibiblio.org/osrt/omf/" target="_top">Open Metadata
Format</a>. This is a standard for indexing open-source documentation
analogous to a library card-catalog system. The idea is to support rich
search facilities that use the card-catalog metadata as well as the
source text of the documentation itself.

</div>

<div>

### <span id="id3007192"></span>SGML

</div>

</div>

In previous sections, we have deliberately omitted a lot of DocBook's
history. XML has an older brother, Standard Generalized Markup Language
(SGML<span id="id3007203" class="indexterm"></span>).

Until mid-2002, no discussion of DocBook would have been complete
without a long excursion into SGML, the differences between SGML and
XML, and detailed descriptions of the SGML DocBook toolchain. Life can
be simpler now; an XML DocBook toolchain is available in open source,
works as well as the SGML toolchain ever did, and is easier to use.

</div>

<div>

### <span id="id3007297"></span>XML-DocBook References

</div>

</div>

One of the things that makes learning DocBook difficult is that the
sites related to it tend to overwhelm the newbie with long lists of W3C
standards, massive exercises in SGML theology, and dense thickets of
abstract terminology. See *XML in a Nutshell*
\[[Harold-Means](apb.html#Harold-Means "[Harold-Means]")\] for a good
book-length general introduction.

Norman Walsh's *DocBook: The Definitive Guide* is available
<a href="http://www.oreilly.com/catalog/docbook/" target="_top">in
print</a> and <a href="http://www.docbook.org/tdg/en/html/docbook.html"
target="_top">on the Web</a>. This is indeed the definitive reference,
but as an introduction or tutorial it's a disaster. Instead, read this:

<a href="http://xml.web.cern.ch/XML/goossens/dbatcern/"
target="_top">Writing Documents Using DocBook</a>. This is an excellent
tutorial.

There is an equally excellent
<a href="http://www.dpawson.co.uk/docbook/" target="_top">DocBook
FAQ</a> with a lot of material on styling HTML output. There is also a
DocBook
<a href="http://docbook.org/wiki/moin.cgi" target="_top">wiki</a>.

Finally, the
<a href="http://xml.coverpages.org/" target="_top">The XML Cover
Pages</a> will take you into the jungle of XML standards if you really
want to go there.
