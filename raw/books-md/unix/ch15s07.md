## <span id="id2989334"></span>Profiling

</div>

</div>

As a general rule, 90% of the execution time of your program will be
spent in 10% of its code. Profilers are tools that help you identify the
10% of hot spots that constrain the speed of your program. This is a
good thing for making it faster.

But in the Unix tradition, profilers have a far more important function.
They enable you <span class="emphasis">*not*</span> to optimize the
other 90%! This is good, and not just because it saves you work. The
<span class="emphasis">*really*</span> valuable effect is that not
optimizing that 90% holds down global complexity and reduces bugs.

You may recall that we quoted Donald Knuth<span id="id2989368"
class="indexterm"></span> observing “Premature optimization is the root
of all evil”<span id="id2989380" class="indexterm"></span> in
[Chapter 1](philosophychapter.html "Chapter 1. Philosophy"), and that
Rob Pike<span id="id2989400" class="indexterm"></span> and Ken
Thompson<span id="id2989408" class="indexterm"></span> had a few pungent
observations on the topic as well. These were the voices of experience.
Do good design. Think about what's <span class="emphasis">*right*</span>
first. Tune for efficiency later.

Profilers help you do this. If you get in the good habit of using them,
you can get rid of the bad habit of premature optimization. Profilers
don't just change the way you work; they change how you think.

Profilers for compiled languages rely on instrumenting object code, so
they are even more platform-dependent than compilers. On the other hand,
a compiled-language profiler doesn't care about the source language of
the programs it instruments. Under Unix, the single profiler gprof(1)
handles C<span id="id2989450" class="indexterm"></span>,
C++<span id="id2989458" class="indexterm"></span>, and all other
compiled languages.

Perl<span id="id2989471" class="indexterm"></span>,
Python<span id="id2989480" class="indexterm"></span>, and Emacs
Lisp<span id="id2989488" class="indexterm"></span> have their own
profilers included in their basic distributions; these are portable
across all platforms on which the host languages themselves run.
Java<span id="id2989500" class="indexterm"></span> has built-in
profiling. Tcl<span id="id2989508" class="indexterm"></span> has no
profiling support as yet.
