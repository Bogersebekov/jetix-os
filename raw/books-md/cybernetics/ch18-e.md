<td><p><strong>The bald concept of evolution</strong> is so powerful and
universal that at times it seems to touch everything. The mystical
archeologist Teilhard de Chardin wrote:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Is evolution a theory, a system, or a hypothesis? It is much more --
it is a general postulate to which all theories, all hypotheses, all
systems must henceforth bow and which they must satisfy in order to be
thinkable and true. Evolution is a light which illuminates all facts, a
trajectory which all lines of thought must follow -- this is what
evolution is.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Evolution's role to explain everything, however, stains it with a
tinge of religiosity. As Bob Crosby of the Washington Evolutionary
Systems Society unabashedly says, "Where other people see the hand of
God, we see evolution."</p>
<p>Much can be said of viewing evolution as a religion. Evolution
theory's framework is encompassing, rich, almost self-evident,
inarguable, and it has now spawned local home fellowships that meet
monthly, as Crosby's large group does. Author Mary Midgley begins her
slim and wonderful monograph Evolution as a Religion, with these four
sentences: "The theory of evolution is not just an inert piece of
theoretical science. It is, and cannot help being, also a powerful
folk-tale about human origins. Any narrative must have symbolic force.
We are probably the first culture not to make that its main
function."</p>
<p>Her arguments are not against the veracity of evolutionary theory in
the least, but rather against the idea that we can divorce the logical
aspects of evolution from all the other things this powerful notion does
to us as humans.</p>
<p>It is the unexamined consequences of evolution -- however it comes
about, and wherever it is headed -- that I believe will shape our future
in the long term. I don't doubt that our discoveries about the hidden
nature of deep evolution will also touch our souls.</p>
