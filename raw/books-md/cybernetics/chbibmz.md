<td><dl>
<dt>Macbeth, Norman. <em>Darwin Retried</em>. Gambit Incorporated,
1971.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A fair "trial" of the evidence for Darwinian
evolution. Short, but effective. Tends to highlight the discrepancies,
but offers no alternatives.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Maes, Pattie. "How to do the Right Thing." <em>Connection
Science</em>, 1; 3, 1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Discusses an algorithm for robotic intelligence
which will bias choice of action in certain directions as an ongoing
"plan."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>----. "Situated Agents Can Have Goals." <em>Robotics and Autonomous
Systems</em>, 6; 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">How functional goals can emerge from a mass of
simple rules in robots.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Malone, Thomas W., Joanne Yates, and Robert I. Benjamin. "Electronic
Markets and Electronic Hierarchies." <em>Communications of the ACM</em>,
30; 6, 1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">How increased use of cheap coordination technology
will shift the economy away from hierarchical forms to market networks.
Excellent paper.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Mann, Charles. "Lynn Margulis: Science's Unruly Earth Mother."
<em>Science</em>, 252; 19 April 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An entertaining account of mainstream evolutionary
biologists' reaction to Margulis's ideas.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Margalef, Ramon. <em>Perspectives in Ecological Theory</em>. The
University of Chicago Press, 1968.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The best treatment of ecosystems as cybernetic
systems.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Margulis, Lynn, and Rene Fester, eds. <em>Symbiosis as a Source of
Evolutionary Innovation: Speciation and Morphogenesis</em>. The MIT
Press, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Lots of case studies on symbiotic relations. A few
good chapters on reevaluating symbiosis' role in evolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Markoff, John. "The Creature That Lives in Pittsburgh." <em>The New
York Times</em>, April 21, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">About Ambler, the huge semismart walking robot built
by CMU in Pittsburgh.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>May, Robert M. "Will a Large Complex System be Stable?"
<em>Nature</em>, 238; 18 August 1972.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An early mathematical demonstration that showed that
beyond a critical value, complexity unstabilizes a system.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Mayo, Oliver. <em>Natural Selection and its Constraints</em>.
Academic Press, 1983.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">This extremely technical book treats the genetic
constraints on natural selection very seriously. Mayo asserts the
constraints create narrow boundaries for evolution. He also dabbles with
some alternative theories, which he woefully concludes cannot replace
the current theory.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Mayr, Ernst. <em>Toward a New Philosophy of Biology</em>. The
Belknap Press of Harvard University Press, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Mayr is the arch-orthodox Darwinian. Not only did he
cofound the Modern Synthesis of Neo-Darwinism, he remains its most
dogmatic defender. Yet, he proposed what later became the bad-boy idea
of punk-eek twenty years before Gould, and in this book he makes a
strong case for radical, cohesive constraints of the gene.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Mayr, Otto. <em>The Origins of Feedback Control</em>. MIT Press,
1969.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A readable history of ancient servomechanisms and
modern mechanical feedback devices, including one invented by the
author's father.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>-----. <em>Authority, Liberty &amp; Automatic Machinery in Early
Modern Europe</em>. John Hopkins University Press, 1986.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">How the metaphors of control shaped and were shaped
by the technologies of control.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Mazlish, Bruce. <em>The Fourth Discontinuity: The Coevolution of
Humans and Machines.</em> Yale University Press, 1993.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An excellent, penetrating history of the bionic
convergence and its philosophical consequences. If this book had been
published earlier, I would have borrowed much from it; but it came out
as mine was being wrapped up.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>McCulloch, Warren S. <em>An Account of the First Three Conferences
on Teleological Mechanisms</em>. Josiah Macy, Jr. Foundation,
1947.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A dense summary of the first three Macy conferences,
which covered an amazing range of topics, all before they hit upon the
term "cybernetics."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>McKenna, Michael, Steve Pieper, and David Zeltzer. "Control of a
Virtual Actor: The Roach." <em>Computer Graphics</em>, 24; 2,
1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">How to direct a virtual roach to walk where you want
it to within a virtual environment.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>McShea, Daniel W. "Complexity and Evolution: What Everybody Knows."
<em>Biology and Philosophy</em>, 6; 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A wonderful review of historical notions of
increasing complexity in biological evolution ("what everybody knows"),
and the author's own evidence against the idea.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Meadows, Donella H., Dennis L. Meadows, et al. <em>The Limits to
Growth</em>. New American Library, 1972.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Notorious simulation from the Club of Rome which
extrapolates economic and environmental trends of the whole Earth.
Widely lauded and critiqued in the 1970s.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Meadows, Donella H., Dennis L Meadows, and Jorgen Randers.
<em>Beyond the Limits: Confronting global collapses, envisioning a
sustainable future</em>. Chelsea Green Publishing, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Sequel to 1972's best-selling The Limits to
Growth.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Metropolis, N., and Gian-Carlo Rota, eds. <em>A New Era in
Computation</em>. The MIT Press, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A very fine collection of essays written for the
layperson which speak on the impact that parallel computing has had and
will have on computer science, culture, and our own thinking.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Meyer, Jean-Arcady, and Stewart Wilson, eds. <em>From Animals to
Animats</em>. The MIT Press, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The papers from a fruitful conference on the
simulation of adaptive behavior, which gathered ethologists studying
real animal behavior and roboticists trying to synthesize behavior in
artificial "animats."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Meyer, Thomas P., and Norman Packard. "Local Forecasting of High
Dimensional Chaotic Dynamics." Center for Complex Systems Research, The
Beckman Institute, University of Illinois, 1991, technical report
CCSR-91-1.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Theoretical underpinning for attempts to make
"local" predictions in complex systems.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Midgley, Mary. <em>Evolution as a Religion: Strange hopes and
stranger fears</em>. Muthuen &amp; Co, Ltd., 1985.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Midgley wrestles with the philosophical consequences
of "belief" in evolution, sometimes successfully and sometimes not. But
she provides much to think about.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Miller, James Grier. <em>Living Systems</em>. McGraw-Hill,
1978.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A massive (we're talking about 1100 pages of
minuscule type here) tome on the levels, sublevels and sub-sublevels of
living systems, including organizations and such. Think of this as a
printout of raw data on all living systems.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Minsky, Marvin. <em>The Society of Mind</em>. Simon &amp; Schuster,
1985.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">In 270 very readable one-page essays, Minsky
presents a society of ideas about the society of mind. It is true Zen.
Every page is a mob of astounding and mind-changing ideas. And at every
point in thinking about complex systems I would come back to Minsky.
This is the book that eventually led me to write this book.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Modis, Theodore. <em>Predictions</em>. Simon &amp; Schuster,
1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">In some ways a little cranky, but still useful
nonetheless as a summary of technological forecasting.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Mooney, Harold A. Convergent Evolution in Chile and California.
Dowden, Hutchinson &amp; Ross, 1977.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Marks the parallel biological forms in two
continents. Primarily ascribes this similarity to the orthodox
explanation of similar climate. Does not address the alternative theory
of internalist reasons for convergent evolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Morgan, C. Lloyd. <em>Emergent Evolution</em>. Henry Holt and
Company, 1923.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A very early and not very successful stab at trying
to articulate emergent control in evolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Moss, J. Eliot B. Nested Transactions: An Approach to Reliable
Distributed Computing. The MIT Press, 1985.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Practical use of hierarchy.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Motamedi, Beatrice. "Retailing Goes High-Tech." <em>San Francisco
Chronicle</em>, April 8, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Story on real-time trend-spotting, inventory
stocking and manufacturing in the top retailers using intensive
networked communications.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Needham, Joseph. Science and Civilisation in China. Cambridge at the
University Press, 1965.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The ancient Chinese invented remarkably
sophisticated mechanical devices, and this series of awesome books
tracks each invention in mind-boggling detail. It's like having a patent
registry for the Han people.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Negroponte, Nicholas P. "Products and Services for Computer
Networks." <em>Scientific American</em>, September 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">What we can expect from pervasive ultrahigh
bandwidth networks, by the director of the MIT Media Lab.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Nelson, Mark. "Bioregenerative Life Support for Space Habitation and
Extended Planetary Missions." <em>Space Biosphere Ventures</em>,
1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Gets into the early attempts at self-sustaining
space habitats.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Nelson, Mark, and Gerald Soffen, eds. Biological Life Support
Systems. Synergetic Press, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The proceedings of a 1989 workshop on closed
biological-based systems as human life support devices in space. Held at
the site of Biosphere II and cosponsored by NASA. Technical but
rich.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Nelson, Mark, and Tony L. Burgess, et al. "Using a closed ecological
system to study Earth's biosphere: Initial results from Biosphere 2."
<em>BioScience</em>, April 1993.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Description of the scientific experiment in
Biosphere 2 written by Bio2 staff after the first year. Has excellent
bibliography for this esoteric subject.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Nitecki, Matthew H., ed. Evolutionary Progress. University of
Chicago Press, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Biologists don't know how to handle the idea of
progress in evolution. Here leading evolutionists, philosophers, and
historians of biology grapple with the controversial idea in these
postmodern times, and come up ambivalent in the aggregate. A few of them
find the notion "noxious, culturally embedded, untestable,
nonoperational, intractable." Those who do acknowledge progress in
evolution are uncomfortable. This is a good, revealing collection of
papers.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>O'Neill, R. V. <em>A Hierarchical Concept of Ecosystems</em>.
Princeton University Press, 1986.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Treats the latest hot trend in ecology: a new
perspective which considers communities as hierarchical structures with
different dynamics for every level. Does a good job in setting out the
questions that need to be answered.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Obenhuber, D. C., and C. E. Folsome. "Carbon recycling in materially
closed ecological life support systems." <em>BioSystems</em>, 21;
1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Measurements of carbon pathways in closed
ecospheres.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Odum, Eugene P. <em>Ecology and Our Endangered Life-Support
Systems</em>. Sinauer Associates, 1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A quick introductory tour of the science of ecology
by the guy who brought energy accounting to the field.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Olson, R. L., M. W. Oleson, and T. J. Slavin. "CELSS for Advanced
Manned Mission." <em>HortScience</em>, 23(2); April 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A paper from a symposium on "Extraterrestrial Crop
Production." Good summary of NASA's closed system experiments.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Pagels, Heinz R. <em>The Dreams of Reason: The Computer and the Rise

</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A satisfyingly rich and perceptive scan on how the
complexity of the computer makes visible the complexity of the
world.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Parisi, Domenico, Stefano Nolfi, and Federico Cecconi. "Learning,
Behavior, and Evolution." In <em>Proceedings of the First European
Conference on Artificial Life</em>, The MIT Press, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Exploration of the role of learned behavior in
accelerating evolution based on neural networks.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
Pattee, Howard H. <em>Hierarchy Theory: The Challenge of Complex
Systems</em>. George Braziller, 1973.
<p>This is a book of all that was known about hierarchical systems 20
years ago, and it wasn't much. The authors ask some good questions which
still have not been answered. In short, we still don't know much how
hierarchies of control work.</p>
<p>Pauly, Philip J. <em>Controlling Life: Jacques Loeb &amp; the
Engineering Ideal in Biology</em>. University of California Press,
1987.<br />
</p>
<span class="small">A scholarly biography of the guy who did most to
make science think of biological organisms as mechanisms.</span>
<dl>
<dt>Pimm, Stuart L. "The complexity and stability of ecosystems."
<em>Nature</em>, 307; 26 January 1984.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Tries to answer the question of how complexity and
stability in ecosystems are related.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>---. <em>The Balance of Nature?</em> University of Chicago Press,
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Pimm treats food-webs as if they were cybernetic
circuits, and out of both simulated and real food-webs has derived some
of the freshest ecological news in a decade.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Pimm, Stuart L., John H. Lawton, and Joel E. Cohen. "Food web
patterns and their consequences." <em>Nature</em>, 350; 25 April
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An extremely informative review article on what is
known about ecological food webs from a systems point of view.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Pines, David, ed. <em>Emerging Syntheses in Science.</em>
Addison-Wesley, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An eclectic bunch of papers signaling the new
science of complexity. The best papers in this anthology, derived from
the founding workshop of the Santa Fe Institute, focus on the problems
of complexity itself.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Porter, Eliot, and James Gleick. <em>Nature's Chaos</em>. Viking,
1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The exquisite color landscape photography of Eliot
Porter is paired with the lyrical science prose of James Gleick. Both
celebrateÑin coffee table book formatÑthe ordered complexities and
complications of nature in its large and small details.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Poundstone, William. <em>Prisoner's Dilemma</em>. Doubleday,
1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Besides telling you more than you'll ever really
want to know about the Prisoner's Dilemma game, this book also ties the
game into the history of think tanks and the use of game theory in the
arms race and the role of John von Neumann in both game theory and the
cold war.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Powers, William T. <em>Living Control System</em>s. The Control
Systems Group, 1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A control engineer looks at the variety of control
circuits in biological systems.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Prusinkiewicz, Przemyslaw, and Aristid Lindenmayer. <em>The
Algorithmic Beauty of Plants</em>. Springer-Verlag, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Plants as numbers.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Pugh, Robert E. <em>Evaluation of Policy Simulation Models: A
Conceptual Approach and Case Study</em>. Information Resources Press,
1977.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Evaluates world economic models such as Limits to
Growth.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
Raup, David M. <em>Extinction: Bad Genes or Bad Luck?</em> W. W. Norton,
1991.
<p>The title is a very good question. This prominent paleontologist
thinks it's a combination of bad genes and bad luck, but that "most
species die out because they are unlucky." And thus he presents his
evidence.</p>
<p>Reid, Robert G. B. <em>Evolutionary Theory: The Unfinished
Synthesis</em>. Croom Helm, 1985.<br />
</p>
<span class="small">This is the most interesting book on evolutionary
theory I have come across. While other books can serve up more
exhaustive critiques of neo-Darwinism, none compare to this one in
presenting a post-Darwinian view. The author is not afraid to dip into
nonbiological studies to shape his notion of evolution; yet he primarily
dwells in biological fact. Most recommended.</span>
<dl>
<dt>Rheingold, Howard. <em>Tools for Thought</em>. Prentice Hall Books,
1985.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Subtitled: "The history and future of mind-expanding
technology," this is a really hip and very informative chronicle of how
computers became personal computers, of the visionary people behind that
transformation, and of its social meaning and cultural consequences. I
recommend it as the best history of computers to date.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Ricklefs, Robert E. <em>Ecology</em>. Chiron Press, 1979.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A textbook on ecology that is lucid, deep, and
gracefully written and full of the author's personal insight, setting it
apart from most rather antiseptic and formulaic ecology
textbooks.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Ridley, Mark. <em>The Problems of Evolution</em>. Oxford University
Press, 1985.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Here are the current bothersome problems in
neo-Darwinian theory from within the perspective of
neo-Darwinism.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Roberts, Peter C. <em>Modelling Large Systems.</em> Taylor &amp;
Francis, 1978.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Primarily on the difficulties of getting meaningful
results via miniaturizing a large system.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Robinson, Herbert W., and Douglas E. Knight. <em>Cybernetics,
Artificial Intelligence, and Ecology</em>. Spartan Books, 1972.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A few helpful ideas and a fair representation of
cybernetic thinking.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Root, A. I., ed. <em>The ABC and XYZ of Bee Culture</em>. A. I. Root
Company, 1962.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">For over a hundred years a perennial encyclopedia of
bee culture lore for first-time beekeepers. Remarkably timeless, last
updated in 1962.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Rosenfield, Israel. <em>The Invention of Memory</em>. Basic Books,
1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A survey view of the brain as having a nonlocalized
memory, and a long prologue to an exposition of Gerald Edelman's
controversial idea of "Neural Darwinism," or the natural selection of
thoughts in the brain.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Sagan, Dorion. <em>Biospheres: Metamorphosis of Planet Earth</em>.
McGraw-Hill, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Speculations on the science of biosphericsÑhuman
habitats as extensions of Gaia.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
Salthe, Stanley N. <em>Evolving Hierarchical Systems: Their Structure
and Representation</em>. Columbia University Press, 1985.
<p>Can't say I completely understand this book, but it is very
provocative in picturing evolution as working differentially at various
levels.</p>
<p>Saunders, Peter T. "The complexity of organisms." In <em>Evolutionary
Theory: Paths into the Future</em>, Pollard, J. W., ed. John Wiley and
Sons, 1984.<br />
</p>
<span class="small">Saunders sees complexity arising out of
self-organization rather than from natural selection.</span>
<dl>
<dt>Schement, Jorge Reina, and Leah A. Lievrouw. <em>Competing Visions,
Complex Realities: Social Aspects of the Information Society.</em> Ablex
Publishing, 1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Thoughts on communication networks as social
structure.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Schneider, Stephen, H. Penelope, and J. Boston, eds. <em>Scientists
on Gaia</em>. The MIT Press, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Some of the papers in this compendium are more
rigorous than others, but all strive to describe Gaia in scientific
rather than poetical terms. I found the papers which worried about the
definitions of Gaia to be the most productive.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Schrage, Michael. <em>Shared Minds: The New Technologies of
Collaboration</em>. Random House, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">In a network society the tools of collaboration
become essential and wealth- generating. Schrage reports on current
research into new network skills.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Schull, Jonathan. "Are species intelligent?" <em>Behavioral and
Brain Sciences</em>, 13; 1, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Since the analogy between learning and evolution is
at least as old as the idea of evolution itself, the author examines
species as thinking structures. His idea is critiqued by cognitive
scientists and evolutionists.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Schulmeyer, G. Gordon. <em>Zero Defect Software</em>. McGraw-Hill,
1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An introduction to the controversial zero defect
concept. I take this book as one method to construct reliable complex
systems.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Scientific American, eds. <em>Automatic Control</em>. Simon and
Schuster, 1955.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Primarily for historical interest, this anthology of
early Scientific American articles on cybernetic control talks about the
impact of automatic systems on society at a time (late '40s) when the
population of computers in the world was exactly one.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Simon, Herbert A. <em>The Sciences of the Artificial</em>. The MIT
Press, 1969.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">There's a lot of common sense about how to build
complex systems packed into this small book. It also offers rare insight
into the role and meanings of simulations.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>---. <em>Models of My Life</em>. Basic Books, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A dull autobiography about the extraordinary life of
the last renaissance man in the 20th century. In his spare time he
helped invent the field of artificial intelligence.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Slater, Philip. "Democracy is Inevitable." <em>Harvard Business
Review</em>, September/October 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Best argument I'm aware of for this provocative
thesis: "Democracy becomes a functional necessity whenever a social
system is competing for survival under conditions of chronic
change."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Smith, Reid G. <em>A Framework for Distributed Problem Solving</em>.
UMI Research Press, 1981.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">General computer science introduction to
constructing programs that work in a distributed environment.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Smith, John Maynard. <em>Did Darwin Get it Right? Essays on Games,
Sex and Evolution</em>. Chapman and Hall, 1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Deals with current controversies in evolutionary
biology in an even-handed and intelligent way.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Sober, Elliott. <em>The Nature of Selection: Evolutionary Theory in
Philosophical Focus</em>. The University of Chicago Press, 1984.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">This is an incredibly profound book. It is a
philosophical examination of evolutionary theory which begins with the
frequent criticism that neodarwinism is rooted in a contradiction, that
"survival of the fittest is a tautology." Sober illuminates this
causality puzzle and then goes on to reveal evolution as a system of
logic. His work should not be missed by anyone doing computational
evolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Sonea, Sonrin and Maurice Panisset. <em>A New Bacteriology</em>.
Jones and Bartlett, 1983.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The "new" here is a view that sees bacteria as not
primitive and not independent, but as a superorganism communicating
genetic changes worldwide and rapidly.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Spencer, Herbert. <em>The Factors of Organic Evolution</em>.
Williams and Nograte, 1887.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">At the time of Darwin, the philosopher Herbert
Spencer had an enormous impact in forming popular notions of the meaning
of evolution. As laid out in this book, evolution is progressive,
internally directed to improvement and perfection, among other
things.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Stanley, Steven. "An Explanation for Cope's Rule."
<em>Evolution</em>, 27; 1973.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">One of the rare accepted trends in biological
evolutionÑincreasing size in animalsÑgets debunked.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>---. <em>The New Evolutionary Timetable</em>. Basic Books,
1981.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Gingerly considers selection of units larger than
individuals and addresses long-term directions in macroevolution, but
does so without strong conclusions.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Steele, E. J. <em>Somatic Selection and Adaptive Evolution: On the
Inheritance of Acquired Characters</em>. University of Chicago Press,
1979.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The controversial experiments of immunologist Ted
Steele, who claims to demonstrate Lamarckian evolution in inbred strains
of mice, is presented in the experimenter's own words. Steele's work has
not been confirmed.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Stewart, Ian. <em>Does God Play Dice?</em> Basil Blackwell,
1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">For technical insight on chaos and dynamical
systems, a better book than Gleick's bestseller "Chaos." Stewart doesn't
have Gleick's narrative flair, but he does go deeper into the whys and
hows, with numerous graphs, illustrations, and a bit of math.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Stewart, Thomas A. "Brainpower." <em>Fortune</em>, June 3,
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Article about the role of knowledge in creating
wealth for companies. I picked up the term network economics
here.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Symonds, Neville. "A fitter theory of evolution?" <em>New
Scientist</em>, 21 September 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">In lay science terms addresses results suggesting
"Lamarckian" evolution in E. coli soups.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Tainter, Joseph A. <em>The Collapse of Complex Societies</em>.
Cambridge University Press, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">I disagree with the author's basic tenet that
declining returns on increasing complexity causes collapse of stable
civilizations, but his argument is worth reviewing.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Taylor, Gordon Rattray. <em>The Great Evolution Mystery</em>. Harper
&amp; Row, 1982.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Taylor treats evolution as an unsolved mystery and
trots out both conventional Darwinian explanations and conventional
doubts about those explanations. It is the most palatable and easy to
digest anti-Darwinian book, although a real skeptic of anti-Darwinism
will need to proceed further via its good bibliography for the
convincing details.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Thompson, D'Arcy. <em>On Growth and Form</em>. Cambridge University
Press, 1917.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A classic reminder of the ubiquitous influence of
form in life.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Thompson, John. <em>Interaction and Coevolution</em>. Wiley &amp;
Sons, 1982.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Solid compendium of the most current thinking,
evidence, and analysis in coevolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Thompson, Mark. "Lining the Wild Bee." In <em>Fire Over Water</em>,
Williams, Reese, ed. Tanam Press, 1986.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Story of the guy who put his head inside a wild bee
swarm, and who writes about the meaning of bees and hives.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Thomson, Keith Stewart. <em>Morphogenesis and Evolution</em>. Oxford
University Press, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A wonderfully refreshing and completely undogmatic
view of evolution by a renegade group (the "heretics") at Yale. Thomson
theorizes that internal constraints determine "themes" within evolution
and "clusters" of species. Highly recommended.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Thorpe, Col. Jack. "73 Easting Distributed Simulation Briefing."
Institute for Defense Analyses, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An executive summary of the Gulf War 73 Easting
Simulation pitched to win support for further military
simulations.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Tibbs, Hardin. "Industrial Ecology." Arthur D. Little, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">This white paper for an industrial consultant is an
early sketch of what a full-bore industrial ecology would look
like.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Todd, Stephen, and William Latham. <em>Evolutionary Art and
Computers.</em> Academic Press, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">In addition to gorgeous color plates of William
Latham's evolutionarily generated art forms, this book doubles as a
technical manual for the computer science and philosophy behind the
images.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Toffler, Alvin. <em>PowerShift</em>. Bantam Books, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Futurist Toffler speculates pretty convincingly on
expected trends in a networked economy and society.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Toffoli, Tommaso, and Norman Margolus. <em>Cellular Automata
Machines: A New Environment for Modeling</em>. The MIT Press,
1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Tiny universes created by simple rules as a means to
explore world-making. This is the most comprehensive text on the science
of cellular automata.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Travis, John. "Electronic Ecosystem." <em>Science News</em>, 140;
August 10, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Good introduction and background on Tom Ray's
artificial evolutionary Tierra system.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Vernadsky, Vladimir. <em>The Biosphere</em>. Synergetic Press,
1986.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">First published (and ignored) in 1926, this Russian
monograph has only recently garnered attention in the West. It is a
poetic-scientific foreshadowing of the Gaian notion-life and Earth as
one organism.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Vernon, Jack A. <em>Inside the Black Room</em>. Clarkson N. Potter,
Inc., 1963.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An early follow-up to Hebb's original experiments in
sensory deprivation at McGill University, Vernon did his at Princeton
University during the late '50s in a soundproof room in the basement of
the psychology building.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Vrba, Elisabeth S., and Niles Eldredge. "Individuals, hierarchies,
and process: towards a more complete evolutionary theory."
<em>Paleobiology</em>, 10; 2, 1984.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">There is a hunch that large-scale pattern in
evolution (macroevolution) derives from the hierarchical nature of
nature. This paper makes a preliminary case for the argument.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Waddington, C. H. <em>The Strategy of the Genes</em>. George Allen
&amp; Unwin Ltd, 1957.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The book that gave theoretical biology respect.
Waddington wrestles with the influence of the gene's agenda upon
evolution and tackles the Baldwin effect.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Waddington, C. H., ed. <em>Towards a Theoretical Biology</em>.
Aldine Publishing, 1968.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">For a field that lacks more than one example,
biology has always yearned for more theory. These proceedings stemmed
from a series of memorable symposia that Waddington hosted to launch a
more comprehensive systems-style look at biological organisms. The
"Waddington conferences" have taken on legendary status in the
post-Darwinian community.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Wald, Matthew L. "The House That Does Its Own Chores." <em>The New
York Times</em>, December 6, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Report on the opening of the first demonstration
"smart" house in Atlanta.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Waldrop, M. Mitchell. <em>Complexity: The Emerging Science at the
Edge of Order and Chaos</em>. Simon &amp; Schuster, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A popular account of the Santa Fe Institute's
approach to complex adaptive systems. Good stuff on economist Brian
Arthur and biologist Stuart Kauffman. Waldrop's book is better than
Roger Lewin's identically named Complexity, because he explains more and
attempts to synthesize the ideas.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Warrick, Patricia S. <em>The Cybernetic Imagination in Science
Fiction</em>. The MIT Press, 1980.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Science fiction has enlarged the thought space for
imagining cybernetic possibilities, which science proper can later
fill.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Weinberg, Gerald M. <em>An Introduction to General Systems
Thinking.</em> John Wiley &amp; Sons, 1975.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Helpful introduction course on "thinking
whole."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Weinberg, Gerald M., and Daniela Weinberg. <em>General Principles of
System Design</em>. Dorset House Publishing, 1979.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Perhaps the best book on modern cybernetics. Works
well in a classroom because it includes cybernetic exercises.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Weiner, Jonathan. <em>The Next One Hundred Years.</em> Bantam Books,
1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A journalistic survey of our Earth as a closed
system.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Weintraub, Pamela. "Natural Direction." <em>Omni</em>, October
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Readable and fairly reliable report on Hall and
Cairns's work on directed mutation in bacteria.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Weiser, Mark. "The Computer for the 21st Century." <em>Scientific
American</em>, September 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">It may be a while, but I believe that someday this
will be considered a seminal article staking out the role computers will
play in our everyday lives.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Wesson, Robert. <em>Beyond Natural Selection</em>. MIT Press,
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">At times, a mere tedious cataloging of evidences and
examples of nonadaptationist evolution. At rare moments, it gets to the
"so what" of it all. I owe the late author a couple of key ideas.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Westbroek, Peter. <em>Life as a Geological Force.</em> W. W. Norton,
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A geologist's personal recounting of evidence that
life shapes rocks.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Wheeler, William Morton. <em>Emergent Evolution: and the development
of societies</em>. W. W. Norton &amp; Company, 1928.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An early, slim volume - a paper really - on
holism.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Whyte, Lancelot Law. <em>Internal Factors in Evolution</em>. George
Braziller, 1965.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An informed and bold speculation on the internal
selection within the genome. Readable and thought provoking.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Wiener, Norbert. <em>Cybernetic, or control and communication in the
animal and the machine</em>. John Wiley, 1961.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The germ of all cybernetic texts. Except for the
preface, it is unexpectedly technical and mathematical. But worth
delving into.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Wilson, Edward O. <em>The Insect Societies.</em> Harvard University
Press, 1971.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An indispensable book of fascination, great insight,
and clear, lucid science. Required meditations for Net-mind.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Wright, Robert. <em>Three Scientists and Their Gods</em>. Times
Books, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Wonderfully crafted profiles of three world-class
thinkers on a quest for the unifying theory of information. Wright has
much to say about whole systems and complexity. Highly recommended. On
rereading this book after I finished mine, I realize that it is probably
closest to my own in spirit and range.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Yoshida, Atsuya, and Jun Kakuta. "People Who Live in an On-line
Virtual World." IEEE International Workshop on Robot and Human
Communication, technical report 92TH0469-7; 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A fairly intensive study of users of a virtual
networked world - Fujitsu's Habitat system in Japan - and how they used
it.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Zeltzer, David. "Autonomy, Interaction and Presence." <em>Presence:
Teleoperators and Virtual Environments,</em> 1; 1, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Locating autonomy and control as one axis of three
in a matrix of virtual reality. (The other two are degree of interaction
and presence.)</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Zorpette, Glenn. "Emulating the battlefield." <em>IEEE
Spectrum</em>, September 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">From the engineers' own mouths, a report on the new
and increasing role of simulations in warfare.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Zubek, John P., ed. <em>Sensory Deprivation: Fifteen years of
research</em>. Meridth Corporation, 1969.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A compendium of survey articles reviewing the
literature of sensory deprivation up to 1969, when this topic was
fashionable. The effects of SD are about as elusive as those of
hypnosis, and all the hopes for the field have evaporated as uneven data
piled up.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Zurek, Wojciech H., ed. <em>Complexity, Entropy and the Physics of
Information</em>. Addison-Wesley, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Some attempts to define complexity.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><em>Cybernetics</em>. Josiah Macy, Jr. Foundation, 1953.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Contains transcripts from one set of the Macy
meetings. Including great dialogues of befuddlement as Ashby introduces
his "homeostat machine."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><em>Self-Organizing Systems</em>. Pergamon Press, 1959.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The fascinating proceedings of a major conference
with an all-star line up of principal cybernetic pioneers. After each
paper is a revealing record of the panel discussions, where the true
learning happens. Why don't other books do this?</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><em>Transactions of the 9th Conference on Cybernetics</em>. Josiah
Macy, Jr. Foundation, 1952.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Remarkable discussions that have hardly aged on the
emergence of control in biological and</span>
</dd>
