<td><dl>
<dt>Ahmadjian, Vernon. <em>Symbiosis: An Introduction to Biological
Associations.</em> Hanover, 1986.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A comprehensive text on symbiosis which is clear and
crammed with insights.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Alberch, Pere. "Orderly Monsters: Evidence for internal constraint
in development and evolution." In <em>The construction of organisms:
Opportunity and constraint in the evolution of organic form.,</em>
Thomas, R. D. K. and W. E. Reif, eds.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">One of the most amazing papers I have ever read.
Explains why monstrosities in living creatures are so similar and
"orderly" given all possibilities.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Aldersy-Williams, Hugh. "A solid future for smart fluids." <em>New
Scientist,</em> 17 March 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Fluids and gel that change their state when
signaled. Engineers can use this response to make them "smart."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Allen, Thomas B. <em>War Games: The Secret World of the Creators,
Players, and Policy Makers Rehearsing World War III Today.</em>
McGraw-Hill, 1981.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Fascinating history and insider's view of the
large-scale simulations which the U.S. military agencies run to decipher
the life-and-death complexity of war.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Allen, T. F. H., and Thomas B. Starr. <em>Hierarchy: Perspectives
for Ecological Complexity.</em> University of Chicago Press, 1982.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Very ambitious book, but a bit soft in its arguments
and clarity. The main point: patterns in ecological systems can only be
perceived if viewed or measured at the appropriate scale.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Allen, John. <em>Biosphere 2: The Human Experience.</em> Penguin,
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Coffee table book on the making of Biosphere 2 by
its original visionary. Good history on how the idea arose and was tried
out. It covers the experiment until shortly before it "closed."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Allman, William F. <em>Apprentices of Wonder: Inside the Neural
Network Revolution.</em> Bantam Books, 1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Neural networks are the paramount example of
connectionism and bottom-up control. A light journalistic treatment of
the major players in the field; a good intro.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Amato, Ivan. "Capturing Chemical Evolution in a Jar."
<em>Science,</em> 255; 14 February 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Self-replicating RNA which can generate mutant
forms.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Amato, Ivan. "Animating the Material World." <em>Science,</em> 255;
17 January 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Brief report on various experiments to put smartness
into inanimate materials.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Anderson, Philip W., Kenneth J. Arrow, and David Pines. <em>The
Economy as an Evolving Complex System.</em> Addison-Wesley, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A landmark series of papers in the esoteric realm of
physics, math, computer science, and economics. Does a great job in
reinventing how we think of the economy. The central shift is away from
classical equilibrium. For lay readers the summary is in English and
newsworthy.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Aspray, William and Arthur Burks, eds. <em>Papers of John von
Neumann on Computers and Computer Theory.</em> MIT Press, 1967.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">If you are math-challenged (as I am), you need only
read the fine introduction and summary by Burks.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Axelrod, Robert. <em>The Evolution of Cooperation.</em> Basic Books,
1984.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Lucid account of how Prisoner's Dilemma and other
open-ended games can illuminate political and social thought.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Badler, Norman I., Brian A. Barsky, and David Zeltzer, eds.
<em>Making Them Move: Mechanics, Control, and Animation of Articulated
Figures.</em> Morgan Kaufmann Publishers, 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">To best exploit the technical details outlined in
this book, you'll need the accompanying video of experimental figures
trying to move. Some move quite well.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bajema, Carl Jay, ed. <em>Artificial Selection and the Development
of Evolutionary Theory.</em> Hutchinson Ross Publishing, 1982.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A banquet of benchmark papers on what artificial
selection (breeding) has to say about natural selection. What is most
noticeable is how paltry the feast is.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Basalla, George. <em>The Evolution of Technology.</em> Cambridge
University Press, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Makes the case (with fascinating examples) that all
innovation is incremental and not abrupt. Emphasizes the importance of
novelty in technological change.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bass, Thomas A. <em>The Eudaemonic Pie.</em> Houghton Mifflin,
1985.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The bizarre true story of how a California hippie
commune of physicists and computer nerds beat Las Vegas using chaos
theory. Addresses the problem of time-series predictions. An overlooked
great read.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. "Road to Ruin." <em>Discover,</em> May 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Story about Joel Cohen's expansion of the Braess
paradox--that adding more roads to a network may slow it down.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bateson, Gregory. <em>Steps to an Ecology of Mind.</em> Ballentine,
1972.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A great book about the parallels between evolution
and the mind. Of particular interest is the chapter on "The Role of
Somatic Change in Evolution."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. <em>Mind and Nature.</em> Dutton, 1979.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Bateson stresses and stretches the similarities
between mind and evolution in nature.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bateson, Mary Catherine. <em>Our Own Metaphor.</em> Smithsonian,
1972.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Mary Catherine Bateson's personal account of an
informal conference on evolution, progress, and learning in human
adaptation organized by her father, Gregory Bateson. The meeting was
held to deal with the role of conscious purpose in such complex systems.
Every conference should have such a document.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. With a Daughter's Eye. William Morrow, 1984.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A memoir of Margaret Mead and Gregory Bateson that
is more than a memoir. Written by daughter Mary Catherine, who is an
intellectual of equal caliber to her parents, this is a book of
cybernetic family stories.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bateson, Gregory and Mary Catherine Bateson. <em>Angels Fear: Toward
an Epistemology of the Sacred.</em> Macmillian, 1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Interwoven between the final writings of Gregory
Bateson--completed posthumously by his daughter Mary Catherine--are
dialogues between father and daughter that convey Gregory's deep ideas
of sacrament, communication, intelligence, and being.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Baudrillard, Jean. <em>Simulations.</em> Semiotext(e), Inc.,
1983.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Short, very French, very dense, very poetic, very
impenetrable, and somewhat useful in that he attempts to wring some
meaning out of simulations.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Beaudry, Amber A., and Gerald F. Joyce. "Directed Evolution of an
RNA Enzyme." <em>Science,</em> 257; 31 July 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Elegant experimental results of directed breeding of
RNA molecules.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bedau, Mark A. "Measurement of Evolutionary Activity, Teleology, and
Life." In <em>Artificial Life II,</em> Langton, Christopher G., ed.
Addison-Wesley, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A most intriguing attempt to quantify direction in
evolutionary activity.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. "Naturalism and Teleology." In <em>Naturalism: A Critical
Appraisal,</em> Wagner, Steven and Richard Warner, eds. University of
Notre Dame Press, 1993.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Can natural systems have purpose? Yes.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bedau, Mark A., Alan Bahm, and Martin Zwick. "The Evolution of
Diversity." 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Offers a metric for measuring diversity in an
evolutionary system.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bell, Gordon. "Ultracomputers: A Teraflop Before Its Time."
<em>Science,</em> 256; 3 April 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A bet whether parallel computers will beat serial
computers in the race for power.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bergson, Henri. <em>Creative Evolution.</em> Henry Holt, 1911.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A classic of philosophy about the idea that
evolution proceeds by some vital force.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Berry, F. Clifton. "Re-creating History: The Battle of 73 Easting."
<em>National Defense,</em> November 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Blow-by-blow account of the pivotal Gulf War battle
that has been recreated as a Pentagon simulation.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bertalanffy, Ludwig von. <em>General System Theory.</em> George
Braziller, 1968. For many years this was the cybernetic bible.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">It's still one of the few books on whole systems or
"systems in general." But it seems to me to be vague even in the places
I agree with. And Bertalanffy's signature idea--equifinality--I think is
wrong, or at least incomplete.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Biosphere 2 Scientific Advisory Committee. "Report to the Chairman,
Space Biosphere Ventures." <em>Space Biosphere Ventures,</em>
1992,<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Evaluates the validity and quality of Biosphere 2's
first nine months from a scientific viewpoint.<strong></strong></span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><strong></strong>Bolter, Jay David. <em>Writing Space: The Computer,
Hypertext, and the History of Writing.</em> Lawrence Erlbaum Associates,
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A marvelous, overlooked little treasure that
outlines the semiotic meaning of hypertext. The book is accompanied by
an expanded version in hypertext for the Macintosh. I consider it a
seminal work in "network culture."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bonner, John Tyler. <em>The Evolution of Complexity, by Means of
Natural Selection.</em> Princeton University Press, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A pretty good argument that evolution evolves toward
complexity.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Botkin, Daniel B. <em>Discordant Harmonies: A New Ecology for the
Twenty-First Century.</em> Oxford University Press, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Essays in natural history by an ecologist who has a
fresh view of nature as a disequilibrial system.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bourbon, W. Thomas, and Williams T. Powers. "Purposive Behavior: A
tutorial with data." Unpublished, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An intriguing claim that much behavior is not
"caused" but emanates from emergent internal purposes. Illustrated with
a simple experiment.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bowler, Peter J. <em>The Eclipse of Darwinism: Anti-Darwinian
Evolution Theories in the Decades around 1900.</em> The John Hopkins
University Press, 1983.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">This history serves as an excellent primer on
alternative scientific theories to strict neodarwinism.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. <em>The Invention of Progress.</em> Basil Blackwell,
1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A fascinating scholarly examination of how during
the Victorian era evolutionary theory initially created a notion of
progress, a legacy only now eroding.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Braitenberg, Valentino. <em>Vehicles: Experiments in Synthetic
Psychology.</em> The MIT Press, 1984.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Shows how very simple circuits can produce the
appearance of complicated behaviors and movement. The experiments were
eventually implemented in tiny model cars.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Brand, Stewart. <em>II Cybernetic Frontiers.</em> Random House,
1974.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A curious, small book that is pleasantly two-faced.
One-half is the first published report on computer hackers playing
computer games, and the other is Gregory Bateson talking about evolution
and cybernetics.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. <em>The Media Lab: Inventing the Future at MIT.</em> Viking,
1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Although about media future, there are enough gems
of insight about the future of interconnectivity to keep this rich book
ahead of the curve.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bratley, Paul, Bennet L. Fox, and Linus E. Schrage. <em>A Guide to
Simulation.</em> Springer-Verlag, 1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The best overview of the role and dynamics of
simulations in theory and practice.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Briggs, John. <em>Turbulent Mirror.</em> Harper &amp; Row,
1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Goes from the theory of chaos to the "science of
wholeness." Pretty good introduction to the strange behavior of complex
systems, with many wonderful pictures and diagrams. Emphasizes the
turbulent chaotic side, rather than the self-organizing side of
wholeness.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Brooks, Daniel, and R. E. O. Wiley. <em>Evolution as Entropy.</em>
The University of Chicago Press, 1986.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An important book although I have read only a little
of it. I wish I had a more technical and mathematical background to
plunge deeper into it and to appreciate its attempt to be a "unified
theory of biology."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Brooks, Rodney A. "Elephants Don't Play Chess." <em>Robotics and
Autonomous Systems,</em> 6; 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Instead elephants wander around doing things in the
real world. This paper summarizes Brooks's lab's attempts (about eight
robots so far) to make intelligence situated in the real physical
environment.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. "Intelligence without representation." <em>Artificial
Intelligence,</em> 47; 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Treats the evolutionary aspects of bottom-up control
in robots.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. "New Approaches to Robotics." <em>Science,</em> 253;
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Summary of Brooks's subsumption architecture for
robots.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Brooks, Rodney A., and Anita Flynn. "Fast, Cheap and Out of Control:
A Robot Invasion of the Solar System." <em>Journal of The British
Interplanetary Society,</em> 42; 1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">About "invading a planet with millions of tiny
robots." This is the source of my book title.<strong></strong></span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt><strong></strong>Brooks, Rodney A., Pattie Maes, Maja J. Mataric,
and Grinell More. "Lunar Base Construction Robots." <em>IROS,</em> IEEE
International Workshop on Intelligence Robots &amp; Systems, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Proposal for a swarm of minibulldozers, saturated
with "collective intelligence."</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Bruckman, Amy. "Identity Workshop: Emergent Social and Psychological
Phenomena in Text-Based Virtual Reality." Unpublished, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Excellent study of the new sociology of teenage
obsessives building and playing online MUDs.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Buss, Leo W. <em>The Evolution of Individuality.</em> Princeton
University Press, 1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Difficult book to grasp. The introductory and
summary chapters are clear and fascinating, and probably important in
understanding hierarchical evolution. Buss is onto something vital: that
the individual is not the only unit of selection in evolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Butler, Samuel. "Darwin Among the Machines." In <em>Canterbury
Settlement.</em> AMS Press, 1923.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An essay written in 1863, by the author of
"Erewhon," suggesting the biological nature of machines.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. <em>Evolution, Old and New.</em> AMS Press, 1968.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An early (1879), but still persuasive, philosophical
rant against Darwinism penned by an early supporter of Darwin who
renegaded into a fierce anti-Darwinian stance.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Cairns-Smith, A.G. <em>Seven Clues to the Origin of Life.</em>
Cambridge University Press, 1985.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The freshest book to date on the puzzle of the
origin of life. Written as a scientific detective story. Digests in lay
terms his more technical treatment in <em>Genetic Takeover.</em></span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Card, Orson Scott. <em>Ender's Game.</em> Tom Doherty Associates,
1985.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A science fiction novel about kids trained to fight
real wars while playing simulated war games.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Casdagli, Martin. "Nonlinear Forecasting, Chaos and Statistics." In
<em>Nonlinear Modeling and Forecasting,</em> Casdagli, M., and S.
Eubank, eds. Addison-Wesley, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Some heavy-duty algorithms for extracting order from
irregularity.</span>

possible AI, but one embedded in parallel and evolutionary processes.
His are some of the most intelligent remarks I've heard on
intelligence.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Hiltz, Starr Roxanne and Murray Turoff. <em>The Network Nation:
Human Communication via Computer</em> (Revised Edition). The MIT Press,
1993.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A visionary book when it was first published in
1978, it accurately forecasted many of the effects of intensely
connected computer communications and distributed groups. It still has
much to say about the coming network culture. A new section in the
revised edition addresses the authors' current thoughts on
superconnectivity.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Hinton, Geoffrey E., and Steven J. Nowlan. "How Learning Can Guide
Evolution." <em>Complex Systems,</em> 1; 1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">This very brief paper presents intriguing results of
a type of Lamarckian evolution running on computers and some provocative
speculations of other postdarwinian evolutions.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Ho, Mae-Wan, and Peter T. Saunders. <em>Beyond Neo-Darwinism.</em>
Academic Press, 1984.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Not too many non-Darwinian books are published
within science itself. This one comes from real biologists getting
results that are suggestive, or merely permit a hint, of non-Darwinian
evolution. This is good science at work.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Hofstadter, Douglas. <em>Gödel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden
Braid.</em> Basic Books, 1979.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Identical in all respects to the strangely loopy
Pulitzer Prize-winning volume, <em>Copper, Silver, Gold: an
Indestructible Metallic Alloy</em> by Egbert B. Gebstadter, now out of
print.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Holldobler, Bert, and Edward O. Wilson. <em>The Ants.</em> Harvard
University Press, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Deep, deep, rich, rich. All that is known about ants
to date (including some expanded and revised sections from Wilson's
earlier "Insect Societies"). A book to own and get lost in. Deserves the
Pulitzer Prize it won.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Huberman, B. A. <em>The Ecology of Computation.</em> Elsevier
Science Publishers, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A most interesting collection of pioneering papers
on using economic and ecological dynamics within computation to manage
complex computational tasks.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Johnson, Phillip E. <em>Darwin on Trial.</em> Regnery Gateway,
1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Johnson is a lawyer who treats Neo-Darwinism as a
defendant on trial, and subjects its evidence to the strict rules of
court. He concludes that it is an unproven hypothesis that does not at
this point seem to fit the evidence at hand. For the uninitiated
layperson, a good first read on anti-Darwinism, but it follows lawyerly
logic rather than science logic.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Kanerva, Pentti. <em>Sparse Distributed Memory.</em> MIT Press,
1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A dry, but daring monograph on a new architecture
for computer memory, one that relies on weak associative connections.
Wonderful forward by Douglas Hofstadter, who explains the novel design's
significance.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Kauffman, Stuart A. "Antichaos and Adaptation." <em>Scientific
American,</em> August 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A very accessible summation of Kauffman's important
major ideas, with nary an equation in it. Read this one first.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. "The Sciences of Complexity and `Origins of Order'." Santa
Fe Institute, 1991, technical report 91-04-021.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A personal and almost poetic short history of
Kauffman's own idea of self-<br />
organizing order.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>------. <em>The Origins of Order: Self Organization and Selection in
Evolution.</em> Oxford University Press, 1993.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A sprawling, deep, massive magnum opus of a book, as
dense as a dictionary. Kauffman tries to tell you everything he knows,
and he's bright, so hang in there. It's about the yin and yang of
natural selection and self-organization. A seminal work, not to be
missed.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Kauppi, Pekka E., Karl Mielikainen, and Kullervo Kuusela. "Biomass
and Carbon Budget of European Forests, 1971 to 1990." <em>Science,</em>
256; 3 April 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Shows a biomass increase in Gaia which may be due to
atmospheric carbon dioxide increase.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Kay, Alan C. "Computers, Networks and Education." <em>Scientific
American,</em> September 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An notable vision of how peer-to-peer networks might
change education.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Kleiner, Art. "The Programmable World." <em>Popular Science,</em>
May 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">About a chip that could be the basis for smart
houses and distributed cooperative computing in the fabricated
environment.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Kochen, Manfred. <em>The Small World.</em> Ablex Publishing
Corporation, 1989.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Small world as in "there must be only 200 people in
the whole world because I keep running into the same ones." If you go
deeper into this incredibly rich volume of studies on social networks,
you'll find it contains some of the coolest data for network culture
seen yet. Here are real numbers on how many friends-of-a-friend connect
us all.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Koestler, Arthur. <em>Janus: A Summing Up.</em> Random House,
1978.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">No critic of Darwin in modern times has been as
literate or influential as the brilliant Koestler. He spends the latter
third of this book summing up his objections to Darwinism, and offering
some suggestions for alternatives. His agile thinking on the subject
loosened up my mind.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
Korner, Christian, and John A. Arnone. "Responses to Elevated Carbon
Dioxide in Artificial Tropical Ecosystems." <em>Science,</em> 257; 18
September 1992.<br />
&#10;<dl>
<dt>Where the CO2 goes in closed greenhouses.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Koza, John. <em>Genetic Programming: On the
Programming of Computers by Means of Natural Selection.</em> The MIT
Press, 1992.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>More than anyone else, Koza has tried to evolve software in
systematic ways. This humongous tome is the record of his experimental
details and results.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Langreth, Robert. "Engineering Dogma Gives Way to
Chaos." <em>Science,</em> 252; 10 May 1991.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>How engineers can outsmart chaotic vibration and injury with
antichaos.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Langton, Christopher G., ed. <em>Artificial
Life.</em> Addison-Wesley, 1987.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>The mother of all artificial life studies. This is the proceedings
of the first a-life workshop. The breadth of the articles is
amazing.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Langton, Christopher, et al, eds. <em>Artificial
Life II.</em> Addison-Wesley, 1992.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
True news here. The most recent results of simulations of artificial
evolution and protolife in computers. Original, deeply significant, and
very accessible papers. Probably the most important book in this
bibliography.
<dl>
<dt>Lapo, Andrey. <em>Traces of Bygone Biospheres.</em> Synergetic
Press, 1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Very Russian reclassification of life types on Earth
by a sort of grand biomystic combining Chardin's "noosphere" with
Lovelock's "Gaia," and Vernadsky's geochemcial vitalism. Hard to read
but intriguing.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Laszlo, Ervin. <em>Evolution, the Grand Synthesis.</em> Shambhala,
1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">New-agey speculations of the role of evolutionary
change in the universe. I guess I found the freewheeling style and long
view refreshing although I can't say I learned anything in particular
from it.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Latil, Pierre de. <em>Thinking by Machine: A Study of
Cybernetics.</em> Houghton Mifflin, 1956.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A real find. This French author had the most
insightful and news-filled takes on feedback cybernetics I found
anywhere. All the more amazing for having been written in 1956. I owe
much to him.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Layzer, David. <em>Cosmogenesis: The Growth of Order in the
Universe.</em> Oxford University Press, 1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Seems a bit flaky to me, but he did have an unusual
idea or two that I couldn't dismiss. He came up with "reproductive
instability" as a driving force in evolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lenat, Douglas B. "The Heuristics of Nature: The Plausible Mutation
of DNA." Stanford Heuristic Programming Project, 1980, technical report
HPP-80-27.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">The most heretical, yet plausible, alternative
theory to Darwinian evolution I am aware of is compactly presented in
this technical report from the Stanford Computer Science
Department.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Leopold, Aldo. <em>Aldo Leopold's Wilderness: Selected early
writings by the author of A Sand County Almanac.</em> Stackpole Books,
1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Among many other things, this volume airs Leopold's
early thoughts about the role of fire in natural systems.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Levy, Steven. <em>Artificial Life.</em> Pantheon, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">An extremely enjoyable narrative of the making of
the artificial life movement and a memorable overview of its central
ideas and characters.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lewin, Roger. <em>Complexity: Life at the Edge of Chaos.</em>
Macmillian Publishing, 1992.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Annotated interviews with some of the central
characters currently involved in making complexity itself a science. Not
as deep or satisfying as Waldrop's book about the same subject, or
Levy's on artificial life; this one gives a quick but superficial
overview, and has a more biological, rather than mathematical, slant.
Best part is the treatment of the problem of direction or trends in
evolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lightman, Alan, and Owen Gingerich. "When Do Anomalies Begin?"
<em>Science,</em> 255; 7 February 1991.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Provocative thesis on the mechanism of progress
within science.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lima-de-Faria, A. <em>Evolution without Selection: Form and Function
by Autoevolution.</em> Elsevier, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">A difficult book. He seems to arrive at the same
place as Kauffman but by intuitive and poetic means, rather than
mathematics and science.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lipset, David. <em>Gregory Bateson: The Legacy of a Scientist.</em>
Prentice-Hall, 1980.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Bateson was interested in all things mysteriously
complex. This biography of him and his interests illuminates the range
of complexities that might be understood by looking at language,
learning, the unconscious, and evolution.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lloyd, Seth. "The Calculus of Intricacy." <em>The Sciences,</em>
October 1990.<strong></strong><br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small"><strong></strong>The best general introduction to
defining complexity I have seen, and gracefully written to boot.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lovece, Joseph A. "Commercial Applications of Unmanned Air
Vehicles." <em>Mobile Robots and Unmanned Vehicles,</em> 1, August-July
1990.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Comprehensive roundup of current work-in-progress in
commercial autonomous robots.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lovelock, James. <em>The Ages of Gaia: A Biography of Our Living
Earth.</em> W. W. Norton, 1988.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">Lovelock rounds out his Gaia hypothesis into a
theory here, and offers his best arguments and observations in support
of it. He also speaks of how Gaia might have evolved.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dt>Lovtrup, Soren. <em>Darwinism: The Refutation of a Myth.</em> Croom
Helm, 1987.<br />
</dt>
<dd>
<span class="small">This is detailed blow-by-blow history of the ideas
and personalities of anti-Darwinism. It's chock-full of delicious
excerpts and quotes from past critics up until the present. It goes deep
into the doubts of other experts about Darwinism.</span>
</dd>
</dl>
