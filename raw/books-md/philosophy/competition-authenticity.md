<div class="content">

**Nivi:**<span style=""> Let’s discuss your tweet:</span>
“<span style="">[Escape competition through
authenticity.](https://twitter.com/naval/status/975975798204112896?lang=en)</span>”<span style="">
It sounds like part of this is a search for who you are.</span>

**Naval:** <span style="">It’s both a search and a recognition.
Sometimes when we search our egos, we want to be something that we’re
not. Our friends and family are actually better at telling us who we
are. Looking back at what we’ve done is a better indicator of who we
are. </span>

<span style="">Peter Thiel talks a lot about how [competition is besides
the point](https://startupclass.samaltman.com/courses/lec05/). It’s
counterproductive. We’re highly memetic creatures. We copy everybody
around us. We copy our desires from them. </span>

<span id="more-28492592"></span>

<span style="">If everyone around me is a great artist, I want to be an
artist. If everyone around me is a great businessperson, I want to be a
businessperson. If everybody around me is a social activist, I want to
be a social activist. That’s where my self-esteem will come from.
</span>

<span style="">You have to be careful when you get caught up in status
games. You end up competing over things that aren’t worth competing
over. </span>

<span style="">Peter Thiel talks about how he was going to be a law
clerk because everybody at law school wanted to clerk for a Supreme
Court justice or some famous judge. He got rejected, and that’s what
made him go into business. It helped him break out of a lesser game and
into a greater game. </span>

<span style="">Sometimes you get trapped in the wrong game because
you’re competing. The best way to escape
competition</span>—<span style="">to get away from the specter of
competition, which is not just stressful and nerve-wracking but also
will drive you to the wrong answer</span>—<span style="">is to be
authentic to yourself.</span>

**No one can compete with you on being you**

<span style="">If you are building and marketing something that’s an
extension of who you are, no one can compete with you. Who’s going to
compete with Joe Rogan or Scott Adams? It’s impossible. Is somebody else
going write a better Dilbert? No. Is someone going to compete with Bill
Watterson and create a better [Calvin and
Hobbes](https://twitter.com/Calvinn_Hobbes?ref_src=twsrc%5Egoogle%7Ctwcamp%5Eserp%7Ctwgr%5Eauthor)?
No. </span>

<span style="">Artist are, by definition, authentic. Entrepreneurs are
authentic, too. Who’s going to be Elon Musk? Who’s going to be Jack
Dorsey? These people are authentic, and the businesses and products they
create are authentic to their desires and means. </span>

<span style="">If somebody else came along and started launching
rockets, I don’t think it would faze Elon one bit. He’s still going to
get to Mars. Because that’s his mission, insane as it seems. He’s going
to accomplish it.</span>

A<span style="">uthenticity naturally gets you away from competition.
Does it mean that you want to be authentic to the point where there’s no
product-market fit? It may turn out that you’re the best juggler on a
unicycle. But maybe there isn’t much of a market for that, even with
YouTube videos. So you have to adjust until you find product-market
fit.</span>

A<span style="">t least lean towards authenticity</span>,
<span style="">towards getting away from competition. Competition leads
to copy-catting and playing the completely wrong game. </span>

**In entrepreneurship, the masses are never right**

<span style="">In entrepreneurship, the masses are never right. If the
masses knew how to build great things and create great wealth, we’d all
be rich by now. </span>

W<span style="">hen you see a lot of competition, sometimes that
indicates the masses have already arrived. It’s already competed over
too much. Or it’s the wrong trend to begin with. </span>

<span style="">On the other hand, if the whole market is empty, that can
be a warning indicator</span>. It can indicate <span style="">you’ve
gone too authentic and should focus more on the product-market part of
founder-product-market fit. </span>

<span style="">There’s a balance you have to find. Generally, people
will make the mistake of paying too much attention to the competition.
The great founders tend to be authentic iconoclasts.</span>

**Combine your vocation and avocation**

**Nivi:** <span style="">Do you think one way of getting to authenticity
is by finding five or six various skills you already do and stacking
them on top of each other, maybe</span> not even in any purposeful
way<span style="">? If you are expressing who you are, you’re going to
be expressing all of these skills anyway. </span>

**Naval:** <span style="">If you are successful, in the long-term you’ll
find you’re almost doing all of your hobbies for a living, no matter
what they are. As Robert Frost said, </span>“[my goal in life is to
unite my avocation with my
vocation](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/754-my-goal-in-life-is-to-unite-my-avocation-with)<span style="">[.](https://www.goodreads.com/quotes/754-my-goal-in-life-is-to-unite-my-avocation-with)</span>”
That’s really where life is going to lead you anyway.

<span style="">You’re right about the skill stack. Everyone has multiple
skills. We aren’t one-dimensional creatures, even though that’s how we
portray ourselves in online profiles to get employed. You meet somebody
and they say, “I’m a banker.” Or, “I’m a bartender. Or “I’m a barber.”
</span>

**Specialize in being you**

<span style="">But people are multivariate. They have a lot of skills.
One banker might be good at finance. Another one might be good at sales.
A third one might be good at macroeconomic trends and have a feel for
markets. Another one might be really good at picking individual stocks.
Another might be good at maintaining relationships, rather than selling
new relationships. Everyone’s going to have various niches. And you’re
going to have multiple niches. It’s not going to be just one. </span>

A<span style="">s you go through your career, you’ll find you gravitate
towards the things you’re good at, which by definition are the things
you enjoy doing. Otherwise, you wouldn’t be good at them. You wouldn’t
have put in the time. </span>

<span style="">Other people will push you towards the things you’re good
at, too. Because your smart bosses, co-workers</span> <span style="">and
investors</span> <span style="">will realize you’re world-class in this
one thing. And you can recruit people to help you with other things.
</span>

<span style="">Ideally, you want to end up specializing in being you.
</span>
