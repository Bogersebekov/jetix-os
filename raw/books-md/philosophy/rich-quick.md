<div class="content">

**Nivi:** <span style="">We skipped one tweet because I wanted to cover
all of the tweets on the topic of the long-term. The tweet we skipped:
“[There are no get rich quick schemes. That’s just someone else getting
rich off
you.](https://twitter.com/naval/status/1002109022420451328?lang=en)”</span>

**Naval:** <span style="">This goes back to the world being an efficient
place. If there’s an easy way to get rich, it’s already been exploited.
There are a lot of people who will sell you ideas and schemes on how to
make money. But they’re always selling you some \$79.95 course or some
audiobook or seminar.</span>

<span style="">Which is fine. Everyone needs to eat. People need to make
a living. They might actually have really good tips. If they’re giving
you actionable, high-quality advice that acknowledges it’s a difficult
journey and will take a lot of time, then I think it’s realistic.</span>

<span id="more-28492691"></span>

<span style="">But if they’re selling you some get rich quick
scheme</span>—<span style="">whether it’s crypto or whether it’s an
online business or seminar</span>—<span style="">they’re just making
money off you. That’s *their* get rich quick scheme. It’s not
</span>necessarily <span style="">going to work for you. </span>

**We don’t have ads because it would ruin our credibility**

<span style="">One of the things about this whole tweetstorm and podcast
is that we don’t have ads. We don’t charge for anything. We don’t sell
anything. Not because I don’t want to make more
money</span>—<span style="">it’s always nice to make more money; we’re
doing work here</span>—<span style="">but because it would completely
destroy the credibility of the enterprise. If I say, “I know how to get
rich, and I’m going to sell that to you,” then it ruins it. </span>

<span style="">When I was young, one of my favorite books on the topic
was</span> “<span style="">[How To Get
Rich](https://www.amazon.com/How-Get-Rich-Greatest-Entrepreneurs-ebook/dp/B0017SUYY6),</span>”<span style="">
by Felix Dennis, the founder of Maxim Magazine. He had a lot of crazy
stuff in there. But he had some really good insights too. </span>

W<span style="">henever I read something by him or by GoDaddy founder
Bob Parsons or Andrew Carnegie</span>—<span style="">people who are
already very wealthy, and they clearly made their wealth in other
fields, not by selling the how-to-get-rich
line</span>—<span style="">they have a credibility. You just trust them.
</span>

They’re not trying to make money off of you. They’re obviously trying to
win some status and some ego—you always have to have a motivation for
doing something. But at least that’s a cleaner reason and why they’re
probably not lying. They’re probably not fooling you. They’re not
snowing you.

**Every founder has to lie to every employee**

<span style="">At some level every founder has to lie to every employee
of the company they have. They have to convince them, </span>
“<span style="">It’s better for you to work for me than to do what I did
and go work for yourself.</span>” <span style=""> </span>

<span style="">I’ve always had a hard time with that.</span>

The only honest way to do this, in my opinion, is to <span style="">tell
t</span>he <span style="">entrepreneurs I recruit: “You’re going to be
entrepreneurial in this company, and the day you’re ready to start your
own next thing, I’m going to support you. I’m never going to get in the
way of you starting a company. But this can be a good place for you to
learn how to build a good team and build a good culture; how to find
product-market fit; how to perfect your skills; and to meet some amazing
people while you figure out exactly what it is you’re going to do.
Because positioning, timing and deliberation are very important when
starting a company.”</span>

<span style="">What I’ve never been able to do is to look them in the
face and say, “You must be at your desk by 8 a.m.” Because I’m not going
to be at my desk by 8 a.m. I want my freedom. I’ve never been able to
say to them,</span> “<span style="">You’re great at being a director
today, and you’ll be a VP tomorrow,</span>”<span style=""> putting them
into that cold career path track. Because I don’t believe in it myself.
</span>

**Anyone giving advice on how to get rich should have made their money
elsewhere**

<span style="">If anyone is giving advice on how to get rich and they’re
also making money off of it, they should have made their money
elsewhere. You don’t want to learn how to be fit from a fat person. You
don’t want to learn how to be happy from a depressed person. So, you
don’t want to learn how to be rich from a poor person. But you also
don’t want to learn how to be rich from somebody who makes their money
by telling people how to be rich. It’s suspect. </span>

**Nivi:** <span style="">Any time you see somebody who’s gotten rich
following some guru’s advice on getting rich, remember that in any
random process, if you run it long enough and if enough people
participate in it, you will always get every single possible outcome
with probability one. </span>

**Naval:** <span style="">There’s a lot of random error in there. This
is why you have to absolutely and completely ignore business journalists
and economist academics when they talk about private companies. </span>

<span style="">I won’t name names, but when a famous economist rails on
Bitcoin, or when a business journalist attacks the latest company that’s
IPO’ing, it’s complete nonsense. Those people have never built anything.
They’re professional critics. They don’t know anything about making
money. All they know is how to criticize and get pageviews. And you’re
literally becoming dumber by reading them. You’re burning neurons.
</span>

<span style="">I’ll leave you with a quote from Nassim Taleb that I
liked. He said, “</span>[To become a philosopher king, start with being
a king, not being a
philosopher](https://twitter.com/nntaleb/status/1112076802300755971?lang=en)<span style="">[.](https://twitter.com/nntaleb/status/1112076802300755971?lang=en)</span>”

**Nivi:** <span style="">I’m glad you brought up Taleb, because I was
going to finish this by saying: remember the title of his first book,
</span> “<span style="">[Fooled By
Randomness](https://books.google.com/books/about/Fooled_by_Randomness.html?id=DCqFYOrGyegC).</span>”

**Naval:** <span style="">One of the reasons we’re a little vague in
this podcast is because we’re trying to lay down principles that are
timeless, as opposed to giving you the winning lottery ticket numbers
from yesterday.</span>
