<span class="s1">**Nivi:** </span><span class="s1">Before we go and talk
about accountability and leverage and judgment, you’ve got a few tweets
further down the line that I would put in the category of continuous
learning. </span>

<span class="s1">They’re essentially, “there is no skill called
business. Avoid business magazines and business class, study
microeconomics, game theory, psychology, persuasion, ethics, mathematics
and computers.”</span>

<span class="s1">There’s one other comment that you made in a Periscope
that was, “you should be able to pick up any book in the library and
read it.” And the last tweet in this category was, “reading is faster
than listening, doing is faster than watching.”</span>

<span class="s1">**Naval:** </span><span class="s1">Yeah, the most
important tweet on this, I don’t even have in here unfortunately, which
is, the foundation of learning is reading. I don’t know a smart person
who doesn’t read and read all the time.</span>

<span id="more-28492380"></span>

<span class="s1">And the problem is, what do I read? How do I read?
Because for most people it’s a struggle, it’s a chore. So, the most
important thing is just to learn how to educate yourself and the way to
educate yourself is to develop a love for reading. </span>

<span class="s1">So, the tweet that is left out, the one that I was
hinting at is, “read what you love until you love to read.” It’s that
simple. </span>

<span class="s1">Everybody I know who reads a lot loves to read, and
they love to read because they read books that they loved. It’s a little
bit of a catch-22, but you basically want to start off just reading
wherever you are and then keep building up from there until reading
becomes a habit. And then eventually, you will just get bored of the
simple stuff.</span>

<span class="s1">So you may start off reading fiction, then you might
graduate to science fiction, then you may graduate to non-fiction, then
you may graduate to science, or philosophy, or mathematics or whatever
it is, but take your natural path and just read the things that interest
you until you kind of understand them. And then you’ll naturally move to
the next thing and the next thing and the next thing. </span>

<span class="s1">**Read the original scientific books in a
field**</span>

<span class="s1">Now, there is an exception to this, which is where I
was hinting with what things you actually do want to learn, which is, at
some point there’s too much out there to read. Even reading is full of
junk.</span>

<span class="s1">There are actually things you can read, especially
early on, that will program your brain a certain way, and then later
things that you read, you will decide whether those things are true or
false based on the earlier things.</span>

<span class="s1">So, it is important that you read foundational things.
And foundational things, I would say, are the original books in a given
field that are very scientific in their nature. </span>

<span class="s1">For example, instead of reading a business book, pick
up Adam Smith’s The Wealth of Nations. Instead of reading a book on
biology or evolution that’s written today, I would pick up Darwin’s
Origin of the Species. Instead of reading a book on biotech right now
that may be very advanced, I would just pick up The Eighth Day of
Creation by Watson and Crick. Instead of reading advanced books on what
cosmology and what Neil Degrasse Tyson and Stephen Hawking have been
saying, you can pick up Richard Feynman’s Six Easy Pieces and start with
basic physics. </span>

<span class="s1">**Don’t fear any book**</span>

<span class="s1">If you understand the basics, especially in mathematics
and physics and sciences, then you will not be afraid of any book. All
of us have that memory of when we were sitting in class and we’re
learning mathematics, and it was all logical and all made sense until at
one point the class moved too fast and we fell behind.</span>

<span class="s1">Then after that we were left memorizing equations,
memorizing concepts without being able to derive them from first
principles. And at that moment, we’re lost, because unless you’re a
professional mathematician, you’re not going to remember those things.
All you’re going to remember are the techniques, the foundations.
</span>

<span class="s1">So, you have to make sure that you’re building on a
steel frame of understanding because you’re putting together a
foundation for skyscraper, and you’re not just memorizing things because
you’re just memorizing things you’re lost. So the foundations are ultra
important. </span>

<span class="s1">And the ultimate, the ultimate is when you walk into a
library and you look at it up and down and you don’t fear any book. You
know that you can take any book off the shelf, you can read it, you can
understand it, you can absorb what is true, you can reject what is
false, and you have a basis for even working that out that is logical
and scientific and not purely just based on opinions.</span>

<span class="s1">**The means of learning are abundant, the desire to
learn is scarce**</span>

<span class="s1">The beauty of the internet is the entire library of
Alexandria times 10 is at your fingertips at all times. It’s not the
means of education or the means of learning are scarce, the means of
learning are abundant. It’s the desire to learn that’s scarce. So, you
really have to cultivate the desire. </span>

<span class="s1">And it’s not even cultivating you’ve to not lose it.
Children have a natural curiosity. If you go to a young child who’s
first learning language, they’re pretty much always asking: What’s this?
What’s that? Why is this? Who’s that? They’re always asking questions.
</span>

<span class="s1">But one of the problems is that schools and our
educational system, and even our way of raising children replaces
curiosity with compliance. And once you replace the curiosity with the
compliance, you get an obedient factory worker, but you no longer get a
creative thinker. And you need creativity, you need the ability to feed
your own brain to learn whatever you want. </span>
