## D1 — The Universal Principles

The question: are there management principles that genuinely appear in *all ten* canons, or are the apparent universals just retrospective pattern-matching? The exercise below tests five candidate universals against each canon. Counter-evidence is admitted where it exists. The five that survive are named in **bold**; candidates that failed the test are noted explicitly.

### Candidate 1 — **Output over Activity (Effectiveness over Efficiency)**

**Core statement:** Managerial contribution is measured in the quality of what the organisation produces, never in the volume of what the manager personally does. Efficiency (doing things right) is subordinate to effectiveness (doing the right things). This is the single deepest convergence in the canon.

**Cross-canon expression:**

| Canon | Expression |
|---|---|
| A · Grove | *"A manager's output = The output of his organization + the output of the neighboring organizations under his influence."* ([*High Output Management*, p. 40](https://www.thrivestreetadvisors.com/leadership-library/high-output-management)) — individual work is irrelevant to managerial accounting. |
| B · 37signals | *"Keep your costs low, keep your company as small as you possibly can, and make great stuff."* — Jason Fried, [Founders podcast, Feb 2026](https://www.davidsenra.com/episode/jason-fried). Appetite-first scoping: scope serves outcome, never the reverse. |
| C · Netflix | *"Highly aligned and loosely coupled… teams the freedom to move quickly and operate independently, while ensuring responsibility for the outcome."* ([Netflix Culture Memo](https://jobs.netflix.com/culture)) — outcomes, not process compliance. |
| D · Stripe | *"We move with speed on what matters most… we aspire to become the world's fastest company."* Operating principle #3, [Stripe Culture](https://stripe.com/jobs/culture). Speed *on the things that matter*, not generalised busyness. |
| E · Drucker | *"The effective executive does not ask 'How do I perform?' He asks 'What needs to be done?'"* — *The Effective Executive*, Ch. 1, [Ole Miss PDF](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf). The foundational statement. |
| F · Watkins | The *break-even point* — the leader's contribution must exceed organisational investment within 90 days, or the transition has failed ([*First 90 Days*, p. 1](https://www.willpatrick.co.uk/notes/the-first-90-days-michael-watkins)). Output, not tenure, defines success. |
| G · Horowitz | *"Decision debt is the worst debt, by the way, because it paralyzes a company."* — [Sequoia Capital, Feb 2026](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/). Activity without decisions is organisational paralysis. |
| H · Laloux | Buurtzorg's hard constraint: ≥61% of time in *direct care* — the outcome — with everything else structured around that [Corporate Rebels, 2025](https://www.corporate-rebels.com/blog/10-questions-about-buurtzorgs-self-management-model-answered). |
| I · Solopreneur | Naval Ravikant: *"Code and media are permissionless leverage. They're the leverage behind the newly rich."* ([nav.al](https://nav.al/judgment)) — leverage-adjusted output, not hours worked, is the variable. |
| J · Holdco | Buffett, 2022 letter: *"Our satisfactory results have been the product of about a dozen truly good decisions — that would be about one every five years."* ([2022 Berkshire Letter, PDF](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2022ltr.pdf)) — decision quality over activity. |

**Why invariant:** Every mature management doctrine independently discovers that the supply of managerial effort is fungible while the supply of judgment is not. When organisations grow, activity scales linearly but decision quality does not — so compound returns go to whoever maximises judgment density. This is true whether the "organisation" is Intel's manufacturing floor (Grove), a solo creator's product line (Levels), or a $561B holdco (Buffett).

**AI-native adaptation:** The principle *intensifies*. When LLM agents execute at near-zero marginal cost, the binding constraint moves almost entirely from doing to *deciding what to do and evaluating what was done*. A 2025 practitioner essay on applying Grove in AI-augmented teams states it directly: *"In the age of AI, measure outcomes, not activity. Pair every goal with a counterbalance, keep your data current/valid/relevant, treat tools like production equipment."* ([LinkedIn, Brand, August 2025](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/rethinking-output-age-ai-andy-grove-my-compass-wolfgang-brand-ogg7f))

**Survives the test: Yes, unanimously.** This is the strongest universal in the canon.

### Candidate 2 — **Situational Calibration (Diagnose Before You Prescribe)**

**Core statement:** No management technique works unconditionally. Every canon prescribes a *diagnostic step* — assessing the task, the operator, the business situation, the phase — before choosing a style.

**Cross-canon expression:**

| Canon | Expression |
|---|---|
| A · Grove | *Task-relevant maturity* is task-specific, not person-general. Three postures: structured (low TRM), supportive (medium), objective-setting only (high). ([*High Output Management*, Ch. 12](https://getlighthouse.com/blog/management-concept/)) |
| B · 37signals | Shape Up's *appetite* diagnostic: what is this problem worth before we estimate? ([Ryan Singer, Lenny's Newsletter, Mar 2025](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/shape-up-ryan-singer)) |
| C · Netflix | *"Context not control"* — managers supply decision context to the person closest to the call ([Netflix Culture Memo](https://jobs.netflix.com/culture)). The diagnostic is: does this person have enough context to be the informed captain? |
| D · Stripe | *"Disagree and commit"* shorthand ([Stripe Culture](https://stripe.com/jobs/culture)) — but only after the situation has been argued. Writing-first memos exist to force situational diagnosis. |
| E · Drucker | *"Where do I belong? Given my strengths, my way of performing, and my values, how can I make the greatest contribution to what needs to be done?"* — *Managing Oneself*, [Ole Miss PDF](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf). |
| F · Watkins | The *STARS* diagnostic — Startup, Turnaround, Accelerated growth, Realignment, Sustaining success — is the explicit framework: *"Because of their differing imperatives, it is easy for heroes to stumble in realignment and sustaining-success situations."* ([*First 90 Days*, p. 82](https://www.willpatrick.co.uk/notes/the-first-90-days-michael-watkins)) |
| G · Horowitz | *Wartime vs. peacetime* CEO — *"everything you learn about decision-making and delegation and 'don't micromanage' — all these things are very peacetime oriented."* ([YouTube, 2014](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kWnKLUesjV4)) |
| H · Laloux | The *advice process* — decision authority belongs to the role-holder, but only *after* consulting those affected and those with expertise ([Laloux, Sounds True, 2024](https://enliveningedge.org/views/frederic-laloux-responds-bursting-bubble-teal-aint-real/)). |
| I · Solopreneur | Sivers's *"Hell Yeah or No"* filter ([*Anything You Want*, 2011](https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/16836190-anything-you-want)) — diagnose enthusiasm before committing. Graham's *Founder Mode* ([paulgraham.com, 2024](https://paulgraham.com/foundermode.html)) is explicitly a situational counter to the "manager mode" default. |
| J · Holdco | Leonard's tiered *hurdle rates* — 30% IRR for deals under $1M, 25% for $1M–$4M, 20% for larger, 15% for transformational ([In Practise](https://inpractise.com/articles/constellation-software-competition-hurdle-rates-and-deal-flow)). The diagnostic precedes the deal. |

**Why invariant:** Any prescription applied without diagnosis becomes cargo-cult. The canon repeatedly documents failure cases caused by applying correct-but-mistimed techniques (peacetime processes in wartime, Shape Up at solo scale, teal-install-by-consultancy). The diagnostic step is the universal meta-rule.

**AI-native adaptation:** TRM extends to LLM agents as *capability-situational*, not dispositional. Watkins's STARS extends to AI agent deployment: a new agent in a novel domain is a "startup" STARS and requires explicit objective-setting and close monitoring — which is the same low-TRM prescription Grove gave in 1983. The frameworks transfer cleanly; the substrate changed, the diagnostic logic did not.

**Survives the test: Yes, unanimously.**

### Candidate 3 — **Talent Density (Few Great, Not Many Average)**

**Core statement:** Organisational output is a function of talent density, not headcount. Adding average performers compounds negatively because they consume the attention of the great ones.

**Cross-canon expression:**

| Canon | Expression | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| A · Grove | Implicit — TRM and constructive confrontation work only if subordinates are capable of self-directed improvement. Not named as "talent density". | Present but implicit. |
| B · 37signals | *"We don't have any middle management… pulled back from [83 employees], that wasn't helpful."* — Fried, [Founders podcast, Feb 2026](https://www.davidsenra.com/episode/jason-fried). Density-by-refusal-to-scale. | Explicit. |
| C · Netflix | The originator: *"With the right density of talent, there is very little process needed."* — Hastings, [Startup Archive](https://www.startuparchive.org/p/netflix-founder-reed-hastings-on-the-importance-of-talent-density-and-using-a-keeper-test). The Keeper Test is density enforcement. | Explicit and central. |
| D · Stripe | *"Obsess over talent… relentlessly uphold our high talent bar."* Operating principle #5, [Stripe Culture](https://stripe.com/jobs/culture). | Explicit. |
| E · Drucker | *"Build on strengths; waste as little effort as possible on areas of low competence."* — *Managing Oneself*, [Ole Miss PDF](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf). Density is intra-person (strengths) more than inter-person. | Present in a different register. |
| F · Watkins | Implicit — the STARS framework assumes leaders who can apply it. *Not directly about talent density.* | Weak — **counter-evidence**. |
| G · Horowitz | *"What You Do Is Who You Are"* culture doctrine; hire for behaviours not credentials. Density-by-selection. Wartime hiring bar is explicitly higher. | Explicit. |
| H · Laloux | Self-management *requires* high-agency individuals. Buurtzorg's model collapses with average nurses ([Sounds True, 2024](https://enliveningedge.org/views/frederic-laloux-responds-bursting-bubble-teal-aint-real/)): *"you still need one or two leaders who really have that hope."* | Present as pre-condition, not as hiring doctrine. |
| I · Solopreneur | The canon is density-by-construction — one person. Not a management principle so much as an operating constraint. | Tautologically true. |
| J · Holdco | *"Operator quality is the binding constraint… the holdco cannot create operator quality; it can only find it."* Buffett: *"hire well, manage little"*; Wilkinson: *"finding great people to run these companies is one of the hardest aspects of this model."* [[Tiny Operating Manual](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/andrew-wilkinson-tiny-capital-operating-manual)] | Explicit and central. |

**Why invariant — with a caveat:** Seven canons endorse talent density explicitly (B, C, D, G, H pre-condition, J). Drucker (E) reframes it as intra-person strengths rather than inter-person selection. Watkins (F) does not address it directly. The solopreneur canon (I) is density-by-construction. The universal holds but is expressed differently depending on whether the canon is about *managing a team* (explicit density) or *managing yourself* (strengths concentration).

**AI-native adaptation:** Density extends to the agent substrate. A 2026 operator running 5 excellent specialist partners with Claude/Opus 4.5 and Cursor is denser — in output terms — than a 50-person traditional consultancy. [Welsh, Dec 2025](https://www.linkedin.com/in/justinwelsh/): *"AI can generate content. But it can't generate credibility."* Density shifts from headcount to judgment-per-dollar-of-compute.

**Survives the test: Yes, though expressed in two registers (team density / strengths concentration).**

### Candidate 4 — **Write It Down (Writing as Coordination Primitive)**

**Core statement:** In any organisation above minimal scale, writing is the primary coordination technology. Meetings and verbal communication are the exception, not the default. Writing forces thought, creates durable context, and scales without the founder.

**Cross-canon expression:**

| Canon | Expression | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| A · Grove | Meetings taxonomy and *one-on-one* doctrine; Grove wrote prolifically. But not explicitly "write-first". | Present, weak. |
| B · 37signals | *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work* codifies asynchronous-by-default. Basecamp is a writing tool. | Explicit. |
| C · Netflix | Culture memo itself is the artefact — 2024 revision done with *"over 1,500 employee comments"* [[about.netflix.com](https://about.netflix.com/news/sharing-our-latest-culture-memo)]. | Explicit via mechanism. |
| D · Stripe | *"We don't really have slide decks… From leadership on down, we default to writing"* — Dave Nunez, [Slab, 2020](https://slab.com/blog/stripe-writing-culture/). Collison's footnoted emails are the norm. | Explicit and central. |
| E · Drucker | Drucker's own practice; feedback analysis requires *writing down expectations*. Not explicitly about organisational writing culture. | Present. |
| F · Watkins | Learning plans, 30/60/90 plans, stakeholder maps — all written artefacts. The framework is implemented in writing. | Present. |
| G · Horowitz | *"a CEO's job is to design the company's communication architecture."* [[Under30CEO, March 2026](https://www.under30ceo.com/managing-people-like-freelancer/)] — architecture includes writing. | Present. |
| H · Laloux | Morning Star's *Colleague Letter of Understanding* (CLOU) is a peer-written contract replacing job descriptions [[Reinventing Organizations Wiki](https://reinventingorganizationswiki.com/en/cases/morning-star/)]. | Explicit and specific. |
| I · Solopreneur | Graham's *Writes and Write-Nots*: *"The result will be a world divided into writes and write-nots"* [[paulgraham.com, 2024](https://paulgraham.com/writes.html)]. Writing IS thinking. | Explicit. |
| J · Holdco | Berkshire's annual letter is the coordination artefact; Buffett's two-year letter to managers is the entire control mechanism [[2010 letter, PDF](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2010ltr.pdf)]. Leonard's President's Letters are similar. | Explicit. |

**Why invariant:** Written artefacts are the only medium that survives founder absence, scales across time zones, enforces thinking discipline, and creates institutional memory. Stripe (~8,000 people), Berkshire (60+ years), Buurtzorg (15,000 nurses) all rely on writing for coordination-at-distance. Verbal meetings don't compound; documents do.

**AI-native adaptation:** Writing *is* the interface between human and agent. An LLM agent requires a written brief, working notes, and a written review artefact to be useful. Organisations that already codified writing (Stripe, 37signals, Berkshire) will plug AI agents in directly. Organisations that run on verbal culture will rebuild as their first transformation step. This is the single principle most *strengthened* by AI-native operating conditions.

**Survives the test: Yes, unanimously.** Stronger in 2026 than in any prior decade.

### Candidate 5 — **Culture is Behaviour, Not Belief**

**Core statement:** Culture is the pattern of actions an organisation exhibits when no one is watching — not its stated values, mission, or aspirations. Values on walls are decorative; behaviours are operational.

**Cross-canon expression:**

| Canon | Expression | Verdict |
|---|---|---|
| A · Grove | Clean-desk culture, constructive confrontation as daily ritual, punctuality enforced by founder behaviour ([Sequoia podcast, Feb 2026](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/)). Implicit. | Strong. |
| B · 37signals | Calm-company norms enforced in product and schedule. The 2021 controversy is the canonical evidence: the founders' actions defined the culture, not the stated values [[Platformer](https://www.platformer.news/-what-really-happened-at-basecamp/)]. | Explicit through failure. |
| C · Netflix | *"Ensuring constructive feedback is part of our everyday work (like brushing your teeth)"* [[Netflix Culture Memo](https://jobs.netflix.com/culture)] — behavioural hygiene. | Explicit. |
| D · Stripe | Culture principles include specific *actions* (talk to users, debate energetically, abandon ideas when better ones emerge) — not just aspirations [[Stripe Culture](https://stripe.com/jobs/culture)]. | Explicit. |
| E · Drucker | *"The first secret of effectiveness is to understand the people you work with and depend on so that you can make use of their strengths, their ways of working, and their values."* [[*Managing Oneself*](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf)] — values *in practice*, not on paper. | Present. |
| F · Watkins | Early wins *must align with existing cultural norms* — "violating culture to win fast is a pyrrhic victory." [[Runn summary](https://www.runn.io/blog/the-first-90-days-summary)] Culture is the behavioural substrate within which transitions operate. | Present. |
| G · Horowitz | The canonical statement: *"Culture is not a set of beliefs, it's a set of actions… When people say they have 20 values, I ask how many of those behaviors they have. If it's none, your culture is hypocrisy."* [[Startup Archive, 2019](https://www.startuparchive.org/p/ben-horowitz-culture-is-not-a-set-of-beliefs-it-s-a-set-of-actions-0a65)] | **Central thesis.** |
| H · Laloux | *"Don't talk about self-management, don't talk about teal organization — say: we can't continue treating nurses the way we treat them."* [[Sounds True, 2024](https://enliveningedge.org/views/frederic-laloux-responds-bursting-bubble-teal-aint-real/)] Culture is what you *do*, not the label. | Explicit. |
| I · Solopreneur | Sivers's owner-exit test (*"if you left for a year, would the business do better?"*) is a behavioural culture test. Welsh: *"Real expertise… comes from solving real problems, making real mistakes."* [[LinkedIn, Dec 2025](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh)]. | Present. |
| J · Holdco | Munger: *"We operate Berkshire via a seamless web of deserved trust."* [[25iq](https://25iq.com/2015/09/26/a-dozen-things-ive-learned-from-charlie-munger-about-the-berkshire-system/)] — trust is behavioural, not contractual. | Explicit. |

**Why invariant:** Cultures that diverge from stated values always collapse toward behaviours. The 2021 Basecamp controversy is the cleanest natural experiment: the stated culture (calm, respectful, values-driven) and the actual culture (founder control, unwillingness to engage with employee concerns) diverged, and the organisation lost 30% of staff in days. The principle holds independently of canon.

**AI-native adaptation:** Behavioural culture encoded in *prompts, agent rubrics, incentive contracts, and milestone structures* is culture's new substrate in human-AI hybrid teams. An LLM agent has no shame, loyalty, or pride — culture with agents is fully contractual and evaluative. This is new territory. Horowitz at Columbia (September 2025) warned that cultures of "positive messaging" will fail to detect AI-era Strategic Inflection Points; cultures of honest information flow will survive them [[Columbia Business School / YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JFUSTctUxGM)].

**Survives the test: Yes, unanimously.**

### Candidates Rejected

Two candidates from the prompt's hypothesis did **not** survive the test:

- **"Ship, measure, learn" (Lean Startup style)** — present in B, D, I, J (via "hell yeah or no" filter, deal testing) but notably absent as doctrine from A (Grove), E (Drucker — who explicitly distrusts ship-first logic for knowledge work), F (Watkins — who prescribes *listening* first, not shipping), G (Horowitz — wartime ships, peacetime plans), H (Laloux — emergence, not iteration). The Lean Startup vocabulary is a subset, not a universal. Do not confuse with genuine universals.

- **"Weekly 1-on-1s"** — explicit in A (Grove) and G (Horowitz), implicit in C (Netflix candor) and D (Stripe), *absent* from E, F, H, I, J. A strong *practice* but not a *principle* — rejected as a universal and demoted to D5.

### Summary — The Five Surviving Universal Principles

1. **Output over Activity (Effectiveness over Efficiency)** — strongest, unanimously endorsed.
2. **Situational Calibration (Diagnose Before You Prescribe)** — unanimous, expressed through TRM / STARS / wartime-peacetime / hurdle-rates / advice-process.
3. **Talent Density (Few Great, Not Many Average)** — seven of ten explicit; Drucker reframes as intra-person; Watkins absent. Survives.
4. **Writing as Coordination Primitive** — universal, strongest in 2026.
5. **Culture is Behaviour, Not Belief** — unanimous, Horowitz's central thesis but independently present in every canon.

These five, taken together, are the operating kernel. Everything else in Part 2 (D2–D7) is downstream of them.

## D2 — What Each Canon Uniquely Contributes

Even among the universals, each canon offers one contribution that no other canon emphasises with equal weight. These are the *differentiae* — what you would miss if you dropped any single canon from the reading list.

### A · Grove — Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM) as a Dynamic Variable

**Unique contribution:** The recognition that management style should vary *by task* for the same person — not by person generally. Grove separates delegation into three postures keyed to the subordinate's specific competence on the specific task at hand. ([*High Output Management*, Ch. 12](https://getlighthouse.com/blog/management-concept/))

**Why only from Grove:** Intel's manufacturing context meant Grove watched the same engineer succeed at one process and fail at another. He resisted the temptation to classify the engineer as "mature" or "immature" and kept the analysis task-anchored. Most other canons generalise to person.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** Yes, directly. TRM for an LLM agent is *capability-situational by construction* — the same model handles code review brilliantly and stakeholder negotiation poorly. Grove's three-posture prescription maps to prompt engineering: structured low-TRM prompts for novel domains, objective-only high-TRM prompts for familiar ones.

---

### B · 37signals — Appetite, Not Estimates

**Unique contribution:** The inversion from *"how long will this take?"* to *"how much time is this worth?"* Time is the input; scope is the output. Projects that exceed appetite are cut, not extended. ([Ryan Singer, *Shape Up*](https://basecamp.com/shapeup); [Lenny's Newsletter, March 2025](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/shape-up-ryan-singer))

**Why only from 37signals:** Most doctrines inherit from project management traditions where estimates are the primary control variable. 37signals, as a bootstrapped cash-flow-positive shop from day one, could not afford overruns — the constraint forced the inversion.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** Yes, with an asterisk. DHH himself (April 2026) said Shape Up's two-month cycle *"needs rewriting because AI acceleration has made that timeline feel slow"* [[Pragmatic Engineer, Apr 2026](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/dhhs-new-way-of-writing-code)]. The appetite *principle* survives; the specific cycle length does not. For Jetix, expect two-week appetite cycles at solo scale, two-month at holding scale.

---

### C · Netflix — The Keeper Test

**Unique contribution:** A single operational question — *"if X wanted to leave, would I fight to keep them?"* — that replaces annual reviews, PIPs, and most of HR. Failures receive generous severance (4+ months) rather than improvement plans. ([Netflix Culture Memo](https://jobs.netflix.com/culture))

**Why only from Netflix:** The keeper test assumes a *liquid talent market* with credible replacements — a precondition only reliably met in elite tech hubs. Other canons could not generalise it; Netflix could only because the LA/SF talent market is unusually liquid.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** Partially. For Jetix's partner network, the keeper test applies *at partnership level* (should we renew/deepen this partnership?), not at termination. In a niche specialist network, replacement is months, not weeks. HBS's 2025 case study flags that keeper-test failure modes include *"employees paranoid about termination, existing in a state of fear"* [[HBS Working Knowledge](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/freedom-fear-and-feedback-should-other-companies-follow-netflixs-lead)]. Adapt, don't copy.

---

### D · Stripe — Writing as Craft (Footnoted Emails, RFCs, No Slide Decks)

**Unique contribution:** Writing is not just coordination (universal D1.4) — it is a *craft discipline* with explicit quality standards. Collison emails literally include footnotes. Pre-meeting memos are required. No slide decks. ([Slab, Dave Nunez interview](https://slab.com/blog/stripe-writing-culture/))

**Why only from Stripe:** The Collisons' intellectual formation (Patrick's *Stubborn Attachments* preface, Stripe Press as a publishing imprint) treats writing as scholarship. This is an unusual commitment in a payments infrastructure company, and it created a distinctive internal culture.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** Strongly. Stripe's March 2026 benchmark showing Claude Opus 4.5 at 92% on integration tasks ([stripe.com/blog](https://stripe.com/blog/can-ai-agents-build-real-stripe-integrations)) is possible *because* Stripe's documentation and API design were written craftfully in the first place. AI agents amplify documentation quality; bad documentation produces bad agent output. Writing-as-craft is the upstream investment.

---

### E · Drucker — Managing Oneself (Feedback Analysis)

**Unique contribution:** The self as the primary unit of management. Drucker's *Managing Oneself* (HBR, 1999) argues that knowledge workers must treat their own careers with enterprise-grade strategic rigour. The feedback analysis method: *"Whenever you make a key decision or take a key action, write down what you expect will happen. Nine or twelve months later, compare the actual results with your expectations."* ([Ole Miss PDF](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf))

**Why only from Drucker:** Every other canon starts from "you are managing other people." Drucker starts from "you are managing yourself, and only *then* can you manage others." In a solo-founder context, this is the inversion that actually matters.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** Most directly of any canon contribution. When your productive capacity scales through agents, the human differentiator becomes *what you are uniquely good at*. Drucker's strengths-based logic is the single most AI-native piece of classical management doctrine.

---

### F · Watkins — The First 90 Days + STARS Diagnostic

**Unique contribution:** A time-boxed, diagnostic-driven onboarding framework calibrated to the *type* of business situation the leader enters. *"The president of the United States gets 100 days to prove himself; you get 90."* ([*First 90 Days*, p. 1](https://www.willpatrick.co.uk/notes/the-first-90-days-michael-watkins))

**Why only from Watkins:** The canon otherwise treats onboarding as a generic period of "listening." Watkins' contribution is that the *right* listening depends on whether the business is in Startup, Turnaround, Accelerated-growth, Realignment, or Sustaining-success — and the prescriptions differ materially.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** Yes, with extension. Watkins himself (April 2025 Big Think) is actively engaging AI. The STARS model can be applied *recursively*: to each new operator partnership, to each new AI agent deployment, to the founder's own periodic role redefinitions. The 30/60/90 rhythm is a genuine universal and was the second-ranked rejected candidate for D1.

---

### G · Horowitz — Wartime vs. Peacetime CEO + "Don't Eat Shit Nibbling"

**Unique contribution:** The recognition that *management doctrine itself is context-dependent* — peacetime literature actively harms wartime survival. *"If you read the management literature, it's almost entirely written for peacetime CEOs."* ([YouTube, 2014](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kWnKLUesjV4)) Also unique: execution discipline for hard actions, most famously *"if you are going to eat shit, don't nibble"* — one decisive layoff event, never instalments.

**Why only from Horowitz:** Horowitz is the only major author writing from the standpoint of a CEO whose company was repeatedly close to death. His observations are survivorship-biased (valid critique from [PSmiths, May 2025](https://www.thepsmiths.com/p/review-the-hard-thing-about-hard)) but the wartime frame is genuinely distinctive.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** *Highly.* AI-induced Strategic Inflection Points (Grove's concept, extended by Boardy AI 2025) trigger wartime conditions more frequently — SIPs now arrive in weeks, not years. The peacetime/wartime distinction sharpens.

---

### H · Laloux — Evolutionary Purpose + Self-Management at Scale

**Unique contribution:** The proposition that organisations can operate at scale (Buurtzorg: 15,000 nurses) without managers, and that authority can be held by *roles negotiated peer-to-peer* rather than by people. Morning Star's CLOU (Colleague Letter of Understanding) is the canonical mechanism. ([Reinventing Organizations Wiki](https://reinventingorganizationswiki.com/en/cases/morning-star/))

**Why only from Laloux:** Every other canon treats hierarchy as given and works within it. Laloux treats hierarchy as a choice and documents what happens when the choice is made differently. Buurtzorg's 30% higher patient satisfaction and 36% reduction in care hours ([Andy Cleff, 2025](https://www.andycleff.com/2025/05/buurtzorg-evolution-self-organizing-healthcare/)) is the empirical case.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** Structurally. A matchmaker-partnership network is a *de facto* teal structure — peer agreements replace hierarchy, no FTE reporting lines, purpose-held by the founder. The Laloux framework gives Jetix the vocabulary for what it is already building. The critique (installation by consultants reverts when consultants leave) does not apply when the founder is the purpose-holder.

---

### I · Solopreneur Canon — Permissionless Leverage

**Unique contribution:** Naval Ravikant's taxonomy — labor, capital, media, code — ranked explicitly. *"Code and media are permissionless leverage. They're the leverage behind the newly rich."* ([nav.al](https://nav.al/judgment)) The implication: avoid labor and capital leverage as long as possible.

**Why only from this canon:** The classical canon (Grove, Drucker, Horowitz) assumes organisational scale. The company canon (37signals, Netflix, Stripe) assumes multi-person team. Only the solopreneur canon starts from "one person + tools" and works outward.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** AI agents are a fifth leverage type — permissionless, asynchronous, effectively free to reproduce. Welsh (March 2025) operationalises this: *"The future belongs to lean, profitable, one-person businesses."* [[LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh_the-future-belongs-to-lean-profitable-one-person-activity-7301811241793040385-3laJ)] Levels, Dinh, Vassallo are live proof. This canon is the most 2026-relevant.

---

### J · Holdco — Permanent Capital + Centralised Capital Allocation / Decentralised Operations

**Unique contribution:** The structural inversion that has produced Berkshire's 60-year compounding record: *"extreme decentralization of operations combined with extreme centralization of capital allocation"* (Munger, [25iq](https://25iq.com/2015/09/26/a-dozen-things-ive-learned-from-charlie-munger-about-the-berkshire-system/)). The never-sell doctrine is the mechanism that makes it work.

**Why only from this canon:** Every other canon focuses on how to run the business. The holdco canon is the only one that focuses on *how to own many businesses* without running any of them.

**Translates to AI-native 2026:** With modification. The principles (decentralisation, permanent capital, operator quality, cashflow discipline, hurdle rates, trust, compounding time) survive unchanged. The *scale* threshold drops: an AI-augmented solo operator at $100K–$200K net profit is now viable as a holdco unit where Tiny's floor was $500K. Agent 4's dossier: *"An AI-native holdco can potentially compound from operators at the $100K–$200K net profit level — lower absolute size, potentially similar relative returns."*

## D3 — The Jetix Compound Stack (Solo / Partnership / Holding)

The synthesis question: across three phases, which principles dominate, which practices to adopt, which to reject?

### Phase 1 — Solo (€50K/quarter, founder + AI agents, no partners)

**Dominating principles:**
1. **Managing Oneself** (E, Drucker) — strengths-based work allocation; feedback analysis monthly
2. **Permissionless Leverage** (I, Ravikant) — code + media + agents before labor or capital
3. **Output over Activity** (A, Grove; E, Drucker) — weekly outcome review, not activity tracking
4. **Writing as Coordination** (D, Stripe; I, Graham) — *write to think*; document methodology while you build it

**Practices to adopt (with canon citation):**

1. **Daily writing habit** [A, D, I] — 45 minutes each morning, public-facing essays + internal methodology. *"The result will be a world divided into writes and write-nots."* — Graham, [paulgraham.com, 2024](https://paulgraham.com/writes.html)
2. **Feedback analysis** [E] — keep a decisions journal; review every 90 days. Drucker: *"Compare actual results with expectations."*
3. **Hell Yeah or No filter** [I] — refuse low-enthusiasm engagements. Sivers's owner-exit test as north star.
4. **Appetite-first scoping** [B] — two-week appetite cycles replacing estimates. One shaped pitch at a time.
5. **Compound methodology notebook** [D, I] — every engagement writes back into a living methodology document. This is the "Compounding" in Compounding Engineering.
6. **Task-Relevant Maturity for agents** [A] — start every new agent task with structured low-TRM prompts; graduate to objective-only as capability is demonstrated.
7. **One weekly outcome review** [A, E] — not activity, not hours — what did the business actually produce?

**Practices to deliberately reject:**

1. **Blitzscaling** [G critique of Hoffman; I] — the Horowitz–Hoffman dispute is real; at solo scale, efficiency beats speed. *Hoffman's framework is wrong for this phase.*
2. **Premature hiring** [B, I] — the 37signals/solopreneur consensus: no FTEs before Phase 2, and only if compound leverage is demonstrated.
3. **Holacracy-style formal governance** [H critique via Zappos] — peer governance with *one person* is ritualistic nonsense.
4. **OKR cargo cult** [A critique] — Grove's OKRs serve cascading organisations. A solo operator's "objectives" are strategic questions, not quarterly metrics.

---

### Phase 2 — Partnership Network (€200K–1M/year, 5–10 specialists)

**Dominating principles:**
1. **Talent Density** (C, Netflix; J, Buffett) — *"finding great people to run these companies is one of the hardest aspects"* (Wilkinson, [Tiny Operating Manual](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/andrew-wilkinson-tiny-capital-operating-manual))
2. **Context not Control** (C, Netflix) — each specialist is an "informed captain"
3. **Advice Process** (H, Laloux) — decision belongs to the role-holder, but only after consultation
4. **Culture as Behaviour** (G, Horowitz) — the partnership norms are whatever you actually do, not what the MOU says
5. **Writing as Coordination** (D, B, H) — CLOU-style written partnership agreements, appetite-based engagement memos

**Practices to adopt:**

1. **CLOU (Colleague Letter of Understanding) for every partner** [H] — written, bilateral, renewable. Morning Star's mechanism, applied to specialist operators.
2. **Monthly partner 1:1s with written agenda** [A, G] — Grove's one-on-ones: the partner's meeting, not yours. Agenda set by partner. Information flows up.
3. **Appetite-first engagement shaping** [B] — six-week cycles for significant client engagements; partners pitch, founder shapes.
4. **Annual partner-level Keeper Test (deepening, not termination)** [C] — "Should we renew/deepen this partnership?" as an annual review artefact, not a quarterly termination trigger.
5. **Two-year letter to partners** [J, Buffett model] — published once per year covering strategy, capital allocation, and what the founder will and won't do. Buffett's canonical control mechanism, scaled down.
6. **Farming for dissent** [C] — explicit partner-network feedback rhythms; treat "we disagree" as a renewable resource, not a problem.
7. **STARS diagnosis per engagement** [F] — each new engagement (or new client) gets a Startup/Turnaround/Accelerated-growth/Realignment/Sustaining-success tag, and the prescriptions differ.

**Practices to reject:**

1. **Traditional employment-law HR structure** [J critique of corporate governance] — the whole point of the partnership model is to avoid the institutional imperative.
2. **Synergy mandates between partners** [J, Wilkinson] — *"every inter-company transaction is paid at full market rate."* Forced synergies destroy partner autonomy.
3. **Netflix-style Keeper Test as termination trigger** [C critique] — specialist replacement time (months, not weeks) makes termination-via-keeper-test destructive.
4. **Teal-as-branding** [H] — Laloux explicitly warned: *"don't try to engage your organization saying 'we'll become a Teal organization.'"*
5. **Blitzscaling of the partner count** [G critique of Hoffman] — density first, scale later.

---

### Phase 3 — Holding (€1M+/year, 10–50 AI-augmented operators)

**Dominating principles:**
1. **Decentralised operations + centralised capital allocation** (J, Buffett/Munger) — the structural invariant
2. **Permanent capital / never-sell** (J, Leonard) — as a deal-sourcing moat, not sentiment
3. **Hurdle rate discipline** (J, Leonard) — every new operator/engagement must clear a pre-committed threshold
4. **Culture as Behaviour** (G, Horowitz) — encoded in contracts and visible founder conduct
5. **Writing as Coordination** (D, J) — annual letter, hurdle-rate memos, operator quarterly reports

**Practices to adopt:**

1. **Annual founder letter** [J, Buffett/Leonard] — published, written, substantive. The single most leveraged governance artefact.
2. **Per-operator two-year letter** [J, Buffett 2010] — *"hire well, manage little."* The entire control mechanism is: report bad news early, guard reputation, confer on large capex, submit successor recommendations.
3. **Tiered hurdle rates** [J, Leonard] — 30% IRR floor for small deals / partnerships, stepping down with size; *non-negotiable when the hurdle is missed.*
4. **Programmatic holdco coordination** [D4 extension] — dashboards, LLM-assisted reporting, automated capital-routing — used to *reduce* coordination overhead, not add bureaucracy.
5. **Operator selection discipline** [J, Buffett/Wilkinson] — operator quality is the binding constraint; founder time spent on selection compounds far more than founder time spent on operations.
6. **Continuing feedback analysis at holdco level** [E] — written expectations vs. actuals for every capital allocation decision.
7. **Cashflow discipline** [J] — distributable free cash flow as the single north star metric per operator; not EBITDA, not ARR, not growth rate.

**Practices to reject:**

1. **Public-company transition** [J, Tiny critique] — Wilkinson himself now acknowledges the reporting burden and governance costs contradict the original doctrine. Stay private unless capital need is unavoidable.
2. **Central bureaucracy** [J, Constellation; J, Berkshire 25-employee HQ] — every added holdco layer creates new incentive misalignments.
3. **Forced synergies** [J, Wilkinson] — maintained throughout Phase 3.
4. **Blitzscaling doctrine for operator count** [G vs. Hoffman] — density compounds; scale without density doesn't.
5. **"Wartime for everyone" as default** [G] — Horowitz's distinction is context-dependent. Peacetime default, wartime when SIPs demand it.

## D4 — Management Philosophy × Compound Engineering

Compound Engineering (CE) — the Jetix discipline of encoding methodology into code so that each engagement compounds institutional knowledge — reshapes several classical assumptions. The questions below are the ones that matter.

### Q1 — If code is the methodology, what becomes of delegation, review, and control?

**Delegation changes character.** Classical delegation moves *tasks* from manager to subordinate. In CE, delegation moves *tasks* from human to agent, but the *methodology itself* — the prompt structure, the evaluation rubric, the compounding review loop — is authored by the human. The human delegates execution and retains authorship of the delegation *mechanism*. Stripe's writing culture is the closest classical analogue: Collison's footnoted emails set norms that scale through the organisation without Collison being present. ([Slab, Nunez interview](https://slab.com/blog/stripe-writing-culture/))

**Review becomes compounding, not gatekeeping.** Traditional review catches errors; CE review *encodes* corrections into the methodology so the same error cannot occur again. This is Drucker's feedback analysis automated: *"Compare actual results with expectations"* — but the delta is written back into the system, not just into the reviewer's head. Buurtzorg's model is the nearest pre-AI cousin: each team's operational learning feeds back into the shared methodology without a central bureaucracy ([Andy Cleff, 2025](https://www.andycleff.com/2025/05/buurtzorg-evolution-self-organizing-healthcare/)).

**Control becomes architectural.** Horowitz: *"a CEO's job is to design the company's communication architecture"* [[Under30CEO, March 2026](https://www.under30ceo.com/managing-people-like-freelancer/)]. In CE, the architecture *is* the methodology. Control is exercised by shaping the architecture, not by supervising tasks inside it.

### Q2 — If agents and humans both do work, what does "management" mean?

Management becomes three distinct functions:
- **Direction setting** — deciding what should be done (Drucker's "What needs to be done?")
- **Context curation** — supplying humans and agents with the information, constraints, and quality standards to execute (Netflix's "context not control")
- **Outcome evaluation** — judging whether produced work meets the standard (Grove's output equation, extended)

The classical fourth function — *task supervision* — becomes near-zero marginal cost for routine work (agents self-execute, humans handle exceptions) but *more* expensive for novel work (judgment cannot be automated). The manager's time reallocates *away* from supervision and *toward* the other three. This is the core structural shift.

### Q3 — Does Grove's Task-Relevant Maturity apply to LLM agents?

**Yes, with modifications.** TRM for an agent is capability-situational, not dispositional. The agent does not *grow* — it has fixed capabilities per-task-class at any given moment. Grove's three postures map cleanly:
- Low TRM → structured prompts, explicit task decomposition, close monitoring of outputs
- Medium TRM → objective + constraints + a few worked examples
- High TRM → objective + success criteria only

Practitioner literature is still thin. The [Software Philosopher, December 2025](https://softwarephilosopher.com/2025/12/26/high-output-management-my-reading-notes/) is one of the first reading-notes essays to explicitly apply TRM to AI-augmented engineering teams. No peer-reviewed paper yet exists as of Q1 2026. This is an area where Jetix's methodology can publish novel contributions.

### Q4 — Does Netflix's Keeper Test have an analog for agent-specialists? (The $100 rule in CE as a parallel?)

**A partial parallel.** The Keeper Test asks: *"if X wanted to leave, would I fight to keep them?"* For agents, the parallel is: *"if this agent task-class suddenly stopped working, would I build scaffolding around it or drop it?"*

The CE $100 rule (*if the agent output requires more than $100 of human correction time, codify it or replace it*) is a sharper version of the same question. It is more Lean-than-Netflix in flavour — Taichi Ohno's *jidoka* principle applied to AI output — and does not quite match Netflix's elite-talent-market assumption.

For human specialist partners, the partnership-level Keeper Test (D3 Phase 2) is the correct adaptation: annual renewal, not termination.

### Q5 — Is management becoming prompt engineering + context curation?

**Partially, but calling it that understates it.** Welsh's 2025 formulation is the clearest: *"AI can generate content. But it can't generate credibility. Real expertise takes years to earn."* [[LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh)] Prompt engineering and context curation are the *visible* tasks, but the *judgment* that makes them valuable — knowing what to ask for, recognising when the output is wrong, maintaining client trust across outputs — is the irreducible core.

Horowitz at Columbia (September 2025): *"how the next wave of innovation may reshape every industry"* depends on *cultural* adaptability, not technical skill [[Columbia Business School / YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JFUSTctUxGM)]. Management as culture-plus-judgment is the more honest frame. Prompt engineering is a sub-skill, not the new discipline.

### Q6 — Informed Captain (Netflix) vs. Model-as-the-Product (Boris Cherny) — tension or compatible?

**Compatible, with care.** The Netflix "informed captain" gives the person closest to a decision the authority to make it. "Model-as-the-product" (Cherny / Anthropic framing 2025) says the AI model itself is the value, not the application layer.

In CE the informed captain *is the human operator* (founder or partner) — they own the decision. The *model* is the agent that executes within the decision frame. These layers don't conflict as long as authority remains with the human and the model is treated as a capability, not a decider.

The tension arises if the model is given agency without supervision. Watkins (April 2025 Big Think): the three limits of current AI are *hallucination, programmed personality, and optimistic bias*. A model-as-product philosophy that ignores these limits will produce informed-captain failures at scale.

### Summary — How CE Reshapes Classical Management

| Classical assumption | CE-native shift |
|---|---|
| Delegation = moving tasks to people | Delegation = moving tasks to agents; authoring the methodology remains human |
| Review = error-catching | Review = compounding the methodology with every correction |
| Control = supervision | Control = architecture of prompts, rubrics, contracts |
| Management = four functions (plan/organize/direct/supervise) | Management = three functions (direction / context / evaluation) |
| Supervision scales with headcount | Supervision scales with *novelty* of work; routine is near-zero-cost |
| Culture = stories and rituals | Culture = contracts + incentive design + visible founder conduct |
| Coordination = meetings + writing | Coordination = primarily writing + programmatic dashboards |

None of the five universal principles (D1) are overturned by CE. Every one of them is *intensified*.

## D5 — Top 10 Universal Practices for an AI-Native Solo-to-Holding Founder

Ranked by load-bearing importance, citations across canons, and AI-native translatability.

### 1. Write Everything Down (Canon: D · Stripe; B · 37signals; J · Buffett; I · Graham)

**Practice:** Default to writing for any non-trivial coordination. Pre-meeting memos, engagement specs, methodology notes, annual letters. No slide decks for internal work. Founder writes for 45–60 minutes daily.

**Why universal:** D1 Universal #4. Appears in every canon. Graham: *"writes and write-nots"*; Stripe: footnoted CEO emails; Berkshire: annual letters; 37signals: async-by-default; Buurtzorg: CLOUs.

**AI-native translation:** Writing is the *interface* to LLM agents. Documentation quality upper-bounds agent output quality. The upstream investment that amplifies everything downstream.

**Cost:** High (daily habit; mental energy). **Adoption evidence:** Stripe, Berkshire, 37signals, Amazon, Basecamp, Buurtzorg, Permanent Equity.

### 2. Diagnose Before You Prescribe (Canon: F · Watkins; A · Grove; G · Horowitz)

**Practice:** Every new engagement, operator partnership, AI deployment, or strategic decision begins with explicit situational diagnosis — STARS for business situation, TRM for capability, wartime/peacetime for existential stakes, hurdle rate for capital commitment.

**Why universal:** D1 Universal #2. Every canon has its own diagnostic step; they compose well.

**AI-native translation:** Apply STARS to each new agent deployment (startup-STARS = structured prompts, close monitoring; sustaining-success-STARS = objective-only).

**Cost:** Low (30 minutes of explicit thinking per decision). **Adoption evidence:** IMD programmes, Genesis Advisers practice, Berkshire deal review, Constellation hurdle-rate discipline.

### 3. Weekly Outcome Review, Not Activity Review (Canon: A · Grove; E · Drucker; J · Buffett)

**Practice:** Every Friday, 30–45 minutes. Not "what did I do this week?" but "what did the business produce this week?" — measured in compound artefacts (new methodology, client deliverables, infrastructure, reputation assets).

**Why universal:** D1 Universal #1. Grove's output equation, Drucker's effectiveness, Buffett's operating earnings.

**AI-native translation:** Output-not-activity is *stronger* with agents because activity scales near-infinitely. Outcome discipline is the binding constraint.

**Cost:** Low. **Adoption evidence:** Every serious operator.

### 4. Appetite-First Scoping (Canon: B · 37signals / Shape Up)

**Practice:** For every major project: first set the appetite ("this is worth X weeks"), then shape the problem to fit. Scope is the output, not the input. For solo operators, two-week appetites. For partner engagements, four- to six-week appetites. For holdco-level capital decisions, use Leonard's hurdle rates as the appetite equivalent.

**Why universal:** Unique to 37signals among the named canons but cross-validated by Leonard's hurdle-rate discipline as the capital-allocation equivalent.

**AI-native translation:** DHH himself says the two-month cycle needs shortening for AI-era work [[Pragmatic Engineer, Apr 2026](https://newsletter.pragmaticengineer.com/p/dhhs-new-way-of-writing-code)]. Shorter cycles (one to two weeks at solo scale), same principle.

**Cost:** Medium (discipline to cut scope at deadline). **Adoption evidence:** 37signals, Shape Up community, many indie operators (Levels, Dinh by behaviour).

### 5. Talent / Partner Density Over Headcount (Canon: C · Netflix; J · Buffett/Wilkinson; G · Horowitz; D · Stripe)

**Practice:** Refuse to add operators / partners unless they are top-decile in the specific capability required. Use annual partner-level Keeper Test (deepen, not terminate). At holdco scale, apply Wilkinson's minimum-profitability floor (solo-AI-native equivalent: €100–200K net profit) as hurdle for adding an operator unit.

**Why universal:** D1 Universal #3. Seven of ten canons endorse explicitly.

**AI-native translation:** Density extends to the agent substrate — small human team × high-quality agent tooling > large human team × commodity tooling. Welsh's $10M-at-91%-margin solo business ([LinkedIn, March 2025](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh)) is the 2026 proof-of-concept.

**Cost:** High (psychological — saying no to willing partners / employees). **Adoption evidence:** Netflix, Berkshire, Tiny, Constellation, most high-margin indie operators.

### 6. Monthly 1-on-1s With the Partner / Operator, Agenda Owned by Them (Canon: A · Grove; G · Horowitz)

**Practice:** Replace weekly 1-on-1s (Grove's original prescription) with *monthly* for a partnership network. Partner sets the agenda. Founder prepares by reading recent partner outputs. 60–90 minutes, not 30.

**Why universal:** Grove's information-flow doctrine; Horowitz's culture-via-behaviour doctrine. Relaxed cadence (monthly vs weekly) for a partnership network vs a direct-report team.

**AI-native translation:** Agenda setting and preparation can be AI-assisted (summaries of recent partner outputs, anomaly detection). The meeting itself is still human.

**Cost:** Medium (founder calendar). **Adoption evidence:** Grove at Intel, Horowitz at a16z portfolio, most well-run partnership firms.

### 7. Annual Written Letter (Canon: J · Buffett, Leonard; D · Stripe; C · Netflix culture memo)

**Practice:** Once per year, publish a substantive written letter. Covers: the year's decisions and outcomes, next year's strategic posture, operator updates, capital allocation rationale. Buffett's annual letter is the template; Leonard's president's letters are the technical-depth version; Netflix's culture memo (1,500 employee comments in the 2024 revision) is the participatory version.

**Why universal:** D1 Universal #4 extended. The single most leveraged governance artefact at holdco scale.

**AI-native translation:** Strengthened — LLM agents can index, search, and surface patterns across historical letters, making the compound value of the letter archive grow over time.

**Cost:** High (2–4 weeks of founder time per year). **Adoption evidence:** Berkshire, Constellation, Netflix, Permanent Equity (Beshore publishes regularly).

### 8. Feedback Analysis / Decisions Journal (Canon: E · Drucker)

**Practice:** Every significant decision: write down what you expect to happen. 90 days later, compare actuals. Maintain for life, not just for the current venture. Drucker: *"the only reliable method to discover strengths."*

**Why universal:** Drucker's unique contribution, but operationally the single most actionable management-of-oneself practice. Also present implicitly in Kokonas's decision-making-vs-outcomes framing ([Tim Ferriss, 2020](https://tim.blog/2020/05/17/nick-kokonas-2-transcript/)).

**AI-native translation:** Agents can assist by surfacing pattern summaries at review time. The writing must remain human.

**Cost:** Low (10 minutes per decision). **Adoption evidence:** Drucker, Bezos's one-way-vs-two-way door framework, many operators who don't name the practice.

### 9. Hurdle-Rate Discipline for Any Capital or Time Commitment (Canon: J · Leonard; B · 37signals appetite extension)

**Practice:** Before every engagement / operator / partnership / capital allocation, set an explicit minimum return threshold. Walk away when the threshold isn't met. Leonard: *"The investment didn't quite meet our hurdle rate… the big one got away. The difference between investing and not, was tiny."* ([Acquirers Multiple](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2024/01/mark-leonard-raise-the-bar-how-hurdle-rates-drive-profitable-investments/))

**Why universal:** Cross-canon — Leonard's financial version, 37signals's appetite version, Sivers's "Hell Yeah or No" as the enthusiasm version.

**AI-native translation:** Hurdle rates become easier to maintain when low-conviction engagements can be refused without opportunity cost (agents handle routine work).

**Cost:** High psychologically (saying no to revenue in the moment). **Adoption evidence:** Constellation Software (primary), most disciplined capital allocators.

### 10. Shutdown Ritual / Explicit Stop-Working (Canon: B · 37signals)

**Practice:** Daily end-of-day ritual. Close all tabs. Write three sentences on what shipped today, what is blocked, what is next. Close laptop. DHH's world.hey.com posts repeatedly emphasise that calm is an *output* of discipline, not an input.

**Why universal:** Strongest from 37signals but implicit in Drucker's effectiveness doctrine (time scarcity), Horowitz's decision-debt warning, and most burnout literature.

**AI-native translation:** Critical. Agents can work 24/7; humans cannot. The shutdown ritual is the psychological boundary that makes AI augmentation sustainable rather than colonising. Cal Newport's Deep Work doctrine is adjacent.

**Cost:** Low (15 minutes). **Adoption evidence:** 37signals (company-wide), many knowledge workers anecdotally; low formal adoption in startup culture.

## D6 — Top 10 Management Anti-Patterns in AI-Native Context

### 1. Blitzscaling for Agents (Horowitz critique of Hoffman, extended)

**Pattern:** Treating agent velocity as business velocity. "We shipped 10× more PRs" is not "we created 10× more value." The Hoffman Blitzscaling doctrine (*"prioritizing speed over efficiency in an environment of uncertainty"* [[YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XiANesZ7YOA)]) was calibrated for network-effect businesses in VC context. Applied to AI-agent output volume, it produces noise-scaling without value-scaling.

**Failure signal:** Growing output with flat or declining client outcomes.

### 2. Assuming Agents Need HR (Category Error)

**Pattern:** Applying employee-management frameworks (performance reviews, 1-on-1s, PIPs, feedback culture) to LLM agents. Agents have no shame, loyalty, or continuity; applying HR rituals to them is a category error that wastes founder attention.

**Correct posture:** Agents need prompt architecture, evaluation rubrics, and version control. Specialist *human* partners still need the human management toolkit.

### 3. Over-Ceremony for Solo + Agents Team

**Pattern:** Running daily standups, weekly retros, monthly OKR reviews, quarterly planning — when the team is one human and three agents. The ceremonies originated for teams where information flow was the constraint. For a solo operator the constraint is judgment, not information flow.

**Failure signal:** Calendar full of ceremonies, no time for actual work.

### 4. Mistaking AI Velocity for Business Velocity

**Pattern:** "We are building 3× faster now" with no corresponding increase in clients, revenue, or compound reputation. AI speeds up *execution*; it does not speed up *discovery* of what is worth executing. Compound Engineering's methodology compounding is the correct lens: what compounds in the business (methodology, reputation, client relationships) vs. what doesn't (shipped-but-unused artefacts).

### 5. OKR Cargo Cult (Grove / Doerr mis-application)

**Pattern:** Running OKRs at solo or 3-person scale. Grove's MBO/OKR framework was designed for hierarchical organisations where cascading goals solve coordination at 1000+ headcount. At solo or partnership scale, the overhead exceeds the benefit. Do not conflate OKRs (Grove/Doerr-specific) with generic goal-setting — the former is a specific technology for a specific scale.

**Correct posture:** At solo scale, quarterly strategic questions (not quantified OKRs). At partnership scale, per-engagement appetite and hurdle rates. At holdco scale, the annual letter does the work OKRs do elsewhere.

### 6. Engineering Excellence as Cover for No Product

**Pattern:** Endless codebase refactoring, tooling upgrades, methodology meta-work — without client outcomes. The 37signals 2021 controversy is a social version of this pattern. Compound Engineering's danger mode is becoming an engineering cult that produces methodology for methodology's sake.

**Failure signal:** More time spent on CE tooling than on CE client outputs. Rebalance.

### 7. "I'm a CEO Now" Solo-Founder Delusion (Horowitz warned)

**Pattern:** Adopting CEO-grade process (board meetings, CFO hiring, org charts, VPs of Everything) when the business is one person. Horowitz (Sequoia, February 2026): *"If you are all of the roles and you're leading yourself, then that's more of like a bipolar nature. President is more of an appropriate title than CEO."* [[Sequoia](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/)]

**Correct posture:** Founder / Operator until the network is large enough to warrant governance. Even at 10 partners, stay lean.

### 8. Culture as Perks Instead of Culture as Actions

**Pattern:** Substituting benefits (unlimited vacation, learning stipends, retreats) for behavioural culture. Horowitz's canonical statement: *"When people say they have 20 values, I ask how many of those behaviors they have. If it's none, your culture is hypocrisy."* The 2021 Basecamp controversy is the public case study [[Platformer](https://www.platformer.news/-what-really-happened-at-basecamp/)]. Netflix's 2025 update adds explicit guard rails precisely because they know the mechanism is fragile [[IMDb coverage, October 2025](https://www.imdb.com/news/ni64661925/)].

### 9. Remote-Only Dogma vs. In-Person Dogma

**Pattern:** Treating remote or in-person as a principle rather than a tactic. The 37signals *Remote* (2013) position hardened post-pandemic into a dogma, then began to soften in 2024. Neither extreme is correct; the diagnostic question is "what does this specific work require?" — which is D1 Universal #2 again. For a Berlin solo founder with European partners, hybrid is almost certainly right.

### 10. Ignoring Compliance / Regulatory Layer Until Forced

**Pattern:** Treating GDPR, EU AI Act, data-residency, and professional-liability as post-hoc concerns. In German / EU market the compliance layer is *structural*, not decorative. Jetix's partner-network architecture must incorporate compliance from first engagement. The holdco doctrine (J) is largely silent on this because US operators (Berkshire, Constellation, Tiny) faced different constraints; EU operators must factor it in early.

## D7 — Honest Assessment

### 1. Which canon is most load-bearing for a 2026 AI-native solo-to-holding founder?

**Answer: J (Holdco Doctrine) for the structural design, E (Drucker / *Managing Oneself*) for the personal-operating layer.** These are the two most under-read canons among AI-era founders and the two most directly applicable to the Jetix trajectory.

The holdco canon gives the *structural answer* to "what are you building?" — decentralised operations + centralised capital allocation, permanent capital, operator quality as binding constraint. Every other canon is downstream: A/G tell you how to run a team if you chose to, B/C/D tell you how specific successful companies operate, F tells you how to onboard into a role, H tells you what the extreme-decentralisation end-state looks like, I tells you how to run as one person.

The Drucker canon gives the *personal answer* to "what are you optimising?" — *"What does the situation require? Given my strengths, my way of performing, and my values, how can I make the greatest contribution?"* [[*Managing Oneself*](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf)] For a solo founder whose productive capacity scales through agents, knowing precisely what you are uniquely good at (and what to offload) is the irreducible strategic question.

**(a) Task-Relevant Maturity for AI agents.** No peer-reviewed framework exists. The [Software Philosopher essay (December 2025)](https://softwarephilosopher.com/2025/12/26/high-output-management-my-reading-notes/) and Boardy AI (April 2025) essays are the closest. The field is open.

## Section A — Andy Grove / *High Output Management* (1983/1995) & *Only the Paranoid Survive* (1996)

**Executive summary**

Andy Grove, former Chairman and CEO of Intel, produced in *High Output Management* (1983, republished 1995 with a new foreword by Grove himself) the closest thing Silicon Valley has to a management engineering manual. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Output_Management) Grove's central theorem — that a manager's output equals the output of their organisation plus the output of neighbouring organisations under their influence — inverts the intuition that managers should optimise their own activity. Instead, they should hunt for leverage: activities whose ripple effect on others is disproportionately large. In *Only the Paranoid Survive* (1996), Grove extended this into strategy, introducing the concept of the *Strategic Inflection Point* — a 10× force that permanently alters competitive dynamics — and arguing that only organisations that detect SIPs early and act with paranoid vigilance survive them. Ben Horowitz, who has described Grove as "the greatest teacher and the greatest CEO ever," made *High Output Management* mandatory reading for all Andreessen Horowitz portfolio managers and wrote the foreword for the 1995 paperback reissue, cementing the book's cult status across multiple generations of founders. [Bloomberg/YouTube, 2016](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SqdcyBGaPlQ) For a Berlin-based solo founder managing a network of specialist operators, Grove's frameworks — particularly managerial leverage, task-relevant maturity, and OKRs — transfer more directly than most management doctrines because they were written for *influence without direct authority*, a condition that describes every solo holdco operator.

**Top 5 primary sources**

1. [PRIMARY] Grove, Andrew S. *High Output Management*. Random House, 1983; Vintage Books paperback with author foreword, 1995. The primary source for all doctrine below. Key chapters: Ch. 3 (Managerial Leverage), Ch. 4 (Meetings), Ch. 6 (MBO/OKRs), Ch. 12 (Task-Relevant Maturity). No canonical public URL; widely quoted via secondary summaries at [Thrive Street Advisors](https://www.thrivestreetadvisors.com/leadership-library/high-output-management) (with verbatim page citations).

2. [PRIMARY] Grove, Andrew S. *Only the Paranoid Survive: How to Exploit the Crisis Points that Challenge Every Company*. Currency/Doubleday, 1996. Core for Strategic Inflection Point doctrine. 3. [PRIMARY] Ben Horowitz, "Ben Horowitz on the Lessons He Learned from Intel's Andy Grove," Bloomberg Television interview, March 22, 2016. [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SqdcyBGaPlQ). Horowitz relays primary Grove anecdotes (desk-cleanliness culture, toilet-paper confrontation) as applied management doctrine.

**Core doctrine — 5–10 principles**

1. **The manager's output equation** [PRIMARY]
   > "A manager's output = The output of his organization + The output of the neighboring organizations under his influence." (*High Output Management*, p. 40, per [Thrive Street Advisors](https://www.thrivestreetadvisors.com/leadership-library/high-output-management))
   
   The implication: individual contributor work is irrelevant to managerial accounting. Output is measured through the team and its downstream influence.

2. **Managerial leverage as the unit of analysis** [PRIMARY]
   > "A manager's output is thus the sum of the result of individual activities having varying degrees of leverage. Clearly the key to high output means being sensitive to the leverage of what you do during the day." (*High Output Management*, p. 54, per [Thrive Street Advisors](https://www.thrivestreetadvisors.com/leadership-library/high-output-management))
   
   High-leverage activities: (a) affect many people; (b) change one person's behaviour for a long time based on a brief interaction; (c) provide a critical piece of information to a large group. Negative leverage (meddling, waffling, unprepared meetings) destroys output symmetrically.

3. **Task-relevant maturity (TRM) governs delegation style** [PRIMARY]
   > "How often you monitor should not be based on what you believe your subordinate can do in general, but on his experience with a specific task and his prior performance with it — his task relevant maturity... as the subordinate's work improves over time, you should respond with a corresponding reduction in the intensity of the monitoring." (*High Output Management*, Ch. 12, quoted at [Lighthouse Blog](https://getlighthouse.com/blog/management-concept/))
   
   TRM is *task-specific*, not person-general. Three management postures: (a) Low TRM → structured, task-oriented, tell *what, when, how*; (b) Medium TRM → individual-oriented, two-way communication, support; (c) High TRM → establish objectives and monitor only, minimal involvement. The structure "moves from being externally imposed to internally given." [Software Philosopher notes, 2025](https://softwarephilosopher.com/2025/12/26/high-output-management-my-reading-notes/)

4. **OKRs: the simplest effective planning tool** [PRIMARY]
   > "The idea behind MBO is extremely simple: If you don't know where you're going, you will not get there… Where do I want to go? (The answer provides the objective.) How will I pace myself to see if I am getting there? (The answer gives us milestones, or key results.) The one thing an MBO system should provide par excellence is focus. This can only happen if we keep the number of objectives small." (*High Output Management*, p. 110, per [Thrive Street Advisors](https://www.thrivestreetadvisors.com/leadership-library/high-output-management))
   
   Grove's OKR prototype anticipated John Doerr's *Measure What Matters* by three decades. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Output_Management) Key result for a manager can cascade into objective for the subordinate.

5. **One-on-ones as the primary information channel** [PRIMARY — paraphrase from Ch. 4]
   One-on-ones are the subordinate's meeting (not the manager's). Their purpose is information flow — the manager gains ground-level signal; the subordinate gains coaching, unblocking, and prioritisation guidance. They should be held regularly because irregular cadence destroys their signalling value. [Evan's Notes Substack](https://evansamek.substack.com/p/high-output-management-by-andrew)

6. **Meetings taxonomy: process-oriented vs. mission-oriented** [PRIMARY — paraphrase, Ch. 4]
   Process-oriented meetings (one-on-ones, staff meetings, operational reviews) create the information-sharing infrastructure. Mission-oriented meetings are the *exception*, called for a specific decision. 
   > "Ideally, a manager should never have to call an ad hoc, mission-oriented meeting, because if all runs smoothly, everything is taken care of in regularly scheduled, process-oriented meetings." (*High Output Management*, p. 69–70, per [Thrive Street Advisors](https://www.thrivestreetadvisors.com/leadership-library/high-output-management))

7. **Indicators must be paired to avoid over-optimisation** [PRIMARY]
   > "Indicators tend to direct your attention toward what they are monitoring. It is like riding a bicycle: you will probably steer it where you are looking... your inventories could become so lean that you can't react to changes in demand without creating shortages. So because indicators direct one's activities, you should guard against overreacting. This you can do by pairing indicators, so that together both effect and counter-effect are measured." (*High Output Management*, p. 17, per [Thrive Street Advisors](https://www.thrivestreetadvisors.com/leadership-library/high-output-management))

8. **Strategic Inflection Points and 10× forces** [PRIMARY]
   A Strategic Inflection Point is triggered by a 10× change in any of six competitive forces (competitor, technology, customer, supplier, complementor, regulation). The danger: organisations in transition say one thing and do another (Grove's "strategic dissonance"). Detecting SIPs early requires attending to "Cassandras" — middle managers with ears close to the ground who sense change before executives do. [Goodreads summary](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/66863.Only_the_Paranoid_Survive); [YouTube discussion, 2024](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DyMnEyGEfJ0)

9. **Constructive confrontation as cultural norm** [ANECDOTE — Horowitz, primary first-hand]
   Grove institutionalised confrontation as a positive: arguing vigorously over facts and analysis, then executing the decision. Horowitz in 2026: "Andy Grove used to call it 'constructive confrontation'... Somebody was late to a meeting... Andy goes, 'All I have in this life is time and you're fucking wasting it.' ... that's a culture setter." [Sequoia Capital podcast, February 2026](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/)

**Strongest-case 2024–2026 application**

Grove's frameworks remain operating doctrine across Silicon Valley's most-cited operators. In late 2025, Ben Horowitz shared on the *My First Million* podcast a specific Grove technique for unsticking stalled projects: a daily 8 a.m. meeting with all team members to force blockers into the open, noting that "most of the time, the blockers are shockingly simple — confusion, permission gaps, or basic miscommunication." [a16z on X, December 2025](https://x.com/a16z/status/1995944302791590082) In February 2026, Horowitz at the Sequoia Founder Summit specifically named Ali Ghodsi (Databricks) as the most Grove-like operator he currently works with — "absolutely paranoid. The only person I've ever known as paranoid as Ali — I mean, I guess Elon is this level of paranoid, but Andy Grove was this level of paranoid." [Sequoia Capital podcast, February 2026](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/) The OKR framework Grove originated has become the default operating rhythm at Alphabet, Intel, LinkedIn, and hundreds of growth-stage companies via John Doerr's deployment at Google in 1999. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Output_Management) In August 2025, a practitioner essay in *Software Philosopher* applied Grove's TRM framework directly to 2025 AI-augmented engineering team management. [Software Philosopher, December 2025](https://softwarephilosopher.com/2025/12/26/high-output-management-my-reading-notes/)

**Strongest critique / failure mode**

The book's most systematic practitioner critique centres on three structural limits. First, *High Output Management* is deeply rooted in Intel's hierarchical, in-person, manufacturing-proximate management context. [BookClub review, 2025](https://vocal.media/bookclub/review-of-high-output-management-by-andrew-s-grove) "Modern management trends like remote work and agile methodologies are not covered extensively." The output-as-production metaphor applies poorly to creative, interpretive, or relational work where output is non-fungible. Second, Grove's framework is largely devoid of emotional intelligence considerations: it is measurable-output-centric in a way that can produce a mechanical, dehumanising management culture. Third — and most trenchant — the framework implicitly assumes that managers have *formal authority* over subordinates. In network/contractor/partner models (the Jetix holdco model), the manager's output equation still holds but the levers change: TRM-based oversight via contractual milestones rather than daily standups, and leverage comes through incentive design rather than direct coaching. The Goodreads review corpus surfaces a minority-but-serious critique: "Grove doesn't acknowledge how he concluded which/if any of his methods actually worked" — an absence of controlled evidence that any practitioner should note. [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/27140043-high-output-management)

**AI-native adaptation for 2026**

The core question: does task-relevant maturity apply to human-AI hybrid teams? There is no primary literature by Grove on this (he died in 2016), but the TRM construct translates with modifications that practitioners are actively working through. TRM for an LLM agent is not dispositional (the agent does not "grow") but is *capability-situational*: a reasoning model has high TRM for pattern-matched tasks (code review, synthesis, scheduling) and low TRM for novel judgment calls, ambiguous stakeholder situations, or contexts requiring tacit organisational knowledge. This means the Grovean prescription — structured task decomposition for low TRM, objective-setting and monitoring for high TRM — maps cleanly onto prompt engineering and agent guardrail design. A LinkedIn practitioner essay from August 2025 titled "Rethinking Output in the Age of AI — with Andy Grove as my compass" explicitly argues: "In the age of AI, measure outcomes, not activity. Pair every goal with a counterbalance, keep your data current/valid/relevant, treat tools like production equipment." [LinkedIn/Brand, August 2025](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/rethinking-output-age-ai-andy-grove-my-compass-wolfgang-brand-ogg7f) The *Boardy AI Substack* (April 2025) argued that Grove's paranoia principle needs updating: AI-era Strategic Inflection Points arrive in weeks, not years, so human detection loops must be replaced or supplemented by continuous AI monitoring of competitive signals. [Boardy's Substack, April 2025](https://boardyai.substack.com/p/only-the-paranoid-survive-in-the) For Jetix specifically: the managerial leverage equation becomes **Operator Output = Direct Work + Network Output under Operator Influence + AI-Agent Output under Operator Direction**. Uncertainty admitted: no peer-reviewed literature exists yet on TRM-for-agents; the field is actively developing as of 2026.

## Section B — 37signals / Basecamp (Jason Fried + DHH)

### Executive Summary

37signals — founded 1999, returned to that name in 2022 — is the company most responsible for formalizing the anti-scale, calm-company doctrine in software. Under Jason Fried (CEO) and David Heinemeier Hansson (CTO, creator of Ruby on Rails), it has remained bootstrapped, profitable, and deliberately tiny — maxing out at ~83 employees before cutting back — while publishing a cascade of books and practices that challenged the entire VC-hypergrowth consensus. The canon runs from *Getting Real* (2006) to *Rework* (2010), *Remote* (2013), *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work* (2018), and Ryan Singer's *Shape Up* (2019), which formalized their six-week project cycle. The 2021 Basecamp political controversy — in which Jason Fried banned internal political discussion, triggering the departure of ~30% of staff and Casey Newton's forensic Platformer coverage — remains the sharpest case study in how the "calm company" ethos can function as founder control rather than genuine equanimity.

### Core Doctrine — 5–10 Principles

The doctrine is scattered across the books but can be triangulated to these stable axioms:

1. **Calm company over growth theater.** *"Keep your costs low, keep your company as small as you possibly can, and make great stuff."* — Jason Fried, [Founders podcast, Feb 2026](https://www.davidsenra.com/episode/jason-fried). The company is the envelope; the product is the letter. [PRIMARY]

2. **Appetite, not estimates.** The foundational Shape Up inversion: *"Stop asking 'How long will this take?' and start with 'How much time is this worth?'"* Time budgets drive scope, not the other way around. — [Ryan Singer, Lenny's Newsletter, Mar 2025](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/shape-up-ryan-singer). [PRIMARY]

3. **Six-week cycles with a betting table.** Shaped work is pitched to a "betting table" every cycle. If a project doesn't get a bet, it simply doesn't ship — *there is no backlog.* Unbuilt projects are dropped, not queued. — *Shape Up*, Ch. 7–8. [PRIMARY]

4. **Shaping before building.** The senior/design track shapes work at "the right level of roughness" — enough clarity to remove rabbit holes, but not so much that it's already built. Teams get the shaped pitch and no task list; they create their own implementation tasks. — [Ryan Singer, *Shape Up* transcript](https://37signals.com/podcast/shape-up/). [PRIMARY]

5. **No middle management.** *"We don't have any middle management. We tried a little bit. That's why we had 83 people at one point… pulled back from that and go, 'That wasn't helpful.'"* — Jason Fried, [Founders podcast, Feb 2026](https://www.davidsenra.com/episode/jason-fried). [PRIMARY]

6. **Strong opinions as product differentiation.** 37signals publishes books, runs the REWORK podcast, and advocates loudly for its philosophy — this opinionation is part of the brand moat. [ANECDOTE]

7. **Asynchronous by default.** Communication norms are written-first and async; real-time disruption ("chronic busyness") is treated as a productivity hazard rather than a feature. *It Doesn't Have to Be Crazy at Work* (2018) codifies this. [PRIMARY]

8. **Software for people like you.** *"People who use our products buy our products, and it's the same person."* — Fried, [Founders podcast](https://www.davidsenra.com/episode/jason-fried). Enterprise software sold to buyers who don't use it is the anti-model. [PRIMARY]

9. **Profitability as creative freedom.** *"A business is very simple. You've got to make more than you spend."* — Fried ibid. Not growth-as-goal; profitability-as-prerequisite for keeping control. [PRIMARY]

10. **"Agent first, agent native" (2026 addition).** DHH: *"AI becomes super powerful when you give them these tools inside of a terminal."* Basecamp launched a full CLI for AI agents in March 2026. DHH uses Claude Code and Gemini 2.5 in tmux, rarely writing code by hand. [basecamp.com/agents, PRIMARY].

DHH's 2026 AI pivot is the single most interesting validation of 37signals' "small team" doctrine. With ~20 engineers and 10 designers, 37signals has maintained a 1:2 designer-to-engineer ratio for years. **Remote dogma in post-pandemic context.** 37signals pioneered remote work advocacy (*Remote*, 2013) but the post-2022 return-to-office wave has complicated the evangelical stance; the controversy also undermined the claim that remote + async culture is inherently more humane.

The holding-company model (5–50 AI-augmented operator units) maps to 37signals' multi-product expansion (Basecamp, HEY, ONCE, Fizzy) under a single thin corporate holding layer.

## Section C — Netflix (Reed Hastings / Patty McCord)

### Executive Summary

The Netflix culture model — codified in the 2009 "Culture Deck," a 125-slide PowerPoint authored primarily by Reed Hastings and Patty McCord — is the most widely imitated, most frequently misapplied, and most academically critiqued management framework in modern tech. Its four structural claims are: (1) talent density beats headcount; (2) freedom and responsibility are mutually reinforcing, not opposed; (3) context not control is the management mode; and (4) the "keeper test" is the operational mechanism for sustaining the above. The deck has been updated four times in 15 years, most recently in June 2024 and again in October 2025 — the latter renaming the core section from "Freedom and Responsibility" to "People Over Process" and introducing the title "The Best Work of Our Lives." Netflix's 2022–2024 business turbulence (ad tier, password crackdown) raised legitimate questions about whether culture held during adversity — the answer from the evidence is: broadly yes, with tactical adaptations. The deepest critique is not cultural but demographic: the keeper test assumes a liquid market of elite talent replacements, which makes the model inapplicable to roles with shallow talent pools or organizations operating outside major tech hubs.

### Top 5 Primary Sources

1. [jobs.netflix.com/culture — Netflix Culture Memo (PRIMARY)](https://jobs.netflix.com/culture) — The live, authoritative version of the culture memo as of 2025–2026. Four core principles, keeper test, dream team definition.
2. [about.netflix.com — "Sharing Our Latest Culture Memo" by Sergio Ezama (PRIMARY)](https://about.netflix.com/news/sharing-our-latest-culture-memo) — Official Netflix blog post accompanying the 2024 update (published March 2025 per metadata), describing the revision process and key changes.
3. *No Rules Rules: Netflix and the Culture of Reinvention* — Reed Hastings + Erin Meyer, 2020. [PRIMARY — book]
4. *Powerful: Building a Culture of Freedom and Responsibility* — Patty McCord, 2017. [PRIMARY — book]
5. [library.hbs.edu — Harvard Business School Working Knowledge, "Should Other Companies Follow Netflix's Lead?" Aug 2025 (SECONDARY)](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/freedom-fear-and-feedback-should-other-companies-follow-netflixs-lead) — Academic case study by Hubert Joly (HBS Senior Lecturer, former Best Buy CEO), "Netflix's Culture: Binge or Cringe?"

### Core Doctrine — 5–10 Principles

All quotes below are verbatim from the current live culture memo at [jobs.netflix.com/culture](https://jobs.netflix.com/culture) unless noted [PRIMARY]:

1. **Dream Team, not family.** *"We aim only to have high performers at Netflix — people who are great at what they do, and even better at working together... We model ourselves on a professional sports team, not a family."* [PRIMARY]

2. **Talent density.** Post-dot-com layoff, Netflix discovered that removing the bottom third of the company accelerated everything: *"With the right density of talent, there is very little process needed."* — Reed Hastings, quoted in [Startup Archive](https://www.startuparchive.org/p/netflix-founder-reed-hastings-on-the-importance-of-talent-density-and-using-a-keeper-test). [PRIMARY]

3. **Keeper test.** *"if X wanted to leave, would I fight to keep them?"* Those who fail the keeper test receive a generous severance (4+ months) so *"the company can find someone even better for that position."* — Live culture memo. The name originated from Hastings' father saying *"That's a keeper, Reed!"* after catching a fish. [PRIMARY]

4. **Context not control.** Managers *"give their teams the context and clarity needed to make good decisions instead of trying to control everything themselves."* [PRIMARY] Hastings describes this as the "informed captain" model: the person closest to a decision should make it, armed with full context.

5. **Freedom and responsibility.** *"Our vacation policy... is two words: 'Take vacation.' And our expenses policy is just five words: 'Act in Netflix's best interests.'"* [PRIMARY]

6. **Highly aligned and loosely coupled.** Cross-functional teams operate with independence so long as strategic alignment exists. *"Highly aligned and loosely coupled approach gives teams the freedom to move quickly and operate independently, while ensuring responsibility for the outcome."* [PRIMARY]

7. **Farming for dissent.** Leaders are expected to actively solicit disagreement before decisions are made. The 2025 culture memo update was itself produced with *"over 1,500 employee comments"* — a structural demonstration of the principle. [about.netflix.com, PRIMARY]

8. **Pay top of personal market.** *"We pay personal top of market for the role and location — a judgment about what that person could make in a similar role at another company, and what we would pay to keep or replace them."* [PRIMARY]

9. **Extraordinary candor as hygiene.** *"Ensuring constructive feedback is part of our everyday work (like brushing your teeth)."* [PRIMARY]

10. **Uncomfortably Exciting.** The 2025 update introduced this as a formal principle: *"To entertain the world, we need to be bold and ambitious. That means embracing the thrill of what's next — even when it's uncomfortable."* [jobs.netflix.com/culture, PRIMARY]

### Strongest-Case 2024–2026 Application

Netflix's own performance is the strongest case study. During the 2022 crisis — first subscriber loss in a decade, share price from ~$700 to below $200 — observers questioned whether the culture was contributing to stagnation. Instead, Netflix adapted: the ad-supported tier launched, password sharing enforcement drove net new subscriber growth (estimated $1–3.5bn in incremental revenue per [Macquarie Research](https://www.streamtvinsider.com/video/netflix-us-password-sharing-crackdown-catalyzes-ad-tier-growth-could-net-11b-2024)), and the company returned to growth without a cultural overhaul. The culture memo was updated in June 2024 — but [Variety reported](https://variety.com/2024/digital/news/netflix-updates-culture-memo-freedom-responsbility-people-process-1236046696/) *"no major shifts in foundational principles"*, only a shortening from 3,800 to 2,264 words and a reframing of "Freedom and Responsibility" as "People Over Process." The [October 2025 update](https://www.imdb.com/news/ni64661925/) added "The Best Work of Our Lives" as the new title without changing the substance. This is a culture that proved anti-fragile under real business stress — the keeper test, talent density, and context-not-control held. Harvard Business School's Hubert Joly noted: *"When growth slowed, questions arose if culture was partly to blame... Netflix reinvigorated it without major changes, vindicating it for top performers by tweaking strategy instead."* [HBS Working Knowledge](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/freedom-fear-and-feedback-should-other-companies-follow-netflixs-lead). [SECONDARY]

**Named adopters:** Index Ventures' *Scaling Through Chaos* (2023) explicitly cites Netflix's talent density as one of six scaling principles applied across companies including Airbnb, Figma, and Stripe in its VC portfolio — though this is a generalized adoption, not proof of formal Netflix-culture-deck implementation. [SECONDARY] Claims that specific companies have "adopted the Netflix model" circulate widely but are largely secondhand; verify any such claims against primary HR documentation.

### Strongest Critique / Failure Mode

**Keeper test applicability outside hyper-elite talent pools.** The mechanism presupposes (a) that you can reliably identify and hire replacements for anyone you let go, and (b) that the talent market is liquid enough to make the comparison meaningful. The HBS case study flags that Netflix's culture *"is not right for everyone,"* noting that polarizing effects include *"employees paranoid about termination, existing in a state of fear,"* and that *"'sunshining' (highlighting own mistakes) could cause job loss if revealing significant errors."* [HBS Working Knowledge, SECONDARY]

**Cross-cultural failure modes.** The memo itself acknowledges that Japanese workplace culture (non-confrontational by norm) and some European labor law environments strain implementation. *"Conflicts with local labor laws in some European countries"* are noted by the HBS case. [SECONDARY]

**The keeper test is a popularity test.** As critics on Reddit and LinkedIn have noted (with [James Farrin's LinkedIn post](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jamesfarrin_at-netflix-theyve-found-theres-one-question-activity-7417554310378622976-2dxU) drawing 24 comments on the failure modes): *"Gut reactions are shaped by recency bias, interpersonal chemistry, even mood. Someone who challenges us constructively might trigger relief. Someone likable but ineffective might trigger panic."* Survivor bias also corrupts the self-reported success of keeper-test outcomes. [SECONDARY/ANECDOTE]

**Conflation with generic "feedback culture."** The Netflix model is frequently described in management content as simply "radical candor" or "a culture of feedback" — this strips out the structural mechanics (keeper test, talent density as prerequisite, top-of-market pay, unlimited vacation as trust signal) that make it operational. Generic feedback without those structural supports is incoherent.

### AI-Native Adaptation for 2026

For Jetix (solo founder → matchmaker-partner network → holding structure):

- **Talent density is the only viable strategy at small scale.** A network of 5–50 AI-augmented specialist operators is talent density by construction — no room for average performers when each node is simultaneously operator and partner. The keeper test operates at the partnership level: *"If this operator announced they were leaving the Jetix network tomorrow, would I fight to restructure terms to keep them?"*
- **Context not control is the governance model for distributed operators.** In a zero-FTE holding structure, the founder cannot control each portfolio company's day-to-day. The Netflix mechanism — strategic context propagated via memos + farming for dissent via regular partner calls — is the correct governance primitive. The "informed captain" principle applies: each operator should have enough context to make their own decisions, with the founder as dissent-farmer rather than approver.
- **Freedom and responsibility translates to operator autonomy with explicit accountability.** The Netflix version of this is: no expense policy, but *"act in Netflix's best interests."* The Jetix version: operator autonomy over execution, with explicit revenue-share and milestone commitments replacing HR-style accountability.
- **Caution on keeper test timelines.** Unlike Netflix, where termination can happen in weeks, replacing a specialist operator in a niche network takes months. Apply the keeper test as a diagnostic (should we renew/deepen this partnership?) rather than an exit trigger.
- **Do not conflate talent density with elitism.** A Jetix operator network should include specialists with deep domain expertise who may not be "Netflix-level" communicators. The valuable signal is craft + accountability — not interview performance.

## Section E — Peter Drucker / *The Practice of Management* (1954), *The Effective Executive* (1967), *Management* (1973), *Innovation and Entrepreneurship* (1985), *Managing Oneself* HBR (1999)

He asks 'What are my organisation's goals and performance standards?' ... Only when the job is clear can the executive begin to ask 'What must I contribute?'" [paraphrase from primary; PDF excerpts at [Ole Miss XA](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf)]
   
   Drucker distinguishes: efficiency = doing things right; effectiveness = doing the right things. Knowledge work makes efficiency almost irrelevant because there is no external standard — the worker defines the task.

2. **Knowledge workers own the means of production** [PRIMARY]
   Drucker (1994): "Knowledge workers 'own the tools of production'... collectively they are the capitalists; increasingly, through their pension funds and other savings, the employees own the means of production." [cited in Sage Journals academic paper](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2158244016639631). This has a direct implication for network management: LLM agents and specialist contractors are each portable, self-contained productive units.

3. **Feedback analysis: the only reliable self-knowledge method** [PRIMARY]
   *Managing Oneself*, HBR 1999: "Whenever you make a key decision or take a key action, write down what you expect will happen. Nine or twelve months later, compare the actual results with your expectations." [PDF at Ole Miss XA](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf). Drucker argues this is the only reliable method to discover strengths — not introspection, not psychometric testing.

4. **Build on strengths; waste no effort on low-competence areas** [PRIMARY]
   > "One should waste as little effort as possible on improving areas of low competence. It takes far more energy and work to improve from incompetence to mediocrity than it takes to improve from first-rate performance to excellence." (*Managing Oneself*, HBR 1999, [Ole Miss XA PDF](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf))

5. **The five Drucker questions** [PRIMARY — paraphrase, *The Practice of Management* and consulting practice]
   Drucker's consulting methodology reduced to five strategic questions: (1) What is your mission? (2) Who is your customer? (3) What does your customer value? (4) What results do you seek? (5) What is your plan? [Andrew Sobel summary](https://andrewsobel.com/article/peter-druckers-five-magic-questions/). He reportedly spent entire days on question 1 alone, forcing clients to re-examine what business they were actually in.

6. **Knowledge-worker productivity requires the worker to define their own task** [PRIMARY]
   > "The key to improving knowledge-worker productivity... begins bottom-up, with asking knowledge workers themselves: What is your task? What should it be? What should you be expected to contribute? And what hampers you in doing your task and should be eliminated?" [Conference Board, citing Drucker](https://www.conference-board.org/research/human-capital-briefs/blg-00-1-2400). This bottom-up task definition is the reverse of industrial Taylorism.

7. **Where do I belong? — matching contribution to situation** [PRIMARY]
   *Managing Oneself*: "What does the situation require? Given my strengths, my way of performing, and my values, how can I make the greatest contribution to what needs to be done? And finally, what results have to be achieved to make a difference?" [Sage Journals, quoting Drucker 2005, p. 106](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2158244016639631). This is the foundation for a portfolio career and for placing specialist operators in appropriate STARS contexts (linking forward to Watkins, Section F).

8. **Organisations are "social technology" that translates technical capacity into results** [SECONDARY]
   Richard Straub at the 2024 Drucker Forum: "The key factor for translating [technology] into economic outcomes is the 'social technology' of management and leadership. The finest technology remains underperforming without proper management." [Global Peter Drucker Forum 2024 recap](https://xbn.news/global-peter-drucker-forum-2024-the-next-knowledge-work-2/). This quote, delivered at an event explicitly dedicated to Drucker's legacy, captures the most durable institutional reading.

9. **Managing relationships: understand how others work, not just how you work** [PRIMARY]
   *Managing Oneself*: "The first secret of effectiveness is to understand the people you work with and depend on so that you can make use of their strengths, their ways of working, and their values." [Ole Miss XA PDF](https://www.olemissxa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Peter-Drucker-Managing-Oneself-1.pdf). Each person's performance mode differs; managerial effectiveness depends on accommodation, not standardisation.

**Strongest-case 2024–2026 application**

The 2024 Global Peter Drucker Forum — the world's most credentialed annual gathering of management thinkers, convened in Vienna every November — dedicated its 16th edition to "The Next Knowledge Work," explicitly citing Drucker's dictum that "the most important contribution management needs to make in the 21st century is to increase the productivity of knowledge work and knowledge workers." [Global Drucker Forum 2024](https://xbn.news/global-peter-drucker-forum-2024-the-next-knowledge-work-2/) The forum's July 2024 Digital Drucker session titled "AI Booster: Knowledge is Power" directly addressed generative AI's impact on Drucker's knowledge-worker category. [YouTube, Drucker Forum July 2024](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0eCRoGOd8b4) In October 2024, *Drucker Forum Blog* contributor Karen Linkletter argued that AI does not supersede but *intensifies* Drucker's core challenge: "Workers in many ways are even more independent than in Drucker's era... The trajectory that Drucker discussed has continued, but technology has made knowledge work even more challenging to measure and motivate." [Drucker Forum Blog, October 2024](https://www.druckerforum.org/blog/the-future-of-knowledge-work-what-drucker-can-teach-usby-karen-linkletter/)

**Strongest critique / failure mode**

The scholarly literature identifies two structural limits. First, Drucker's knowledge-worker framework assumed that motivation is intrinsic — workers self-direct once given meaningful tasks and organisational alignment. This has been contested empirically by the post-pandemic "quiet quitting" phenomenon, where knowledge workers disengage even in nominally meaningful roles when overload and micromanagement appear. Linkletter (2024) notes this directly: "Knowledge-worker motivation in the 21st century has become increasingly delicate as organizations face phenomena such as 'quiet quitting.'" [Drucker Forum Blog, October 2024](https://www.druckerforum.org/blog/the-future-of-knowledge-work-what-drucker-can-teach-usby-karen-linkletter/) Second, Drucker's five questions and MBO framework implicitly assume a stable mission and a legible customer — both of which are under continuous revision in AI-native businesses where the product surface changes faster than annual planning cycles. The 2024 Drucker Forum acknowledged: "Old-school industrial management yields poor results in dynamics, creativity, innovation." [Global Drucker Forum 2024](https://xbn.news/global-peter-drucker-forum-2024-the-next-knowledge-work-2/) The Sage Journals academic study (2016) notes that while Drucker's knowledge-worker concept is theoretically rich, "he seldom brought these together into an integrated system" — meaning the practitioner must do synthetic work that Drucker himself did not. [Sage Journals, 2016](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2158244016639631)

**AI-native adaptation for 2026**

Drucker's framework ages *extremely well* in one dimension and poorly in another. The *Managing Oneself* doctrine — build on strengths, perform feedback analysis, know your contribution mode — is perhaps more actionable for a solo AI-native founder than for any other archetype: when your productive capacity scales through LLM agents, knowing precisely what you yourself are uniquely good at (the human differentiator) becomes the central strategic question. An agent can research; it cannot build the trust relationships, domain taste, and contextual judgment that create Jetix's moat. The Drucker question "What does the situation require?" maps cleanly onto the STARS diagnostic (Section F). Where Drucker ages poorly: his effectiveness doctrine assumes that the executive has *time* as the scarce resource. In 2026, with agents performing research, drafting, analysis, and scheduling, time scarcity shifts to *attention* scarcity (deciding what to direct agents toward) and *quality-of-judgment* scarcity (evaluating agent outputs). The MIT State of AI in Business 2025 report notes that 95% of enterprises see no ROI from GenAI pilots, with the failure mode being "shallow adoption vs. deep transformation" — a problem Drucker would diagnose as a mission clarity failure, not a technology failure. [Michael Watkins LinkedIn, citing MIT report, August 2025](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/michaeldwatkins_aireport2025pdf-activity-7363208921924227072-teRr) Uncertainty admitted: no Drucker Institute publication as of 2026 has explicitly published a framework for AI-as-knowledge-worker integration; the application above is extrapolation from the original doctrine.


## Section F — Michael Watkins / *The First 90 Days* (2003/2013) & *The Six Disciplines of Strategic Thinking* (2024)

**Executive summary**

Michael Watkins — Professor of Leadership and Organizational Change at IMD Business School, co-founder of Genesis Advisers, Thinkers50 Hall of Fame inductee — wrote *The First 90 Days* (2003; updated 2013) as the definitive guide to leadership transitions. The Economist called it "the onboarding bible." [Robert Glazer / Elevate Podcast, June 2024](https://robertglazer.com/elevate-podcast/michael-watkins-first-90-days-leadership/) The book's central insight is structural: every leadership transition has a *break-even point* at which the new leader's contribution equals the investment consumed in their ramp-up; the goal is to reach this point as fast as possible, ideally within 90 days. The *STARS* model — Startup, Turnaround, Accelerated-growth, Realignment, Sustaining-success — provides the situational diagnostic that determines which acceleration strategies apply. Watkins's 2024 book *The Six Disciplines of Strategic Thinking* extended the framework from onboarding to ongoing leadership, offering a cognitive skills model (pattern recognition, systems perspective, mental agility, structured problem-solving, visioning, political savvy) directly applicable to navigating AI-era turbulence. For a Berlin solo founder simultaneously managing five or more specialist operators *and* self-onboarding to new domains as the business pivots, the First 90 Days framework applies recursively — to each new operator relationship, to each new holding-company vertical, and to the founder's own periodic role redefinitions.

**Top 5 primary sources**

1. [PRIMARY] Watkins, Michael D. *The First 90 Days: Proven Strategies for Getting Up to Speed Faster and Smarter*. Harvard Business School Press, 2003; updated edition 2013. Primary source for all STARS, break-even, early wins, and learning acceleration doctrine. Full chapter structure summarised at [Will Patrick Notes](https://www.willpatrick.co.uk/notes/the-first-90-days-michael-watkins) and [Runn Summary](https://www.runn.io/blog/the-first-90-days-summary).

2. [PRIMARY] Watkins, Michael D. *The Six Disciplines of Strategic Thinking*. Harper Business, January 2024. ISBN: 9780063357969. New primary source extending pattern-recognition and mental-agility framework. [Porchlight Books](https://www.porchlightbooks.com/products/six-disciplines-of-strategic-thinking-michael-d-watkins-9780063357969); [Genesis Advisers](https://www.genesisadvisers.com/the-six-disciplines-of-strategic-thinking/).

3. [PRIMARY] Watkins, Michael D. Interview with Robert Glazer, "Elevate Podcast," June 2024 and January 2025. [Elevate Podcast](https://robertglazer.com/elevate-podcast/michael-watkins-first-90-days-leadership/). Primary-source 2024 interview covering First 90 Days, STARS model, transition coaching at Genesis Advisers, solo-founder and M&A transition applicability.

4. [PRIMARY] Watkins, Michael D. "How to Ride the Shifting Tide of AI Correctly." *Big Think+*, April 2025. [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ynzgn4slglg). Primary-source 2025 video on agentic AI and leadership adaptation; establishes Watkins's current stance on AI's disruptive implications for leaders.

5. [SECONDARY] Genesis Advisers. "The First 90 Days Can Make or Break Your Leadership Success. Here's How to Get It Right." June 2025 (co-authored with Aidan Slack-Watkins, co-founder of Veita.ai). [Genesis Advisers](https://www.genesisadvisers.com/the-first-90-days-can-make-or-break-your-leadership-success-heres-how-to-get-it-right/). 2025 secondary synthesis from Watkins's own platform, referencing agentic AI in the context of leadership transitions.

**Core doctrine — 5–10 principles**

1. **The break-even point and transition vulnerability window** [PRIMARY — paraphrase, *First 90 Days*, p. 1]
   Every leadership transition begins with a period of *net value consumption*; the leader must reach the break-even point — where contribution equals investment — as quickly as possible.
   > "The president of the United States gets 100 days to prove himself; you get 90. The actions you take during your first few months in a new role will largely determine whether you succeed or fail." (*First 90 Days*, p. 1, quoted at [Will Patrick Notes](https://www.willpatrick.co.uk/notes/the-first-90-days-michael-watkins))
   
   "For a mid-level leader, it takes approximately 6.2 months to get up to speed; applying the 90-day framework can accelerate this by up to 40%." [Readingraphics](https://readingraphics.com/book-summary-the-first-90-days/)

2. **STARS situational diagnostic** [PRIMARY — paraphrase, *First 90 Days*, Ch. 3]
   Five business situations, each requiring distinct strategies: *Startup* (build from scratch; high autonomy, high ambiguity), *Turnaround* (crisis response; rapid diagnosis, hard decisions), *Accelerated growth* (scale existing success; maintain culture while adding capacity), *Realignment* (revitalise before crisis hits; hardest because no burning platform), *Sustaining success* (protect and extend; stewardship over transformation). 
   > "Because of their differing imperatives, it is easy for heroes to stumble in realignment and sustaining-success situations and for stewards to struggle in start-ups and turnarounds." (*First 90 Days*, p. 82, quoted at [Will Patrick Notes](https://www.willpatrick.co.uk/notes/the-first-90-days-michael-watkins))

3. **The action imperative trap** [PRIMARY — paraphrase, *First 90 Days*]
   The most common transition failure is "the action imperative" — the pressure to appear competent by acting decisively before understanding context. Watkins at Elevate Podcast (2024): "The first 30 days are pretty much all listening. It's the hardest thing for people, but listen, meet, gather, and collect. This is probably the opposite of what most people want to do. The faster you change things, the more people get defensive." [Elevate Podcast, June 2024](https://robertglazer.com/elevate-podcast/michael-watkins-first-90-days-leadership/)

4. **Early wins: build credibility before driving change** [PRIMARY — *First 90 Days*, Ch. 5]
   > "Early wins excite and energize people and build your personal credibility. Done well, they help you create value for your new organization earlier and reach the break-even point much more quickly." (*First 90 Days*, Ch. 5, quoted at [Runn](https://www.runn.io/blog/the-first-90-days-summary))
   
   Early wins must align with what the boss cares about and be achievable *within* existing cultural norms — violating culture to win fast is a pyrrhic victory.

5. **Negotiate success: define the relationship with your boss/principal upfront** [PRIMARY — *First 90 Days*, Ch. 4]
   Watkins frames this as the single most underrated early-transition task. The new leader must negotiate: what are the top priorities? What constitutes success? What resources are available? What decisions require escalation? This conversation is the foundation for all subsequent autonomy. [Rick Lindquist Notes](https://www.ricklindquist.com/notes/the-first-90-days)

6. **Six disciplines of strategic thinking** [PRIMARY — *Six Disciplines*, 2024]
   Pattern Recognition, Systems Perspective, Mental Agility, Structured Problem-Solving, Visioning, Political Savvy — grouped into (a) recognition and prioritisation and (b) mobilisation. Watkins defines strategic thinking as "the set of mental disciplines leaders use to recognize potential threats and opportunities, establish priorities to focus attention, and mobilize themselves and their organizations." [PathWise Podcast, February 2024](https://pathwise.io/podcast/michael-watkins) The RPM cycle (Recognize → Prioritize → Mobilize) is the operational kernel.

7. **Managing oneself through transition: identity and control** [PRIMARY — Elevate Podcast, 2024/2025]
   Watkins (January 2025): "If you are all of the roles and you're leading yourself, then that's more of like a bipolar nature. President is more of an appropriate title than CEO." For M&A transitions specifically: "Identity, control, and competence" are the three failure modes when founders sell businesses and must integrate into new structures. [Elevate Podcast, January 2025 reissue](https://robertglazer.com/elevate-podcast/classics-michael-watkins-the-first-90-days-leadership-transitions/)

**Strongest-case 2024–2026 application**

Genesis Advisers reports serving "60 coaches" and "very large global clients," with the First 90 Days framework operationalised across C-level onboarding, M&A integration, and function-level promotions. [Elevate Podcast, June 2024](https://robertglazer.com/elevate-podcast/michael-watkins-first-90-days-leadership/) The IMD *First 90 Days®* programme (offered via open enrollment since 2022) continues to run cohorts in 2025–2026, applying the STARS model to live leadership transitions with peer and 1:1 coaching. [IMD](https://www.imd.org/leadership/f90d/the-first-90-days/) In November 2025, a working-towards.com podcast episode documented Watkins's continued thought leadership with updated emphasis on how transitions play out in organisations managing rapid AI adoption. [Working Towards Podcast, November 2025](https://working-towards.com/192michaelwatkins/) The *Six Disciplines* book (January 2024) was adopted quickly by senior-executive development programmes as a companion to *First 90 Days* — addressing the gap that onboarding is not just about early-phase tactics but about the ongoing cognitive posture required to lead in turbulence.

**Strongest critique / failure mode**

*The First 90 Days* is calibrated for the corporate hierarchical transition — executive hired into an existing reporting structure with a boss, a team, and an institution. Watkins himself acknowledges this in the Elevate Podcast (2024): "My thing is you are not a CEO if you are managing yourself. The CEO is the leader of an executive team. If you are all of the roles and you're leading yourself, then that's more of like a bipolar nature." [Elevate Podcast, January 2025](https://robertglazer.com/elevate-podcast/classics-michael-watkins-the-first-90-days-leadership-transitions/) This directly limits applicability to: (a) solo founders self-onboarding to new domains — no boss to negotiate with; (b) partnership/matchmaker networks where authority is negotiated rather than conferred. The Reddit *BusinessBooks* community notes: "Tailored more to a VP/C-level global blue-chip audience." [Reddit](https://www.reddit.com/r/BusinessBooks/comments/ha6gfi/the_first_90_days_by_michael_watkins/) The STARS taxonomy has no category for *recurring re-onboarding* — the solo operator who must repeatedly assume new functions as the business evolves — which may be the most common form of transition in an AI-native holding structure. The "negotiate success" framework presupposes a single principal; a matchmaker network has multiple parallel stakeholders, each with distinct expectations.

**AI-native adaptation for 2026**

Watkins is the canon figure who has most explicitly engaged with the AI question as of 2025–2026. In his *Big Think+* video (April 2025), he argues that leaders must "be the surfer, not the dinosaur": "You cannot afford to be the dinosaur. You must be the surfer. You need to be agile. You need to be adaptive. You need to embrace these systems and try out what's going on because if you don't understand it, you really can't hope to help your organization adapt and stay in front of this momentous, world-changing technology." [Big Think+, April 2025](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ynzgn4slglg) Watkins identifies three specific limitations of current AI systems that a leader must build workflows around: the *hallucination problem* (AI gives erroneous facts confidently), *programmed personality* (AI tries to make users happy; must be instructed not to), and *optimistic bias* (must be explicitly de-biased for high-stakes decisions). For Jetix's onboarding of AI-augmented specialist operators: the STARS framework applies to each agent deployment as much as each human operator. A new AI agent in a *startup* STARS context (new capability, no prior org context) requires high structure, explicit objective-setting, and close monitoring — exactly the low-TRM prescription from Grove. The Genesis Advisers June 2025 piece (co-authored with Aidan Slack-Watkins of Veita.ai) signals that Watkins's firm is actively building AI-native onboarding infrastructure. [Genesis Advisers, June 2025](https://www.genesisadvisers.com/the-first-90-days-can-make-or-break-your-leadership-success-heres-how-to-get-it-right/) Uncertainty admitted: no published Watkins paper or book as of Q1 2026 has explicitly applied the STARS model to AI agent deployment; the extrapolation above is structural inference from the framework's logic.


## Section G — Ben Horowitz / *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* (2014) & *What You Do Is Who You Are* (2019)

**Executive summary**

Ben Horowitz — co-founder of Andreessen Horowitz, former CEO of Opsware (sold to Hewlett-Packard for $1.6B in 2007) — occupies a unique position in the management canon as the only widely-read author who writes from the perspective of someone who built companies on the verge of failure and survived. *The Hard Thing About Hard Things* (2014) is explicitly an anti-management-book: a first-person account of the decisions that have no good answer — layoffs, firing friends, "eating shit," confronting existential crises. *What You Do Is Who You Are* (2019) is the cultural complement: a historical argument, drawing on Toussaint Louverture, the Samurai bushido code, Genghis Khan, and Shaka Senghor, that culture is not beliefs or values on walls but *behaviours* — specifically the behaviours that define how an organisation acts when no one is watching. In 2025–2026, Horowitz remains one of the most actively cited management thinkers in the practitioner community, appearing on Sequoia's Founder Summit (February 2026) and the Columbia Business School's AI and leadership series (September 2025). For Jetix, Horowitz provides the clearest doctrine on two specific problems: what wartime leadership looks like when the company is under existential threat, and how to build a behavioural culture across a distributed partner network where you cannot rely on hierarchy or employment law to enforce norms.

**Top 5 primary sources**

1. [PRIMARY] Horowitz, Ben. *The Hard Thing About Hard Things: Building a Business When There Are No Easy Answers*. HarperBusiness, 2014. Primary source for wartime/peacetime CEO, "The Struggle," layoff execution, one-on-ones, and organisational communication architecture. No canonical public URL; widely cited.

2. [PRIMARY] Horowitz, Ben. *What You Do Is Who You Are: How to Create Your Business Culture*. Harper Business, 2019. Primary source for culture-as-behaviours doctrine, historical case studies, shocking rules, and ethics. [a16z book page](https://a16z.com/books/what-you-do-is-who-you-are/).

3. [PRIMARY] Horowitz, Ben. "Wartime vs Peacetime: Ben Horowitz on Leadership." a16z podcast with Jorge Conde, August 2023 (republished January 2026). [a16z.com](https://a16z.com/podcast/wartime-vs-peacetime-ben-horowitz-on-leadership-2/). Primary-source 2023 video covering the wartime/peacetime distinction, culture as moat, and scaling communication.

4. [PRIMARY] Horowitz, Ben. Interview at Sequoia Founder Summit, "Ben Horowitz on What Makes a Great Founder," February 2026. [Sequoia Capital](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/). Primary-source 2026 interview covering founder mode critique, constructive confrontation, culture, and Grove legacy.

5. [PRIMARY] Horowitz, Ben. Talk at Columbia Business School with Dean Costis Maglaras, "Ben Horowitz on AI, Culture, and the Future of Innovation," September 2025. [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JFUSTctUxGM). Primary-source 2025 content on AI, culture, and leadership in disruption.

**Core doctrine — 5–10 principles**

1. **Wartime vs. peacetime CEO** [PRIMARY — *Hard Thing*, Ch. 7; a16z podcast 2023]
   > "If you read the management literature, it's almost entirely written for peacetime CEOs. So everything you learn about decision-making and delegation and 'don't micromanage' — all these things are very peacetime oriented, in the sense that in peacetime, you're much more focused on the development of the people and the development of the organization over the long-term." [YouTube, 2014](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kWnKLUesjV4)
   
   In wartime, the CEO must make a larger number of decisions faster because accuracy matters more than development. Peacetime processes actively obstruct wartime survival. The wartime/peacetime distinction is the most cited Horowitz concept and directly relevant to AI-era SIPs (connecting to Grove, Section A).

2. **Culture is behaviours, not beliefs** [PRIMARY — *What You Do Is Who You Are*; multiple primary interviews]
   > "Culture is not a set of beliefs, it's a set of actions... Who you are is what you do... the behaviors are the culture, not the values. When people say they have 20 values, I ask how many of those behaviors they have. If it's none, your culture is hypocrisy." [Startup Archive, quoting Commonwealth Club talk, 2019](https://www.startuparchive.org/p/ben-horowitz-culture-is-not-a-set-of-beliefs-it-s-a-set-of-actions-0a65)
   
   > "Your culture is how your company makes decisions when you are not there. It is how they behave when no one is looking. If you don't methodically set your culture, then two-thirds of it will end up being accidental, and the rest will be a mistake." [*What You Do Is Who You Are*, quoted at Sajith Pai](https://sajithpai.com/book-notes-excerpts-what-you-do-is-who-you-are-by-ben-horowitz/)

3. **"Shocking rules" as culture programming devices** [PRIMARY — *What You Do Is Who You Are*]
   Horowitz draws from Toussaint Louverture: install *shocking* rules that employees encounter daily — they must ask *why*, and the why encodes the culture. Amazon's no-PowerPoint rule is his contemporary example. The rule must be something the team encounters repeatedly, not occasionally. [Goodreads](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/44063692-what-you-do-is-who-you-are)

4. **"Don't eat shit nibbling": the layoff execution principle** [PRIMARY — *Hard Thing*, Ch. 8]
   Horowitz: "If you are going to eat shit, don't nibble." The most humane layoffs are executed in one decisive event, not in instalments. [Lawyers in the Making, quoting p. 61 and p. 275](https://lawyersinthemaking.substack.com/p/book-review-the-hard-thing-about) Multiple small layoffs destroy morale, trust, and the organisation's ability to recover. Simultaneous communication to all employees is mandatory.

5. **The Struggle** [PRIMARY — *Hard Thing*, p. 61–63]
   > "The Struggle is when you wonder why you started the company in the first place. The Struggle is when people ask why you don't quit and you don't know the answer... The Struggle is the land of broken promises and crushed dreams. The Struggle is a cold sweat... The Struggle is not failure, but it causes failure. Especially if you are weak. Always if you are weak. Most people are not strong enough." (*Hard Thing About Hard Things*, pp. 61–63, quoted at [Lawyers in the Making](https://lawyersinthemaking.substack.com/p/book-review-the-hard-thing-about))

6. **Decision debt is the worst debt** [PRIMARY — Sequoia, February 2026]
   > "Decision debt is the worst debt, by the way, because it paralyzes a company." [Sequoia Capital podcast, February 2026](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/)
   
   Over-deferral and hesitation — waiting for certainty that never arrives — are the primary pathology of founder CEOs transitioning out of operator mode.

7. **Constructive confrontation and bad news travelling fast** [PRIMARY — Sequoia, February 2026]
   > "I think really good companies, the very, very, very best companies tend to have founders and CEOs who ask pretty aggressive questions... If you're running away from the truth to preserve feelings, that's a very dangerous thing in a tech company." [Sequoia Capital podcast, February 2026](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/)
   
   Horowitz directly attributes the "constructive confrontation" formulation to Grove ("Andy Grove used to call it 'constructive confrontation'").

8. **The CEO is the company's communication architect** [PRIMARY — *Hard Thing*; cited in Under30CEO, 2026]
   Ben Horowitz: "a CEO's job is to design the company's communication architecture." [Under30CEO, March 2026, citing *Hard Thing*](https://www.under30ceo.com/managing-people-like-freelancer/) This is distinct from executing communication — the CEO designs the *system* that routes information efficiently without distortion.

9. **Culture as strategic moat** [PRIMARY — a16z Culture podcast, 2022; a16z podcast 2023]
   > "The thing that they did culturally that was the most important for them — [Okta's] trust — was the thing that went with the company. Because they were a security company, trust was always such a big thing for them... Can I tell what race or gender you are [when reviewing performance]? And if I can't, then I'm good." [a16z, How Best Leaders Build Culture, 2022](https://a16z.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-how-the-best-leaders-build-culture/)

**Strongest-case 2024–2026 application**

In September 2025, Horowitz gave a wide-ranging talk at Columbia Business School explicitly on "AI, Culture, and the Future of Innovation," positioning culture as the decisive variable in AI adoption: "AI is still in its infancy, and how the next wave of innovation may reshape every industry" — but what separates adopters is cultural, not technical. [Columbia Business School/YouTube, September 2025](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JFUSTctUxGM) In February 2026, the Sequoia Founder Summit interview produced several primary-source updates: Horowitz's nuanced critique of "founder mode" (valid against over-deference to hired executives, but dangerous when used to justify avoiding senior hiring altogether), his endorsement of Ali Ghodsi as the current best practitioner of Grove-Horowitz doctrine, and the "decision debt" formulation as a new principle. [Sequoia Capital, February 2026](https://sequoiacap.com/podcast/ben-horowitz-on-what-makes-a-great-founder/) The wartime/peacetime framework was revisited in the a16z Bio & Health Summit podcast (August 2023, republished January 2026 — signalling editorial reaffirmation of its currency). [a16z, January 2026](https://a16z.com/podcast/wartime-vs-peacetime-ben-horowitz-on-leadership-2/)

**Strongest critique / failure mode**

The most serious scholarly critique of Horowitz centres on survivorship bias and the inapplicability of extreme-crisis doctrine to normal organisational life. The PSmiths Bookshelf review (May 2025) argues that *The Hard Thing* is not a management book at all but "a book about how to be and become a certain kind of man" — one whose management advice is inseparable from a specific cultural archetype (confrontational, Scots-Irish honour culture, physically dominant) that neither scales nor transfers. [Mr. and Mrs. Psmith's Bookshelf, May 2025](https://www.thepsmiths.com/p/review-the-hard-thing-about-hard) The book's case studies all end in survival — by selection: Horowitz writes from the vantage of someone who survived. The frameworks for companies that did not survive are absent. Contrasting with Reid Hoffman's *Blitzscaling* (2018): Hoffman argues that the wartime/peacetime binary is false for network-effect businesses — the correct frame is *efficiency vs. speed*, where the right answer during certain scaling windows is to deliberately embrace inefficiency and even bad management as a price of capturing network effects before competitors. [YouTube, Hoffman Blitzscaling lecture](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=XiANesZ7YOA) Hoffman: "prioritizing speed over efficiency in an environment of uncertainty." The Horowitz doctrine of culture-as-behaviours has also been critiqued for underspecifying the principal-agent problem: in distributed organisations (precisely the Jetix model), behaviours are shaped by incentive architecture as much as cultural modelling. If the founder is not physically present to demonstrate the behaviours, the culture decays toward incentive-driven local optima. The historical case studies in *What You Do Is Who You Are* (Toussaint Louverture, Genghis Khan) have been criticised as "longshots" — analogies that illuminate rhetorical points but do not hold under structural analysis. [LinkedIn review, Francisco Castillo, 2020](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/what-you-do-who-ben-horowitz-review-francisco-castillo)

**AI-native adaptation for 2026**

Horowitz's wartime/peacetime framework is arguably *more* relevant in 2026, not less: an AI Strategic Inflection Point (AI-induced 10× change in competitive dynamics, per Grove Section A) triggers exactly the wartime CEO conditions Horowitz describes — decisions must be made faster than organisational development can absorb, and peacetime management norms actively obstruct survival responses. Horowitz has been explicit at Columbia (September 2025) that AI's disruptive impact is still early-stage and that the primary variable is cultural adaptability: organisations that have built cultures of honest information flow and rapid decision-making will adapt; those with "positive messaging" cultures that suppress bad news will be slow to recognise SIPs. [Columbia Business School/YouTube, September 2025](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JFUSTctUxGM) For Jetix specifically: the culture-as-behaviours doctrine must be operationalised differently when the "team" is a mix of human matchmaker-partners and LLM agents. An agent has no culture in the Horowitz sense — it has no social shame, no reciprocal loyalty, no pride in the company. Culture design for hybrid teams requires encoding desired behaviours into *prompt architecture, incentive contracts, and agent evaluation rubrics*, not into story, ritual, and rule. This is genuinely new territory that neither Horowitz nor anyone in the classical canon has addressed systematically. Uncertainty is high.


## Section H — Teal Organizations & Holacracy (Laloux / Robertson)

### Executive Summary

Frédéric Laloux's 2014 *Reinventing Organizations* (illustrated ed. 2016) synthesized a decade of fieldwork into a three-breakthrough model for "Evolutionary-Teal" organizations: self-management (distributing authority across roles rather than persons), wholeness (bringing the full self to work, discarding professional masks), and evolutionary purpose (letting an organization's direction emerge from collective sensing rather than top-down planning). His primary case studies — Buurtzorg (Dutch home-care, 15,000 nurses), Morning Star (tomato processing, $1B+ revenue, no managers), FAVI (French brass foundry), Patagonia, and Sun Hydraulics — demonstrated that radically decentralized structures can outperform hierarchical ones on both human and financial metrics. Brian Robertson's Holacracy formalized these intuitions into a constitutional governance system, most famously tested at Zappos from 2013–2019. That experiment became the canonical cautionary tale: Zappos partially retreated from Holacracy by 2017, reintroducing managers and shifting toward an internal-marketplace model — though it never fully abandoned decentralization. By 2024–2026 the teal movement has neither collapsed nor gone mainstream; it persists as a live-but-niche paradigm, with the [Teal Team's 2024 Landscape Map](https://thetealteam.com/the-new-teal-map-roadmap) documenting adoption at organizations including Bayer and Spotify. The most serious structural critique — advanced by academics at Harvard Business School and by practitioners-turned-skeptics — is that self-management works only when pre-conditions of cultural fit, right scale, and founder-sustained energy are met, and that "teal" consultants systematically understate the fragility of these conditions. For Jetix, the matchmaker-partnership network maps closely onto what Laloux calls the earliest viable teal precondition: a small cluster of professionals with shared purpose, no imposed hierarchy, and peer-to-peer coordination via agreements rather than job titles.

### Core Doctrine — 10 Principles

**The Three Teal Breakthroughs** (Laloux's primary framework):

1. **Self-management**: *"Instead of having these strict formal hierarchies… you distribute [managerial] responsibilities — there's still clear accountability, people now know who's responsible for what."* [PRIMARY — Laloux, Sounds True video, 2024] Authority is held by roles, not persons; anyone can act within their role without seeking permission; conflict is resolved through peer processes. Key mechanism at Morning Star: the Colleague Letter of Understanding (CLOU) — a bilateral contract negotiated peer-to-peer, replacing job descriptions assigned from above.

2. **Wholeness**: Invite the full person to work — not the professional mask. Practices include check-ins, reflective spaces, and governance that treats emotional and relational facts as organizational data.

3. **Evolutionary Purpose**: The organization has its own calling; its members listen to where it wants to go rather than imposing a founder's vision top-down. *"Don't talk about self-management, don't talk about teal organization — say: we can't continue treating nurses the way we treat them, where we don't trust them."* [PRIMARY — Laloux, Sounds True 2024, on the failure to communicate purpose authentically]

**Five operational principles derived from case studies:**

4. **Small autonomous teams as the basic unit**: Buurtzorg's teams of 10–12 nurses manage their own patient intake, scheduling, finances, and hiring, with one hard constraint: ≥61% of time in direct care. [SECONDARY — Buurtzorg case, Corporate Rebels 2025, https://www.corporate-rebels.com/blog/10-questions-about-buurtzorgs-self-management-model-answered]

5. **Advice process over consensus**: Any member may make any decision if they first seek advice from those affected and those with expertise. This is not consensus — the decision-maker retains authority. *"Self-management does not mean doing whatever you want; it's about being part of determining what to do."* [PRIMARY — Doug Kirkpatrick, Morning Star Co-Founder, via Enlivening Edge]

6. **Distributed roles over job titles**: Morning Star operates with 23 business units, no management positions, no HR department. [SECONDARY — Reinventing Organizations Wiki, https://reinventingorganizationswiki.com/en/cases/morning-star/]

7. **Peer-based conflict resolution**: Direct communication between parties, followed by peer mediation, followed only as a last resort by a community panel — no HR escalation by default.

8. **Transparent financials**: All performance data is open to all members, enabling distributed accountability rather than management surveillance.

9. **Leader-full, not leaderless**: *"What you see in self-managing organizations is that they tend to be very leader-full organizations — what you want is as much leadership as possible."* [PRIMARY — Laloux, Sounds True 2024] Leadership is contextual and distributed, not absent.

10. **Conditions for teal, not teal as a model**: *"I suggest that leaders don't try to engage their organization saying 'we'll become a Teal organization.' How sexy is it to become a concept?"* [PRIMARY — Laloux responding to Corporate Rebels, Enlivening Edge 2018] Laloux explicitly warned against "teal" becoming a consulting methodology — the deeper question is: what does authentic purpose demand?

### Strongest Critique / Failure Mode

**The Zappos cautionary tale** is the canonical case. From 2013–2015, Zappos (1,500+ employees, owned by Amazon) adopted Holacracy under CEO Tony Hsieh. In March 2015, an ultimatum was issued: embrace Holacracy or accept severance. 18% of employees (≈260) departed. [SECONDARY — Quartz, 2025; Forbes/Denning, 2015, https://www.forbes.com/sites/stevedenning/2015/05/23/is-holacracy-succeeding-at-zappos/] By 2017, Zappos had retreated from Holacracy's meeting bureaucracy and reintroduced managers, shifting to an internal-marketplace system where teams operate as small businesses with P&L accountability. After Hsieh's death in 2020, remaining Holacracy elements were further modified. The HBR analysis (Bernstein & Lee, 2016, [PRIMARY]) concluded: *"adopting self-management wholesale — using it to determine what should be done, who should do it, and how people will be rewarded across an entire enterprise — is hard, uncertain work."* The systemic critique has four dimensions:

1. **Scale mismatch**: Research identifies 250–500 employees as a threshold above which flat structures without clear governance show "haphazard execution." At Zappos (1,500+), coordination complexity overwhelmed the system. [SECONDARY — SI Labs, 2026, https://www.si-labs.com/en/articles/why-holacracy-fails/]

2. **Teal-as-consulting-fad**: The [Leadership Society critique (2022)](https://leadershipsociety.world/knowledgehub/articles/TealRetrospective/) argues Laloux's evidence base (12 hand-picked organizations) is insufficient for scientific claims, that the Spiral Dynamics consciousness model rests on "scarce" empirical evidence, and that most referenced organizations "would hardly identify with the book's spiritual theme." Seven years after publication, "a grassroot teal-ization of large companies is nowhere to be found."

3. **Installation vs. emergence**: A practitioner-turned-skeptic documented in a 2026 Substack essay (*New Ways of Working*, [SECONDARY — https://newwaysofworking.substack.com/p/the-book-i-didnt-expect-to-write]) that after years of coaching self-management: *"I started writing differently too. More questions, fewer answers. More uncertainty, less prescription…What if most people just don't want this?"* Teams reverted when consultants left. The conditions for complex systems to thrive *"have to emerge from within. They can't be installed from outside, no matter how good the installation team."*

4. **Founder-dependency**: At Zappos, *"after [Hsieh's] death, this driver was missing."* [SECONDARY — SI Labs 2026] Teal organizations frequently depend on a founding leader holding the evolutionary space — a structural vulnerability that undermines the model's scalability claims.

Laloux himself acknowledged this: *"If you want to see this transition, you still need one or two leaders who really have that hope, that dream, that vision, and they hold a space that's strong enough for this little bubble of the next consciousness to manifest."* [PRIMARY — Laloux, Sounds True 2024]

### AI-Native Adaptation for 2026

For Jetix's matchmaker-partnership network, the teal framework offers structural vocabulary more than a blueprint. The network is already teal-adjacent by design: no FTE hierarchy, peer accountability through project agreements, evolutionary purpose (Compounding Engineering as a practice, not a headcount strategy). The AI-native upgrade adds three dimensions Laloux did not anticipate:

**1. Advice process at machine speed.** Laloux's advice process assumed human consultation cycles of days or weeks. In 2026, an AI-augmented network can run parallel async consultations across 5–50 specialist partners in hours, with summaries surfaced by LLM. The CLOU (Colleague Letter of Understanding) concept maps directly onto AI-assisted scope agreements between Jetix and partner operators — auto-drafted, versioned, and tracked.

**2. Evolutionary purpose via signal-sensing AI.** Morning Star operators "set themselves targets for their part of the process, to stimulate continuous improvement" and compare against self-set targets. An AI dashboard can continuously surface market signals (client patterns, emerging capability gaps, competitive moves) and feed them into a living purpose document — actualizing what Laloux described philosophically.

**3. Teal at scale without the scale problem.** Buurtzorg's scaling breakthrough was *replication over expansion* — each new team of 10–12 is independent rather than a new org chart node. Jetix's partner-node model is isomorphic: each AI-augmented specialist operator is a Buurtzorg team of one. The 50-person central support office at Buurtzorg scales to a solo founder + AI coordination layer at Jetix. The "teal fad" critique (installation by consultants, reverting when they leave) does not apply when the founder *is* the purpose-holder.

**Key question answered:** A matchmaker-partnership network *is* a de facto teal structure — it exhibits self-management (no one in the network reports to Jetix), peer accountability (partner relationships governed by agreements not hierarchy), and evolutionary purpose (the network's shape is determined by where demand emerges). The absence of wholeness practices (Laloux's second breakthrough) is the gap most worth intentionally closing as the network scales beyond 5 active operators.


## Section I — Modern Solopreneur / Small-Team Canon

The solopreneur canon spans three generations of voice: the lifestyle-design pioneers of the 2000s (Ferriss, Guillebeau), the purpose-led founder-philosophers of the 2010s (Sivers, Godin, Graham, Ravikant), and the AI-native solo operators of 2024–2026 (Welsh, Levels, Dinh, Vassallo, Kahl). ### Core Doctrine — Synthesized by Claim-Pattern

#### Cluster 1: Leverage Is the Only Honest Variable (Ravikant / Graham)

Naval Ravikant's foundational framework, distributed through the [*Almanack* (Jorgenson, 2020)](https://bagerbach.com/books/the-almanack-of-naval-ravikant/) [SECONDARY compiled-primary], identifies four types of leverage, explicitly ranked:

- **Labor** (oldest, worst — permission required, requires managing humans)
- **Capital** (permission required from investors/lenders)
- **Media** (permissionless — content created once, works while you sleep)
- **Code** (permissionless — *"the final form of leverage… products with no marginal cost of replication"*)

The key insight: *"Code and media are permissionless leverage. They're the leverage behind the newly rich."* [PRIMARY — nav.al, 2019] In 2026, for an AI-native solo operator, a fifth leverage type has emerged: **AI agents** — permissionless, asynchronous, effectively free to reproduce. This directly extends Naval's framework rather than contradicting it.

The corollary: *"In an age of infinite leverage, judgment is the most important skill."* [PRIMARY — nav.al] When leverage is abundant and cheap, the constraint is the quality of the person directing it. This is the direct antecedent of Welsh's 2025 AI-expertise claim.

Graham's [Superlinear Returns essay (2023)](https://paulgraham.com/superlinear.html) [PRIMARY] formalizes the math: *"Whenever how well you do depends on how well you've done, you'll get exponential growth."* [PRIMARY — Graham, 2023] Compounding applies to audiences, infrastructure, knowledge, and reputation. The heuristic: *"Seek work that compounds… if you're not learning, you're probably not on a path that leads to superlinear returns."* For Jetix, "Compounding Engineering" is the correct name for this dynamic — each engagement compounds reputation and methodology, creating superlinear returns over time.

#### Cluster 2: Founder-Mode as the Anti-Manager Manifesto (Graham, 2024)

Graham's September 2024 [Founder Mode essay](https://paulgraham.com/foundermode.html) [PRIMARY] generated over 20 million Twitter views within days of publication. Its core claim: *"There are things founders can do that managers can't, and not doing them feels wrong to founders, because it is."* [PRIMARY] The conventional advice — *"hire good people and give them room to do their jobs"* — *"often turns out to mean: hire professional fakers and let them drive the company into the ground."* [PRIMARY] Founder Mode breaks the principle that CEOs engage only via direct reports: skip-level meetings become the norm; involvement in details is a feature, not a bug.

For a solo founder running a partner network rather than an employee org, this essay is less prescriptive (there is no manager mode to resist) and more confirmatory: the founder's judgment is the irreducible core of the operation, and systems that deplete it (unnecessary meetings, delegated strategy) are organizational pathogens.

Graham's [2024 essay "Writes and Write-Nots"](https://paulgraham.com/writes.html) [PRIMARY] extends this: *"The result will be a world divided into writes and write-nots."* Writing is thinking; the ability to write clearly is a proxy for the ability to reason clearly. In AI-saturated 2026, the ability to reason clearly is the last moat a solo knowledge business can defend.

#### Cluster 3: The Anti-Scale Ethic (Sivers / Godin)

Derek Sivers's [*Anything You Want* (2011)](https://www.goodreads.com/work/quotes/16836190-anything-you-want) [PRIMARY] argues from personal experience (building, running, and selling CD Baby for $22M) that business is not primarily about scale or money but about designing a life: *"In the end, it's about what you want to be, not what you want to have."* [PRIMARY — Sivers, 2011] Two constraints define Sivers's model: the "Hell Yeah or No" filter (*"When deciding whether to do something, if you feel anything less than 'Wow! That would be amazing! Absolutely! Hell yeah!' — then say no"*) [PRIMARY] and the owner-exit test (*"To be a true business owner, make it so that you could leave for a year, and when you came back, your business would be doing better than when you left."*) [PRIMARY] This is the Berkshire/Constellation structure in miniature: build systems that don't require founder presence.

Sivers's 2024 [*Useful Not True*](https://sive.rs/u) [PRIMARY] adds an epistemological layer: *"This book is about reframing — changing how you think about something — and choosing a perspective that's useful to you right now, whether or not it's universally true."* [PRIMARY — sive.rs] For Jetix, this is operationally significant: the question "is this partner relationship structurally teal or not?" is less useful than "does framing it as a teal partnership produce the behaviors I want?"

Seth Godin's canon ([*The Dip*, 2007; *Tribes*, 2008; *The Icarus Deception*, 2012](https://seths.blog)) [PRIMARY] contributes three durable claims: (1) tribes (small groups with shared identity and purpose) are the unit of market change, not mass audiences; (2) the Icarus Deception — we are taught to fear flying too high, but the real danger is flying too low; (3) the linchpin is irreplaceable because they make judgment calls, not because they execute reliably. In 2024–2026, Godin's blog continues to publish daily, with recent posts emphasizing: the second circle (what do your supporters tell *their* friends?), value-based pricing over cost-plus, and the importance of solving systemic problems rather than symptoms.

### Strongest Critique / Failure Mode

The deepest structural critique of the solopreneur canon is that it **systematically underweights coordination costs and platform risk**. Tony Dinh's Black Magic sale in 2023 (forced by Twitter's API price hike to $42K/month against $14K revenue) is the canonical example of platform risk at solo scale. [PRIMARY — Dinh story, supabird.io profile] The bootstrapper-optimistic "build on someone else's platform" doctrine assumes platform stability that 2022–2024 demonstrated to be false. The second failure mode is **income ceiling without systems**: Sivers's own warning — *"Being self-employed feels like freedom until you realize that if you take time off, your business crumbles"* [PRIMARY — *Anything You Want*] — applies to knowledge businesses that have not productized their expertise. The third failure mode is **niche collapse**: Walling's niche doctrine works until AI commoditizes the niche itself (e.g., solo content agencies dissolved by LLMs in 2024–2025).

### AI-Native Adaptation for 2026

For Jetix, the synthesis is:

1. **Naval's leverage taxonomy as strategic filter**: Before taking on any activity, ask: does this compound (superlinear)? Does it create permissionless leverage (media, code, AI agents)? Consulting retainers are labor leverage — necessary early-stage, but must be systematically converted into codified methodology (media) and AI-tooling (code).

2. **Welsh's credibility moat as competitive strategy**: In a world where AI generates noise at near-zero cost, verified judgment (track record, named results, a recognizable perspective) is the only non-commodifiable asset. Jetix's *Compounding Engineering* brand is this moat.

3. **Vassallo's portfolio logic as risk management**: Treat each new engagement type and product as a small bet. Don't optimize any single line until it demonstrates traction. The matchmaker-partner network is a portfolio of small bets on specialist operators — structurally aligned with Vassallo's philosophy.

4. **Levels as operational benchmark**: The $3.5M ARR, 90%+ margin, near-zero headcount model is the target state architecture. AI writes the code, AI runs the first-pass analysis, AI drafts the deliverables — the founder's judgment shapes and signs off. Levels's 2015 observation — *"Instead of hiring people, do things yourself to stay relevant"* [PRIMARY — levels.io archive, 2019] — now means: build AI systems that augment doing-it-yourself rather than hiring humans to dilute your judgment.

5. **Graham's "Writes and Write-Nots" warning as cultural marker**: The Berlin solo founder in 2026 who writes clearly (reasons publicly, documents thinking, produces original frameworks) will be structurally advantaged over AI-amplified generalists who cannot. Jetix's public thought-leadership is not marketing — it is the primary product of the leverage stack.

## Section J — Holding Company Doctrine (Buffett / Leonard / Tiny / Micro-Holdcos)

### Executive Summary

The holding company doctrine — as practiced by Buffett, Leonard, Wilkinson, and the micro-holdco wave — converges on a small set of structural truths that have proven robust across asset classes, sizes, and eras. **The core pattern is this: extreme decentralization of operations combined with extreme centralization of capital allocation.** Charlie Munger captured it directly: *"There are two main reasons Berkshire has succeeded. One is its decentralization. Decentralization almost to the point of abdication. The other reason is our extreme centralization of capital deployment."* [[25iq.com](https://25iq.com/2015/09/26/a-dozen-things-ive-learned-from-charlie-munger-about-the-berkshire-system/)] Across all practitioners, the holdco is not a management layer — it is a capital-routing and talent-selection engine. Operators run their businesses; the holdco selects, incentivizes, and deploys cash. The second shared pattern is **permanent capital orientation**: the never-sell doctrine maximizes the compounding surface area of each acquired business, removes the forced-exit pressure that distorts PE decision-making, and creates a trusted buyer brand that attracts better deal flow at lower prices. Third, every successful holdco invests heavily in **operator quality and incentives** rather than systems and monitoring — the Berkshire playbook of "hire well, manage little" is replicated by Leonard's abdication model and Wilkinson's "lazy leadership." Fourth, **cashflow discipline** (not GAAP, not EBITDA as typically adjusted, but distributable free cash flow) is the north star metric at every level of the hierarchy. Fifth, the holdco model's Achilles heel — acknowledged by Buffett explicitly by 2023-2024 — is that **scale eventually defeats the capital allocation edge**: size compresses the opportunity set and forces progressively lower-return deployment. For a solo founder in 2026 building Jetix toward a micro-holdco of 5–50 AI-augmented operators, the doctrine translates into: acquire or network-in proven specialist operators, leave them alone operationally, centralize pricing strategy and capital routing at the holdco level, never sell the compounders, and maintain a ruthless hurdle rate on every new engagement or acquisition.

### 1. Warren Buffett / Berkshire Hathaway

**Source:** Berkshire Hathaway Letters to Shareholders 1977–2024 [[berkshirehathaway.com](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/letters.html)] [PRIMARY]

Berkshire Hathaway's holdco doctrine is the oldest and most systematically documented in business history, and Buffett's letters remain the primary scripture for every practitioner who followed. The doctrine rests on three interlocking pillars that have not meaningfully changed in 50 years: decentralization of operations, centralization of capital allocation, and permanent ownership of businesses with wide moats.

**Decentralization principle.** The 2010 letter contains the canonical statement: *"At Berkshire, managers can focus on running their businesses: They are not subjected to meetings at headquarters nor financing worries nor Wall Street harassment. They simply get a letter from me every two years and call me when they wish. And their wishes do differ: There are managers to whom I have not talked in the last year, while there is one with whom I talk almost daily. Our trust is in people rather than process. A 'hire well, manage little' code suits both them and me."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2010ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2010ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] The two-year letter to managers specifies the full mandate: guard reputation, report bad news early, confer on post-retirement benefit changes and unusually large capex, adopt a fifty-year time horizon, and submit successor recommendations. That is the entirety of the control mechanism. [[25iq.com](https://25iq.com/2015/09/26/a-dozen-things-ive-learned-from-charlie-munger-about-the-berkshire-system/)] [SECONDARY]

**Capital allocation.** Buffett's 2022 letter contains the clearest distillation of his self-assessment: *"In 58 years of Berkshire management, most of my capital-allocation decisions have been no better than so-so. Our satisfactory results have been the product of about a dozen truly good decisions — that would be about one every five years."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2022ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2022ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] The 2022 letter also sharpens the operator/holdco division: *"Berkshire directs capital allocation at these subsidiaries and selects the CEOs who make day-by-day operating decisions."* The 2024 letter is candid about errors: *"During the 2019–23 period, I have used the words 'mistake' or 'error' 16 times in my letters to you."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY]

**Circle of competence and moats.** The 2022 letter states: *"Our goal in both forms of ownership is to make meaningful investments in businesses with both long-lasting favorable economic characteristics and trustworthy managers. Charlie and I are not stock-pickers; we are business-pickers."* The 2020 letter continues this: *"Charlie and I will simply deploy your capital into whatever we believe makes the most sense, based on a company's durable competitive strengths, the capabilities and character of its management, and price. If that strategy requires little or no effort on our part, so much the better."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2020ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2020ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY]

**Intrinsic value as north star.** Buffett repeatedly returns to operating earnings as the only honest metric, rejecting GAAP net income due to mark-to-market distortions. The 2023 letter specifies operating earnings of $37.4 billion for 2023 vs. GAAP net earnings of $96 billion — a gap of $58.6 billion attributable entirely to unrealized investment gains. [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY]

**What Buffett said 2020–2024 that he didn't say before** (see deep-dive section below): The post-pandemic letters are notable for increasing candor about size as a constraint, a detailed forest fire / Forest River acquisition story about incentive alignment, explicit succession planning (Greg Abel named), and the Munger tribute as "architect." The core decentralization doctrine did not change, but Buffett's acknowledged limitations became sharper.

### 2. Mark Leonard / Constellation Software

**Source:** Constellation Software President's Letters 2007–2021 [[csisoftware.com](https://www.csisoftware.com/category/pres-letters)] [PRIMARY]; In Practise interview with Former CSI M&A Executive [[inpractise.com](https://inpractise.com/articles/constellation-software-competition-hurdle-rates-and-deal-flow)] [SECONDARY]

Constellation Software is the most rigorous execution of the holdco doctrine in software history. Where Berkshire operates in insurance, railroads, and consumer brands, Constellation applied identical structural logic to hundreds of niche vertical-market software (VMS) businesses: acquire monopoly-like software in verticals too small for large players, hold permanently, decentralize operations to the general manager level, and redeploy cash into new acquisitions at a disciplined hurdle rate.

**Acquire-and-hold, vertical-market focus.** Leonard's 2016 letter states explicitly: *"CSI's strategy is to be a good owner of hundreds (and perhaps someday thousands) of growing autonomous small businesses that generate high returns on capital. Our strategy is unusual. Most CEOs of public companies would rather run a single big business."* [[quartr.com](https://quartr.com/insights/business-philosophy/mark-leonard-the-mysterious-brain-behind-constellation-software)] [SECONDARY, citing letter] The VMS focus is deliberate: vertical software is mission-critical, has high switching costs, and is too small for strategic buyers to target, creating a durable supply of acquisition targets at reasonable prices.

**Hurdle rate discipline.** The hurdle rates are tiered by deal size: 30% IRR for businesses under $1M revenue, 25% for the $1M–$4M band (approximately 90% of deals), 20% for larger deals above $4M, and a 15% floor for transformational deals above $50M. [[inpractise.com](https://inpractise.com/articles/constellation-software-competition-hurdle-rates-and-deal-flow)] [SECONDARY] The 2017 President's Letter contains the single most important statement on hurdle discipline: *"I recently worked on a large transaction. With every day that passed, I could feel my commitment to the process growing… not because the news was getting better, just because I was spending more time on the prospect. The investment didn't quite meet our hurdle rate. We were not able to negotiate a structure that got us an extra couple of points of IRR, and the big one got away. The difference between investing and not, was tiny."* [[acquirersmultiple.com](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2024/01/mark-leonard-raise-the-bar-how-hurdle-rates-drive-profitable-investments/)] [SECONDARY, citing 2017 letter]

**Never-sell doctrine.** Leonard writes in the letters that *"Constellation's objective is to be a perpetual owner of inherently attractive software businesses."* [[biographynuts.com](https://www.biographynuts.com/p/chapter-154-constellation-software)] [SECONDARY, citing letters] The permanent-ownership framing is not sentiment — it is a deal-sourcing moat. Sellers who care about their teams and customers select Constellation at below-maximum valuations because they trust the business will not be flipped. The In Practise interview confirms this explicitly: "One specific business knew he wasn't getting the biggest check, but it was big enough for him to be happy and he cared about the team he left behind." [[inpractise.com](https://inpractise.com/articles/constellation-software-competition-hurdle-rates-and-deal-flow)] [SECONDARY]

**Decentralized operating groups.** The letters describe abdication as the deliberate management model: *"We've handled our geometric growth to date by largely abdicating management to the general managers of each of our vertical businesses. We have a very thin overlay of infrastructure at CSI."* [[biographynuts.com](https://www.biographynuts.com/p/chapter-154-constellation-software)] [SECONDARY, citing letters] Best practices are shared across the 199+ business units but never mandated. Leonard's unique contribution vs. Buffett is the explicit use of shared metrics (the "five stable ratios" of attrition, professional services revenue, support revenue, maintenance, and pricing) as cultural tools rather than compliance mechanisms.

**Organic growth vs. acquisition.** Leonard was consistently skeptical of organic growth as a signal — he built frameworks to separate "maintained" growth (keeping existing customers) from "new" growth (adding customers), and measured attrition as lost + downgrade combined, not just churn count. [[inpractise.com](https://inpractise.com/articles/constellation-software-competition-hurdle-rates-and-deal-flow)] [SECONDARY]

**Why Leonard stopped writing after 2021.** The last public President's Letter is from February 2021. [[csisoftware.com](https://www.csisoftware.com/category/pres-letters)] [PRIMARY] No 2022, 2023, or 2024 letter appears on the archive. Per a March 2026 press release announcing Leonard's departure from the board, *"Mark Leonard, the founder and former President of Constellation, will not stand for re-election to the Board of Directors."* [[csisoftware.com/docs/.../director-press-release---mar27-26.pdf](https://www.csisoftware.com/docs/default-source/press-releases/director-press-release---mar27-26.pdf)] [PRIMARY] The operational succession to Mark Miller has been in motion for several years. Leonard's deliberate withdrawal from public communication — combined with the depth of his earlier letters — is itself a signal: the system is designed to be self-sustaining without a charismatic author.

### 3. Andrew Wilkinson / Tiny

**Sources:** Tiny Ltd. investor presentation 2023 [[q4cdn.com](http://s202.q4cdn.com/676416790/files/doc_presentation/2023/08/TINY-Presentation-28-08-23.pdf)] [PRIMARY]; Andrew Wilkinson, *Never Enough* (2024) [[benbellabooks.com](https://benbellabooks.com/shop/never-enough/)] [PRIMARY]; Andrew Wilkinson 2023 thread on Tiny lessons [[hackernoon.com](https://hackernoon.com/andrew-wilkinson-lessons-learned-based-on-the-last-20-years-of-running-tiny)] [PRIMARY]; Wilkinson on Lenny's Newsletter, 2025 [[lennysnewsletter.com](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/ive-run-75-businesses-andrew-wilkinson)] [SECONDARY]

Andrew Wilkinson built Tiny as an explicit imitation of Berkshire Hathaway applied to profitable internet businesses. The 2023 investor presentation states it directly: *"In 2014, learned about Warren Buffett and were inspired by his hands-off, decentralized model. Began spinning out each business and hiring CEOs. Each business grew faster with a dedicated CEO."* [[q4cdn.com](http://s202.q4cdn.com/676416790/files/doc_presentation/2023/08/TINY-Presentation-28-08-23.pdf)] [PRIMARY]

**Operating model.** Tiny acquires majority or full stakes in "profitable, simple, and often boring" internet businesses with a minimum $500K/year in annual profit, 3–5+ years of operating history, and a strong team or simple enough operations that no team is required. [[colinkeeley.com](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/andrew-wilkinson-tiny-capital-operating-manual)] [SECONDARY] The head office has two functions only: capital allocation and management selection/incentivization — identical to the Berkshire structure. [[q4cdn.com](http://s202.q4cdn.com/676416790/files/doc_presentation/2023/08/TINY-Presentation-28-08-23.pdf)] [PRIMARY]

**"Lazy leadership" / management by abdication.** Wilkinson describes his management philosophy as: *"A good CEO should be able to get run over by an ice cream truck and have the company continue to operate for months or years without a problem."* [[sbo.financial](https://sbo.financial/blog/financial-teardowns/tiny-capital-financial-teardown/)] [SECONDARY, quoting Wilkinson] Controls are minimal: CEOs get a high-limit credit card (~$10K), cannot enter contracts over $50K without board approval, and have no direct bank account access. There are no cross-company synergy mandates — every inter-company transaction is paid at full market rate. [[colinkeeley.com](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/andrew-wilkinson-tiny-capital-operating-manual)] [SECONDARY]

**AeroPress, Dribbble, MetaLab.** The AeroPress acquisition — paying ~$70M for the inventor Alan Adler's coffee maker — is the signature Tiny deal: a durable consumer brand with a cult following, no VC pressure, simple operations, and genuine organic growth. Online sales grew 500% post-acquisition. [[goodreads.com](https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/199348906-never-enough)] [SECONDARY, citing Never Enough]

**Anti-goals framework.** The *Never Enough* book documents Wilkinson's "anti-goals" acquisition strategy: before acquiring any business, map out what you hate about work (meetings, technical complexity, regulatory exposure) and filter out businesses that generate those. The framework produces the "New Zealand company" portfolio: durable, self-sufficient, not dependent on any single platform for traffic or revenue, growing quietly, and not attracting attention from large strategic acquirers. [[benbellabooks.com](https://benbellabooks.com/shop/never-enough/)] [PRIMARY]; [[colinkeeley.com](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/andrew-wilkinson-tiny-capital-operating-manual)] [SECONDARY]

**20 years of Tiny lessons (2023 thread).** Wilkinson's 2023 public thread on lessons learned is one of the most operationally specific practitioner documents available. [[hackernoon.com](https://hackernoon.com/andrew-wilkinson-lessons-learned-based-on-the-last-20-years-of-running-tiny)] [PRIMARY] Key anti-patterns he identifies: advisory board equity without skin in the game ("thanks for the free equity"); cost-plus contracts (incentivize overruns); synergy mandates between acquired companies (rarely works unless management teams organically want it); hiring big-company operators for small businesses ("no scrappiness"); and CEOs hoarding cash when dividend incentives don't align.

**2024–2026 updates: public company transition and critique.** Tiny went public via reverse merger in April 2023 at ~$1B equity value. [[matrixbcg.com](https://matrixbcg.com/blogs/brief-history/tiny)] [SECONDARY] FY2024 total revenue was $194.2M with adjusted EBITDA of $31.0M. Both Wilkinson and Sparling stepped down as Co-CEOs in June 2024. Critics have questioned whether Tiny's 2023 EBITDA of $46.4M was inflated by one-time non-cash items, and whether the public-company reporting burden contradicts the "owner's mindset" philosophy. [[thevalueroad.substack.com](https://thevalueroad.substack.com/p/tiny-ltd-a-collection-of-lousy-internet)] [SECONDARY] Wilkinson declined Charlie Munger's offer to merge Tiny into Daily Journal Corporation — a decision he frames in *Never Enough* as preserving personal freedom over liquidity. [[reddit.com/r/fatFIRE](https://www.reddit.com/r/fatFIRE/comments/1f50xsw/never_enough/)] [SECONDARY, citing book] The Wilkinson/Munger meeting is the most vivid anecdote in the holdco literature about the tension between scale (Munger's offer: ~$800M liquid) and the operator's intrinsic motivation to stay in the game.

### 4. Nick Kokonas — Tock / Alinea Group

**Sources:** Tim Ferriss Show transcript, episode #341 (2018) [[tim.blog](https://tim.blog/2018/10/22/the-tim-ferriss-show-transcripts-nick-kokonas/)] [PRIMARY]; Tim Ferriss Show transcript, episode #2 with Kokonas (2020) [[tim.blog](https://tim.blog/2020/05/17/nick-kokonas-2-transcript/)] [PRIMARY]; This Week in Startups interview, August 2021 [[youtube.com](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-f_467baE1k)] [SECONDARY]; Patrick O'Shaughnessy / Investor's Field Guide interview [[kenburgin.com.au](https://www.kenburgin.com.au/productivity-cost-control/cost-control/i-love-this-business-interview-with-nick-kokonas-of-alinea-and-tock/)] [SECONDARY]

**Honest caveat up front:** Kokonas does not describe himself as a "holdco operator" and there is no published body of holdco-doctrine writing attributed to him in the same register as Buffett or Leonard. He is included here for two distinct contributions: (1) his derivatives-trader mental model applied to owning and operating multiple interdependent businesses, and (2) his articulation of owning the customer relationship as the foundational moat.

**Compound business logic.** Kokonas built the Alinea Group (Alinea, Next, The Aviary, Roister — now sold in 2024) [[youtube.com - Grit Podcast](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KSZ5kPEcwXg)] [SECONDARY] and Tock simultaneously, using each to reinforce the other: Alinea was the proof-of-concept customer and live lab for Tock's pre-payment reservation model, and Tock's $430M acquisition by Squarespace in 2021 validated the thesis. [[youtube.com/TWIST](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-f_467baE1k)] [SECONDARY] In the 2018 Ferriss interview, Kokonas describes the logic: *"I always spend time looking for the high, small hoops. Then I spend my time learning as much as I can, and then I jump through that hoop."* [[podcastnotes.org](https://podcastnotes.org/tim-ferris-show/kokonas/)] [SECONDARY, quoting 2018 interview] This is a derivatives-trader's frame for capital allocation: find asymmetric structures where the downside is bounded and the upside is large.

**Owning the customer relationship.** Tock's core innovation — pre-paid reservations, dynamic pricing, direct CRM — was explicitly about eliminating the intermediary (OpenTable) and making the restaurant the center of the relationship. In the 2020 Ferriss conversation, Kokonas describes the clearing-firm analogy: Tock acts as the counterparty in every reservation transaction, holds the float, and owns the customer data. [[tim.blog](https://tim.blog/2020/05/17/nick-kokonas-2-transcript/)] [PRIMARY] This is the restaurant version of Berkshire's insurance float: money that doesn't belong to you but that you can deploy.

**Decision-making vs. outcomes.** The recurring Kokonas framework across all interviews is: *"The overarching theme was about decision making, not outcomes. I try to measure myself and my business life and personal life on making the decisions, not whether or not they sort of paid off or not."* [[tim.blog](https://tim.blog/2020/05/17/nick-kokonas-2-transcript/)] [PRIMARY] This maps to the Munger/Buffett concept of process over results, which is the hidden infrastructure of any long-duration holdco.

**Source limitation.** Kokonas's most relevant post-2022 thinking is not well-documented in long-form. He exited Alinea as of October 2024 and Tock was sold to Squarespace in 2021, then to American Express. His Twitter/X writings exist but are not consolidated into a primary-source document comparable to Buffett's letters or Leonard's President's Letters. This sub-section reflects a genuine limitation: Kokonas is more useful as an *illustration* of holdco-adjacent thinking than as a doctrine-source.

### 5. Micro-Holdco Playbooks

**Sources:** Permanent Equity website [[permanentequity.com](https://permanentequity.com)] [PRIMARY]; Brent Beshore operating manual synthesis [[colinkeeley.com/blog/brent-beshore-permanent-equity](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/brent-beshore-permanent-equity)] [SECONDARY]; SureSwift Capital model overview [[sureswiftcapital.com](https://www.sureswiftcapital.com/blog/the-sureswift-model)] [PRIMARY]; Enduring Ventures about page [[enduring.ventures/about](https://enduring.ventures/about)] [PRIMARY]; Xavier Helgesen LinkedIn [[linkedin.com](https://www.linkedin.com/in/xavierhelgesen)] [SECONDARY]

The micro-holdco wave — operators building Berkshire-style structures in the sub-$100M range — has produced a distinct set of practitioners whose operating principles are often better documented in blog posts and podcasts than the original templates, because they are explaining the doctrine to an audience of first-generation founders.

**Permanent Equity (Brent Beshore).** Permanent Equity uses 30-year funds to invest in lower-middle-market businesses ($2M–$25M in annual free cash flow). The core structural innovation is fund duration: *"Most other private equity funds regularly acknowledge that the biggest drag on returns is not from making bad investments, but from selling good companies prematurely."* [[colinkeeley.com](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/brent-beshore-permanent-equity)] [SECONDARY] Beshore's terms: no management fee outside of carry; 15% minimum GP co-investment; complete decision-making control; zero external debt on portfolio businesses ("small businesses are too volatile... more durable long-term without debt"). [[permanentequity.com](https://permanentequity.com)] [PRIMARY] The Messy Marketplace (2020) — Beshore's book on the lower-middle market — documents the practical reality of buying "adolescent businesses: too big to be small, too small to be big," including the owner-dependency problem (businesses where all value is "tied up in the goodwill of the owner"). The preferred valuation methodology is a multiple of true earnings (not EBITDA, not adjusted anything), typically 4–6x. Deal flow is entirely inbound, driven by content (book, letters, blog, podcast, Capital Camp conference). [[colinkeeley.com](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/brent-beshore-permanent-equity)] [SECONDARY]

**SureSwift Capital.** Founded in 2015, SureSwift pioneered the B2B SaaS micro-acquisition model — buying bootstrapped SaaS from founders at the $500K–$3M ARR level, operating through what they call the "Exceptional Operator Framework," and distributing monthly cash returns to investors. SureSwift's investment thesis statement: *"We're focused on a buy-and-hold model — we don't buy companies with the intention of flipping them. We want to find well-run, enduring businesses with an unfair competitive advantage, buy them for a fair price, and grow and hold them for the long-term."* [[sureswiftcapital.com](https://www.sureswiftcapital.com/blog/saas-acquisition-deal-structures)] [PRIMARY] By 2025, SureSwift is evolving toward 6–10 larger "platform companies" per fund with direct sponsorship (deal-by-deal) rather than pooled fund structures. [[sureswiftcapital.com/blog/sureswift-new-investment-thesis](https://www.sureswiftcapital.com/blog/sureswift-new-investment-thesis)] [PRIMARY]

**Enduring Ventures (Xavier Helgesen & Sieva Kozinsky).** Founded in 2019 in the explicit Berkshire model: *"We buy beautiful businesses and hold them forever. We are a holding company in the model of Berkshire Hathaway, working to build a more sustainable economy where wealth and economic growth is broadly shared."* [[enduring.ventures/about](https://enduring.ventures/about)] [PRIMARY] Portfolio of 25+ businesses across software, home services, hospitality; agnostic on industry; mostly control (51%–100%); closes in under 30 days; founders can stay or leave. The critical philosophy statement: *"Having invested in companies, sold companies and raised funding, we have seen the costs of the short-term mindset of private equity and venture capital up close. We wanted to do something fundamentally different. Following the inspiration of Berkshire Hathaway, we decided to build the company that we would want to buy our own company."* [[enduring.ventures/about](https://enduring.ventures/about)] [PRIMARY]

**Common principles across all three.** Synthesizing: (1) permanent or very long hold (30-year funds, "forever" language); (2) cashflow over EBITDA — distributable cash is the only real metric; (3) founder replacement is the hardest problem and the most critical lever; (4) no PE extraction tactics — no dividend recaps, no forced layoffs, no covenant-heavy debt; (5) deal flow from reputation and content, not outbound sourcing; (6) decentralized operations with centralized capital; (7) hurdle rates or cash-yield thresholds as non-negotiable filters.

### Berkshire Letter Extraction 2020–2024: Deep-Dive

*What survived, what changed, and what Buffett said 2020–2024 that he hadn't said before.*

**What survived unchanged.** The decentralization doctrine — "hire well, manage little" — appears verbatim in letters across five decades and was not modified by the pandemic. The 2020 letter repeats the logic in different words: *"Berkshire's job is simply not to meddle with the company's success."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2020ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2020ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] The preference for operating earnings over GAAP net income as the honest signal survived unchanged. The America-optimism frame ("bet against America and you lose") survived. The anti-management-fee, anti-leverage, pro-patience capital allocation framework survived.

**What changed: the size problem becomes explicit.** The 2023 letter is the first time Buffett fully acknowledges that size has become a binding constraint — not as a theoretical problem but as a stated reality: *"Those days are long behind us; size did us in, though increased competition for purchases was also a factor."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] The 2024 letter reinforces this: *"Berkshire's present size… diminishes this valuable option [changing course on marketable equities]. We can't come and go on a dime. Sometimes a year or more is required to establish or divest an investment."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] At $561 billion GAAP net worth (2023 year-end), there are essentially no acquisition targets large enough to move the needle. This is the central irony of the Berkshire model for founders: the doctrine works best before scale makes it work less.

**What changed: error acknowledgment.** The 2024 letter contains this striking admission: *"During the 2019–23 period, I have used the words 'mistake' or 'error' 16 times in my letters to you. Many other huge companies have never used either word over that span."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] This is not a new behavior — Buffett has always publicly flagged errors — but the 2024 framing is more systematic: he is teaching the error-acknowledgment culture explicitly.

**What changed: succession.** The 2024 letter names Greg Abel as CEO-designate: *"At 94, it won't be long before Greg Abel replaces me as CEO."* This is the first explicit succession statement in the public letters. The question of whether Abel preserves the trust-over-process culture at Berkshire scale is the single most important unresolved question in holdco doctrine as of 2026.

**What changed: operating earnings composition.** The pandemic and its aftermath reshuffled Berkshire's earnings mix significantly. GEICO, which had been a consistent earner, required a five-year transformation managed by Todd Combs. BNSF had flat or declining earnings from 2022 to 2023. Berkshire's Japanese holdings (Itochu, Marubeni, Mitsubishi, Mitsui, Sumitomo) emerged as a new and significant position — cost of ¥1.6 trillion, market value ¥2.9 trillion by 2023. [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] The Japanese trading house investments are structurally Berkshire-similar: diversified, cash-generating, permanently held.

**What changed: the Munger tribute (2023).** Charlie Munger died in November 2023, and the 2023 letter opens with his eulogy, including the single most important structural insight: *"Charlie was the 'architect' of the present Berkshire, and I acted as the 'general contractor' to carry out the day-by-day construction of his vision."* [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] Munger's role — pushing Buffett away from Benjamin Graham's "cigar butt" value investing toward quality-at-fair-price — is now fully documented as the ideological pivot point of Berkshire's modern success.

**Summary of 2020–2024 evolution.** The doctrine survived. The acknowledgment of its limits sharpened. The candor about errors increased. Succession became explicit. The composition of the business shifted (more Japanese, more Occidental, flatter BNSF). For a founder building a micro-holdco, the most important 2020–2024 lesson is: **the doctrine works best at human scale.** A $561B holdco cannot move; a €1M/year holdco of 10–50 operators can.

### Common Synthesis — The 7 Principles of the Holdco Operator

Derived from Berkshire (Buffett/Munger), Constellation (Leonard), Tiny (Wilkinson), Permanent Equity (Beshore), Enduring Ventures (Helgesen), and the micro-holdco wave. Each principle is tagged to its primary source.

**Principle 1: Extreme decentralization of operations, extreme centralization of capital.**
Operations are delegated to the general manager or CEO of each business unit. Capital allocation — which businesses to buy, what to pay, when to acquire vs. hold vs. (rarely) sell — is centralized at the holdco level and never delegated. Source: Munger at Berkshire [[25iq.com](https://25iq.com/2015/09/26/a-dozen-things-ive-learned-from-charlie-munger-about-the-berkshire-system/)] [SECONDARY]; Leonard at CSI [[biographynuts.com](https://www.biographynuts.com/p/chapter-154-constellation-software)] [SECONDARY]; Wilkinson at Tiny [[q4cdn.com](http://s202.q4cdn.com/676416790/files/doc_presentation/2023/08/TINY-Presentation-28-08-23.pdf)] [PRIMARY]

**Principle 2: Permanent capital and the never-sell doctrine.**
The holdco commits to holding businesses indefinitely. This is not sentiment — it is deal-sourcing infrastructure. Sellers who care about their teams select permanent owners at below-maximum price. Source: Leonard (CSI objective as "perpetual owner") [[biographynuts.com](https://www.biographynuts.com/p/chapter-154-constellation-software)] [SECONDARY]; Beshore (30-year funds, never sold a portfolio company) [[permanentequity.com](https://permanentequity.com)] [PRIMARY]; Helgesen (explicit "hold forever" language) [[enduring.ventures](https://enduring.ventures/about)] [PRIMARY]

**Principle 3: Operator quality is the binding constraint.**
All other principles are contingent on selecting the right operators. The holdco cannot create operator quality; it can only find it, price it correctly, and incentivize it. Source: Buffett ("hire well, manage little"; "run by able and honest managers") [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2010ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2010ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY]; Wilkinson ("finding great people to run these companies is one of the hardest aspects of this model") [[colinkeeley.com](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/andrew-wilkinson-tiny-capital-operating-manual)] [SECONDARY]

**Principle 4: Cashflow discipline — distributable cash is the only honest metric.**
EBITDA as typically reported is not the measurement unit. Distributable free cash flow (after maintenance capex, after working capital requirements, after owner compensation) is the only signal that matters for capital allocation decisions. Source: Buffett (operating earnings vs. GAAP net income emphasis, 2022–2024 letters) [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2022ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2022ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY]; Beshore (multiple of true earnings, not EBITDA) [[colinkeeley.com](https://www.colinkeeley.com/blog/brent-beshore-permanent-equity)] [SECONDARY]; Wilkinson (Munger: EBITDA is "bullshit earnings", 2023 annual letter) [ANECDOTE]

**Principle 5: Hurdle rates as non-negotiable culture.**
A minimum return threshold that is maintained even when it means walking away from deals is not a financial tool — it is a cultural one. It prevents good-money-after-bad, aligns the entire organization around value creation, and resists the institutional pressure to deploy capital for its own sake. Source: Leonard (25% IRR as cultural line; 2017 letter on almost doing the deal at 24.8%) [[acquirersmultiple.com](https://acquirersmultiple.com/2024/01/mark-leonard-raise-the-bar-how-hurdle-rates-drive-profitable-investments/)] [SECONDARY]; Beshore (15% gross cash-on-cash as minimum; structure to never be a forced actor) [[permanentequity.com](https://permanentequity.com)] [PRIMARY]

**Principle 6: Culture over compliance — trust as operating system.**
Rules, compliance systems, and monitoring are expensive substitutes for trust. The holdco that succeeds long-term selects operators it trusts completely and gives them maximum autonomy. Source: Buffett ("our trust is in people rather than process" — 2010 letter) [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2010ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2010ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY]; Munger ("we operate Berkshire via a seamless web of deserved trust") [[25iq.com](https://25iq.com/2015/09/26/a-dozen-things-ive-learned-from-charlie-munger-about-the-berkshire-system/)] [SECONDARY]; Leonard (autonomy motivates, bureaucracy does the opposite) [[biographynuts.com](https://www.biographynuts.com/p/chapter-154-constellation-software)] [SECONDARY]

**Principle 7: Compounding requires staying in the game.**
The holdco's structural advantage is time. Most of the value in any quality business is 10–20 years out. Every premature sale, every forced exit, every governance crisis that disrupts the operator resets the compounding clock. Source: Senra distilling Thiel: "Don't over-optimize for growth at the expense of durability" [[podcastnotes.org](https://podcastnotes.org/modern-wisdom/15-harsh-truths-from-historys-greatest-founders-david-senra-modern-wisdom-with-chris-williamson/)] [SECONDARY]; Buffett (2023 letter): "When you find a truly wonderful business, stick with it. Patience pays" [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2023ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY]

### Strongest Critique

**The PE extractive critique.** The standard private-equity argument against the holdco model is that permanent ownership destroys the incentive for operators to maximize value: without a sale event, there is no liquidity and no shareholder alignment mechanism. Traditional PE's retort to Berkshire is that capital is better allocated by specialists — sector-focused funds with deep operational teams — than by generalists like Buffett. The evidence does not support this critique at scale (Berkshire's 60-year compounding record dwarfs the PE industry average), but it is partially valid at the subsidiary level: without a defined exit horizon, some Berkshire or Constellation operators have arguably been allowed to run suboptimal businesses longer than a PE sponsor would.

**Matt Levine and the Berkshire size problem.** Matt Levine (Bloomberg Money Stuff) has covered the Berkshire size problem as the central structural issue for the 2020s: the same model that produced extraordinary results when deploying $10M or $100M at a time fails to work at the $10B+ deployment scale Berkshire now faces. [[newsletterhunt.com](https://newsletterhunt.com/emails/67140)] [SECONDARY] The underlying logic is simple: at $561B in net worth, Berkshire must deploy billions to move the needle, and there are very few businesses that meet Buffett's quality threshold at that scale. The 2024 letter's candor about this ($132B in cash and equivalents as of year-end, essentially undeployed) confirms the critique. [[berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf](https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2024ltr.pdf)] [PRIMARY] Buffett's own admission: "size did us in."

**The Tiny model critique.** A pointed critique of Tiny comes from a 2024 analyst short thesis: Tiny's 2023 reported EBITDA of $46.4M was primarily a one-time non-cash accounting item, both Wilkinson and Sparling stepped down as Co-CEOs in June 2024 (the year adjusted EBITDA was set to step down sharply), the stock declined approximately 90% from its 2023 public listing, and related-party transactions raise governance questions. [[thevalueroad.substack.com](https://thevalueroad.substack.com/p/tiny-ltd-a-collection-of-lousy-internet)] [SECONDARY] The structural critique is valid for any founder considering the Tiny model: the transition from private-holdco to public company fundamentally changes the operating environment. Wilkinson himself acknowledged in *Never Enough* that the Anti-Goals framework led him to question whether becoming a public-company CEO would eliminate the freedom the holdco model was built to create. The lesson: the holdco doctrine and the public-company doctrine are not the same thing. Going public adds governance costs, reporting burdens, and investor-relation demands that are in direct tension with the decentralization principle.

**The institutional imperative (Buffett's own critique).** Buffett identified what he calls the "Institutional Imperative" — the tendency of large organizations to do things because other large organizations are doing them, regardless of merit — as the primary failure mode of corporate governance. In his framing, most CEOs of large companies are not stupid; they are captured by the incentive structures of their institutions. The holdco model tries to escape this by keeping headquarters thin (Berkshire: 25 employees; Tiny: small head office; CSI: minimal overlay). But the institutional imperative reasserts itself as the holdco itself grows — every layer of management added to the holdco creates new incentive misalignments.

### AI-Native Adaptation

*How do the holdco principles change when operators are AI-augmented and when coordination is programmatic? This section is explicitly speculative.*

**What is structurally the same.** The 7 principles above do not change because operators use AI. Decentralized operations + centralized capital allocation remains the correct structure. Permanent hold remains the optimal strategy for compounding. Operator quality remains the binding constraint. Cashflow discipline remains the only honest measurement. The holdco model is about **structural design**, and structural design is not disrupted by the tools operators use.

**What changes: the margin profile.** The most significant AI-native adaptation is the margin structure of individual operators. A solo AI-augmented specialist can deliver work that previously required a 5–10 person team. This compresses the "minimum viable operator" size and changes the acquisition economics: where Tiny's minimum was $500K/year in profit (implying a significant human team), an AI-native holdco can potentially compound from operators at the $100K–$200K net profit level — lower absolute size, potentially similar relative returns. For Jetix specifically: each matchmaker-partner in the network is effectively a micro-operator with AI-augmented capacity. The holdco's role is to route capital (deal flow, pricing power, tooling investment) to the operators who compound it best.

**What changes: coordination economics.** Berkshire coordinates via two-year letters and an annual meeting. Leonard coordinated via President's Letters and shared metrics. Wilkinson coordinates via board approval thresholds and annual reports. In an AI-native holdco, coordination can be programmatic: dashboards, automated reporting, LLM-assisted performance reviews. This reduces the cost of maintaining the "thin overlay of infrastructure" that all holdco practitioners describe. But the efficiency gain is only valuable if it is not used to add back bureaucracy — the risk is that low-cost coordination tools enable micromanagement at scale, which destroys exactly the autonomy that makes the model work.

**What the named thinkers say — with honesty about limitations.** No named thinker (Elad Gil, Chamath Palihapitiya, Zuckerberg, Jensen Huang, Satya Nadella, David Sacks) has published a systematic doctrine for AI-native holdcos as of April 2026. What exists is:

- **Elad Gil** has the most specific thesis: own the services asset, not just the software vendor, because ownership allows faster transformation and higher margins. *"If you own the asset, you can transform it much more rapidly than if you're just selling software as a vendor."* [[techcrunch.com](https://techcrunch.com/2025/06/01/early-ai-investor-elad-gil-finds-his-next-big-bet-ai-powered-rollups/)] [SECONDARY] His challenge is team composition — PE + AI engineering is a rare combination. For a solo founder coordinating AI-augmented specialists via matchmaking (Jetix's model), the team composition problem is different: you need specialists who already know their domain and can adopt AI tools, not PE operators.

- **General Catalyst's Marc Bhargava** articulates the margin re-rating thesis most precisely: GC mapped 70 service categories, identified 10 where AI automates 30–70% of workflows, built AI platforms, then acquired distribution via services firms. The moat is *distribution and proprietary data*, not the AI itself. [[generalcatalyst.com](https://www.generalcatalyst.com/stories/europes-ai-transformation-in-services)] [PRIMARY] For Jetix: the matchmaker-partnership network is the distribution asset. The operators carry the proprietary domain knowledge. The AI tooling is table stakes.

- **Jensen Huang** (Nvidia) has argued that AI agents will function as "digital team members" — but his framing is about product-level automation, not structural holdco design. Not directly applicable.

- **David Sacks** (formerly a16z, now US AI/Crypto Czar) has written about AI compressing the cost of software development, enabling smaller teams to produce more output. This is structurally bullish for AI-native holdcos but not a doctrine for running one.

**The honest uncertainty.** The AI-enabled rollup wave is real, but the specific claim that AI can systematically rerate services margins from 10% to 40% has not been demonstrated at scale and may not survive the transition from early deployment to competitive equilibrium. The holdco doctrine has worked for 60+ years across multiple technological transitions (mainframe to PC, PC to internet, internet to mobile). It will likely survive the AI transition as well — but the operators who compound within it will use AI as a productivity tool, not as the structural advantage. The structural advantage remains what it has always been: permanent capital, operator quality, and the patience to let compounding work.

**For Jetix specifically.** At €1M+/year coordinating 10–50 operators, the AI-native adaptation of the holdco doctrine looks like this: (1) use AI tooling to reduce coordination overhead at the holdco level (programmatic reporting, automated client matching, LLM-assisted scoping); (2) use AI augmentation at the operator level to increase each operator's effective capacity without adding headcount; (3) apply the same hurdle rate discipline to new operators/engagements as Leonard applied to acquisitions — not every partnership compounds equally; (4) resist the temptation to add coordination layers just because coordination tooling is now cheap; (5) build the deal-sourcing moat through reputation and content (as Permanent Equity did), not outbound sourcing. The structural lesson from every holdco practitioner reviewed here is consistent: the model fails not from lack of technology, but from failure to maintain the operational discipline that the technology makes easier to circumvent.

# Part 4 — Cross-Canon Observations Appendix

Each of the four research tracks produced cross-section observations at the end of its dossier. They are preserved below as a closing commentary layer.

## Classical Canon Cross-Observations (A / E / F / G)


Across these four thinkers — separated by decades and disciplines — five structural patterns emerge that are relevant to the Jetix operating context:

**1. Output over activity, always.** Grove (managerial leverage equation), Drucker (effectiveness vs. efficiency), Watkins (break-even point), and Horowitz (decision debt, communication architecture) all converge on the same anti-intuition: the quality of what you direct matters far more than the volume of what you do. For a solo founder with AI agents amplifying productive capacity by an order of magnitude, this principle becomes even more decisive — attention is now the binding constraint, and attention poorly directed is multiplied waste.

**2. Situational calibration is prior to technique.** Grove's TRM requires diagnosing the task and the operator before choosing management style. Watkins's STARS requires diagnosing the business situation before choosing a transition strategy. Drucker's "where do I belong?" requires diagnosing the contribution mode before choosing a role. Horowitz's wartime/peacetime distinction requires diagnosing the existential stakes before choosing a management posture. The holding-company operator who applies techniques without situational diagnosis will fail systematically.

**3. Culture is reproduced through behaviour, not declaration.** Horowitz states this most explicitly, but it is implicit in Grove (clean desks, punctuality, constructive confrontation as daily rituals), Drucker (feedback analysis as a *practice*, not a belief), and Watkins (early wins as cultural signals, not just operational results). For a distributed matchmaker network, behavioural culture must be encoded in contract structures, milestone rhythms, and the visible conduct of the founder — who functions as a role model even to contractors who never meet them.

**4. The canonical gap: all four frameworks assume authority.** Grove's TRM assumes direct subordinates. Drucker's knowledge-worker productivity assumes an organisational mission to align with. Watkins's STARS assumes a boss to negotiate with. Horowitz's culture doctrine assumes an employee base to model for. The Jetix matchmaker model — a network of independent operators connected by equity or fee structures, without employment relationships — sits partially outside all four frameworks. The practitioner synthesis required: replace *authority-based* levers (direct instruction, performance review, cultural osmosis) with *incentive-based* and *information-architecture* levers (OKRs, milestone structure, transparent output metrics).

**5. All four show consistent 2024–2026 practitioner vitality.** Grove is cited in active operator discourse as of December 2025; Drucker's Forum is running AI-focused panels in Vienna in November 2024; Watkins published a new book in January 2024 and is explicitly engaging agentic AI as of April 2025; Horowitz spoke at Sequoia in February 2026. The "foundational, verify 2026 relevance" caveat is warranted for specific implementation details (Grove's meeting formats, Drucker's MBO process design, Watkins's 90-day corporate onboarding timeline) but not for the core operating principles, which show no signs of supersession by AI-native frameworks — possibly because no rigorous AI-native management canon yet exists.

## Modern Company Canon Cross-Observations (B / C / D)


Three patterns emerge from placing 37signals, Netflix, and Stripe in the same frame:

**1. Small is not modest; small is strategic.** All three canons reject bigness as a goal. Fried's *"thin business, thick products"* and Netflix's *"talent density over headcount"* and Stripe's *"we aspire to become the world's fastest company"* (not the largest) converge on the same insight: organizational mass creates entropy faster than it creates capability. The AI-native implication is radical: with agents doing compounding work, the optimal headcount for a given revenue level is dropping every quarter, and the companies that built philosophies around staying small are structurally vindicated.

**2. Writing is the universal coordination primitive.** Stripe's footnoted emails, 37signals' async-by-default manifesto, and Netflix's farming-for-dissent via the culture memo (1,500 employee comments in the 2024 revision) all converge on written documents — not meetings, not decks — as the scalable medium for culture and decision-making. This is not coincidence; it is a discovered truth about how distributed organizations preserve institutional knowledge. For a zero-FTE holding network, it is the only viable coordination mechanism.

**3. Culture breaks first at the founder layer.** All three canons have their most visible failure at the top. 37signals' 2021 implosion originated in the founders' unwillingness to be held accountable to their own stated values. Netflix's keeper test, when applied to Netflix's own leadership, generates uncomfortable questions about how Hastings himself handled the 2022 slowdown. Stripe's explicit articulation of the entropy/hubris risk (Collison at Stripe Sessions 2025) is the most self-aware version of this pattern — but self-awareness does not inoculate against it. For founders building matchmaker-partner networks: the governing philosophy you publish is the standard against which your partners will evaluate your decisions. Write it carefully, implement it visibly, and expect to be held to it.

## Org Design & Solopreneur Cross-Observations (H / I)


The tension between Laloux's org-design formalism and the solopreneur canon's organizational-rejection is productive rather than contradictory, but only when read at the right level of abstraction.

Laloux operates at the level of the *organization as living system*: he assumes a group of people who need coordination, and asks how authority and purpose can be distributed without becoming bureaucracy. His answer requires *at minimum* a stable group willing to negotiate CLOUs, share financial data, and engage in peer conflict resolution. This is a social infrastructure project.

The solopreneur canon begins by refusing the organization as the unit of analysis. For Naval, Sivers, Welsh, and Levels, the fundamental unit is the *leveraged individual* — one person plus tools. Their answer to coordination cost is elimination: don't coordinate, build systems that don't require coordination.

The synthesis available to Jetix is: treat the *partner network* as the teal organization and the *solo operator nodes* as solopreneurs. Each node (including the founder) runs on solopreneur logic — permissionless leverage, judgment-first, no subordination. The network layer runs on teal logic — purpose-held by the founder, peer agreements replacing hierarchy, evolutionary direction emerging from where value accretes.

This is precisely the Buurtzorg architecture: 10–12-person autonomous teams (solopreneur nodes), supported by 50 central staff (the founder + AI stack), held together by shared purpose rather than management authority. The Berkshire/Constellation model Jetix aspires to is the same pattern at holding-company scale.
