## §1 Wiggins + McTighe — *Understanding by Design* (UbD) (2005)

(Synthesized via Tomlinson+McTighe 2006 which integrates UbD canonical content + canonical UbD literature.)

### §1.1 Core thesis — backward design

Design **starts with the desired end-state**, then works backward — not from content-to-cover (forward design / coverage trap). Three-stage process [src: tomlinson-mctighe-integrating-differentiated-instruction-2006.pdf p 1339-1426]:

| Stage | Question | Output |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Desired results** | What should students know, understand, do? Which big ideas + enduring understandings? Which essential questions? | Outcomes spec (Box K = knowledge, Box S = skills, Box U = understandings, Box Q = essential questions) |
| **2 — Acceptable evidence** | How will we know learners reached Stage 1 outcomes? What assessments + performance tasks? | Assessment plan (Box P = performance tasks, Box O = other evidence) |
| **3 — Learning experiences + instruction** | What activities + sequencing enables Stage 2 evidence + Stage 1 outcomes? | Activity plan (Box L = learning) |

[src: tomlinson-mctighe-integrating-differentiated-instruction-2006.pdf p 1322-1465]

### §1.2 The «twin sins» UbD critiques

1. **Activity-focused teaching** — fun activities with no clear outcome link («engaging» but not consequential)
2. **Coverage-focused teaching** — race through textbook chapters; no time for understanding

→ **Both are forward-design failures.** Backward design prevents them by forcing outcome-first.

### §1.3 Big ideas + essential questions

- **Big idea / enduring understanding** = a conceptual claim that organises many facts, transfers to novel contexts, and merits sustained inquiry over a unit
- **Essential question** = open-ended, conceptual, recurring; provokes inquiry not closure («What makes a story worth telling?» not «Who is the main character?»)
- Essential questions are **the visible surface** of enduring understandings in classroom dialogue

[src: tomlinson-mctighe-integrating-differentiated-instruction-2006.pdf p 213, 678, 1322-1327]

### §1.4 6 facets of understanding (Wiggins-McTighe)

| # | Facet | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Explain** | Provide thorough + apt theories + examples |
| 2 | **Interpret** | Tell meaningful stories; translate, offer apt analogies |
| 3 | **Apply** | Use knowledge effectively in new + diverse contexts |
| 4 | **Have perspective** | See multiple critical points of view; aware of bias |
| 5 | **Empathize** | Sense + value other-perspectives |
| 6 | **Have self-knowledge** | Aware of one's own ignorance / habits-of-mind / blind spots |

→ **Anchor for Bloom level 4-6 + Hattie mindframe 9-10.**

### §1.5 Implications for Jetix

- **Method V2 redesign per UbD discipline:** declare big ideas / enduring understandings / essential questions for §A-§Q; design assessments before content; sequence backwards
- Welcome-frame O-144 should be re-framed as **essential question** form («What method should I use to develop myself?») not «here is content»
- Strategic Plan rollouts (Phase 7 Jetix Lens §2.B) = backward-design per learner level

## §2 Tomlinson + McTighe — *Integrating Differentiated Instruction & Understanding by Design* (2006)

### §2.1 Core proposal

UbD = **what** to teach / assess. Differentiated Instruction (DI) = **how** to deliver to diverse learners. Both are necessary; either alone is insufficient [src: tomlinson-mctighe-integrating-differentiated-instruction-2006.pdf p 143-152].

### §2.2 4 elements DI can differentiate

| Element | What it varies |
|---|---|
| **Content** | What students learn (depth or breadth, not core outcomes) |
| **Process** | How students engage with content (groupings, modes) |
| **Product** | How students demonstrate learning (essay, model, performance, code) |
| **Affect/Learning environment** | Pace, tone, sensory aspects, support level |

### §2.3 3 learner factors DI responds to

| Factor | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Readiness** | Current proficiency / ZPD location |
| **Interest** | What learner cares about |
| **Learning profile** | Preferences (group/solo, mode, time-of-day) — but with Willingham caveat (don't over-engineer) |

### §2.4 «Equity ≠ identical» principle

> «To treat all students alike — to give them identical assignments, identical time, identical materials — is to deny equity. Equity is meeting each learner at the appropriate readiness + interest with appropriate product expectations.»
> [synthesised from book pp 483-522]

### §2.5 Critique of «teaching to the middle»

- Lecture aimed at the middle bores top + frustrates bottom
- DI/UbD addresses via tiered task design + flexible grouping + multiple entry points (one big idea, many access paths)

### §2.6 Implications for Jetix

- **Direct anchor for Phase 6 multi-variant programs** — 7 variants is a DI catalog (different readiness + time-budget + audience), but **all converge on the same enduring understandings** of Method V2 / Hypothesis Architecture / R12 discipline
- Workshop format = DI in action (peer scaffolding adjusts per readiness)
- Cohort + curated community O-145/161 = DI infrastructure

## §3 Dirksen — *Design for How People Learn* (2nd ed 2015)

### §3.1 Core framework — the «gap» model

Instructional design = **gap identification + gap bridging** [src: dirksen-design-for-how-people-learn-2015.pdf p 272-289].

| Gap type | Question | Solution category |
|---|---|---|
| **Knowledge gap** | They don't know X | Information (text, video, reference) |
| **Skills gap** | They can't do X | Practice + feedback |
| **Motivation gap** | They don't want to do X | Re-frame value; remove barriers; design for affect |
| **Habit gap** | They sometimes do X, but it's not automatic | Cue-loop design (habit formation) |
| **Environment gap** | The context prevents doing X | Tools / infrastructure / process change |
| **Communication gap** | They don't know they need to do X | Awareness / messaging |

### §3.2 Why this matters

Many «training» problems are misdiagnosed as **knowledge gaps** (which can be solved with one PDF or video) when they're actually **motivation / habit / environment** gaps (which require much different interventions). Spending knowledge-gap effort on a motivation-gap problem = waste.

→ **Jetix application:** People «know» that compound interest matters / system thinking helps / dialogue beats banking — they DON'T do it because of motivation / habit / environment gaps. Method V2 must address these explicitly, not just deliver more information.

### §3.3 Dirksen on motivation

Cross-cite Self-Determination Theory (Ryan + Deci 1985) — 3 intrinsic motivators:
- **Autonomy** — sense of choice / agency
- **Competence** — sense of growing capability
- **Relatedness** — sense of meaningful connection to others

→ All 3 anchor Welcome-frame O-144 + cohort O-161 + Method V2 progression.

### §3.4 «Working memory bouncer» metaphor

Dirksen popularised the working-memory-as-bouncer metaphor: limited bouncer at the door of long-term memory; only some things get in. Implications:
- Cognitive load minimisation (anchor Sweller §7 Phase 2)
- Chunking + scaffolding so bouncer admits items in manageable groups
- Reduce extraneous load (decoration, distraction)

### §3.5 Implications for Jetix

- **Welcome-frame O-144** must address motivation gap, not just knowledge gap
- **Hook Model canvas** wiki = habit-gap intervention (Eyal Hook + Dirksen habit chapter)
- **Notion templates O-158** = environment-gap intervention (lower friction for desired behavior)
- Method V2 §A welcome explicit framing = autonomy + competence + relatedness anchors

## §4 Cross-reference — Merrill First Principles of Instruction (2002)

### §4.1 5 first principles

After surveying major instructional design theories (Gagné, Reigeluth, Schank, Van Merriënboer, etc.), David Merrill (2002) extracted **5 invariants** present in all effective instruction:

| # | Principle | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Problem-centered** | Learning is facilitated when learners engage with **real-world problems** (whole task, not isolated skills) |
| 2 | **Activation** | Learning is facilitated when existing knowledge is **activated** as foundation for new |
| 3 | **Demonstration** | Learning is facilitated when new knowledge is **demonstrated** (modeled, shown) |
| 4 | **Application** | Learning is facilitated when learners **apply** new knowledge |
| 5 | **Integration** | Learning is facilitated when new knowledge is **integrated** into learner's world (transferred, generalised, owned) |

### §4.2 The pebble-in-the-pond pattern

Merrill's task-centered design pattern:
1. Specify a real-world problem (the «pebble»)
2. Specify progression of simpler-to-complex problem-instances (ripples)
3. Identify required component skills for the simplest problem
4. Demonstrate + apply each component within the problem context
5. Re-integrate as learner progresses to next-complexity ripple

→ **Direct anchor for Phase 6 multi-variant program design** — anchor each program in a real Jetix-relevant problem; sequence in ripples.

### §4.3 Implications for Jetix

- Method V2 should be re-framed: every §-section anchored to a real learner problem (not abstract concept transmission)
- Workshop format inherently problem-centered (cohort working on actual offers / clan formations / market-tests)

## §6 Synthesis — instructional design framework

### §6.1 Unified ID model for Jetix

Combining Bloom + Gagné + Tyler + UbD + DI + Dirksen + Merrill:

```
Step 1 — DESIRED RESULT
   (Bloom 6 levels: which cognitive level is target?)
   (UbD Stage 1: big ideas, enduring understandings, essential questions)
   (Tyler Q1: educational purposes)

Step 2 — EVIDENCE OF ATTAINMENT
   (UbD Stage 2: performance tasks + other evidence)
   (Tyler Q4: how do we know?)
   (Hattie mindframes 1-2: teacher-as-evaluator-of-impact)

Step 3 — GAP DIAGNOSIS
   (Dirksen: knowledge / skill / motivation / habit / environment / communication)
   (Gagné: which of 5 capability types?)

Step 4 — INSTRUCTIONAL DESIGN
   (Merrill 5: problem-centered, activation, demo, app, integration)
   (Gagné 9 events: end-to-end checklist)
   (UbD Stage 3: learning activities + sequencing)

Step 5 — DIFFERENTIATION
   (Tomlinson DI: content / process / product / environment differentiated by readiness / interest / profile)
   (Bruner spiral: same big idea at varied levels of abstraction)

Step 6 — EXECUTION + FEEDBACK
   (Hattie mindframes 6-7: bidirectional feedback)
   (Make It Stick: spacing, retrieval, interleaving, calibration)
   (Vygotsky ZPD: in-zone work with scaffolding)
   (Sweller: cognitive load managed)

Step 7 — REFLECTION + ITERATION
   (Hattie mindframe 1 + 3: evaluator + peer-collaboration)
   (Dewey: continuity of experience → new outcomes feed next cycle)
```

### §6.2 Failure-mode → corrective-step map

| Failure | Step that should have caught it |
|---|---|
| «Engaging activity» with no outcome | Step 1 |
| Race through content; no understanding | Step 1 + Step 2 (assessment-first) |
| Wasted training (info-dump on motivation problem) | Step 3 (Dirksen gap) |
| One-size-fits-all bores some + frustrates others | Step 5 (DI) |
| No transfer | Step 4 (Merrill integration) + Step 6 (interleaving) |
| Forgetting curve | Step 6 (spacing) |
| Plateau | Step 6 (deliberate practice conditions) |
