## §1 Willingham — *Why Don't Students Like School?* (2010)

### §1.1 Origin

Daniel Willingham — cognitive psychologist (UVA); column «Ask the Cognitive Scientist» for American Educator. Book = 9 cognitive-psych principles for K-12 + higher-ed teachers. Modern bridge between cognitive science lab + classroom practice.

### §1.2 Nine principles (chapter-by-chapter)

| # | Principle | Source p | Translation for adult / professional learning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **People are naturally curious but not naturally good thinkers** | p 16+ | Thinking is hard work; brain prefers memory retrieval over reasoning. Solution: design problems learner CAN solve (success-rate ≈ 60-70%) |
| 2 | **Factual knowledge precedes skill** | p 42-43 | «Background knowledge is necessary for cognitive skills.» You can't teach critical thinking on a vacuum |
| 3 | **Memory is residue of thought** | p 49 | What learner **thought about** during a lesson is what they remember — not what teacher said |
| 4 | **Abstract ideas are hard; concrete examples + analogy** | p 63 | Always anchor abstraction in 2-3 concrete cases; transfer requires surface-deep mapping practice |
| 5 | **Proficiency requires extensive practice** | p 71-73 | «Practice makes memory long lasting / improves transfer / enables further learning» |
| 6 | **Cognition early in training is fundamentally different from late** | — | Novice ≠ expert mini-version; expertise = chunk-level different representations |
| 7 | **Children are more alike than different cognitively** | — | «Learning styles» are mostly invalid (Pashler 2008) — avoid wasting effort on style-matching |
| 8 | **Intelligence can be changed through sustained hard work** | — | Direct anchor for Dweck growth mindset (cross-cite §3) |
| 9 | **Teaching as a cognitive skill** | p 106-107 | Teaching itself requires deliberate practice; teachers improve via feedback + reflection, not just years |

[src: willingham-why-students-dont-like-school-2010.pdf table of contents + chapters]

### §1.3 «Memory is residue of thought» — operational implication

If you want learners to remember concept X, design the activity such that they **must think about X** to complete it. Lecture → listener may not think about X → may not remember. Problem-set → solver must think about X → remembers.

→ **Direct implication for Jetix:** Method V2 textbook + Workshop = high-residue. Notion templates passively scrolled = low-residue. Khan Academy practice problems = high-residue (matches Khan 2012 mastery-learning thesis).

### §1.4 Anti-«learning styles» — discipline correction

Willingham emphasises (chapter 7, p 86 «Cognitive Styles») that «visual learners» / «auditory learners» distinctions don't reliably affect retention. Match content type to TASK requirements, not «style». 
**Implication:** don't waste design effort on style branching; spend on event-by-event Gagné coverage instead.

## §2 Hattie — *10 Mindframes for Visible Learning* (2nd ed)

### §2.1 Origin + meta-context

John Hattie — University of Melbourne. *Visible Learning* (2008) synthesized 800+ meta-analyses of educational interventions. Effect sizes catalogued. Hinge point: **d = 0.40** = average year of schooling. Interventions with d > 0.40 are «above average» impact.

«10 Mindframes» = the 10 teacher-side dispositions Hattie found most correlated with above-hinge impact. Listed in 3 clusters [src: hattie-10-mindframes-visible-learning-2nd-ed.pdf p 8-9, 642-658]:

### §2.2 A. Impact (mindframes 1-3)

| # | Mindframe | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | I am an **evaluator** of my impact on student learning | Teacher continuously measures outcome, not effort/coverage |
| 2 | I see assessment as informing my impact + next steps | Assessment is teacher feedback, not student-grading |
| 3 | I **collaborate** with peers + students about my conceptions of progress | Peer review of one's own teaching = baseline practice |

### §2.3 B. Change & Challenge (mindframes 4-5)

| # | Mindframe | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 4 | I am a **change agent**; all students can improve | Belief in changeability of every learner (anchor Dweck) |
| 5 | I strive for **challenge** — not «doing my best» | «Best effort» is too low a bar; design for stretch (anchor Vygotsky ZPD upper bound) |

### §2.4 C. Learning Focus (mindframes 6-10)

| # | Mindframe | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 6 | I give + help students understand feedback; I interpret + act on feedback to me | Bidirectional feedback (anchor Gagné event 7) |
| 7 | I engage in **dialogue** as much as monologue | Anchor Freire dialogics; Mazur peer instruction |
| 8 | I explicitly inform students what **successful impact** looks like from outset | Anchor backward design (Wiggins-McTighe Stage 1) |
| 9 | I build **relationships + trust** so learning can occur in a safe-to-fail place | Anchor Vygotsky scaffolding §5 frustration control |
| 10 | I focus on **learning** and the **language of learning** | Metacognitive vocabulary — Workshop format anchor |

### §2.5 Hattie's hinge: d = 0.40

Interventions Hattie's meta-analyses showed > d = 0.40 effect size (above schooling-year baseline):
- Teacher self-evaluation of impact: very high effect
- Feedback (well-timed, specific, actionable): d ≈ 0.70
- Direct instruction (explicit, structured): d ≈ 0.60
- Reciprocal teaching: d ≈ 0.74
- Spaced practice: d ≈ 0.60
- Goal commitment + setting: d ≈ 0.50-0.60

Below hinge (popular but low evidence):
- Ability grouping: d ≈ 0.12
- Learning styles matching: ≈ 0
- Open classroom (low-structure): d ≈ 0.01
- Class size reduction (alone, without changing pedagogy): d ≈ 0.21

[src: Visible Learning 2008 / Hattie ranking + 10 Mindframes 2nd ed chapter cross-cites]

### §2.6 Implications for Jetix

- **Welcome-frame O-144 + dialogue-of-knowing (Freire §4.3)** = mindframe 7
- Method V2 §A welcome explicit goal + §F drill + §H feedback = mindframes 6 + 7 + 8 directly
- Workshop format МИМ = mindframes 3 + 7 + 9 directly
- Cohort progress measurement → mindframe 1 (evaluator) — Jetix needs a measurement substrate (Phase 6 program design point)

## §3 Dweck — *Mindset* (2006)

### §3.1 Origin + thesis

Carol Dweck (Stanford). Decades of research on attribution + motivation in children/adults. Core distinction:

- **Fixed mindset:** ability is innate + static. Effort = sign of low ability. Failure = identity threat. Risk avoidance.
- **Growth mindset:** ability is cultivatable through strategy + effort + help [src: dweck-mindset-2006.pdf p 374, 422]. Effort = path. Failure = data + opportunity.

### §3.2 Praise discipline

**Praise the process (strategy / effort / persistence) — not the trait** [src: p 314 + Dweck-Mueller 1998 study].

«Smart» praise → fixed-mindset development → avoidance of hard tasks (looking smart > learning).
«Hard work / strategy» praise → growth-mindset development → seeking challenge.

### §3.3 The «false growth mindset» trap (added 2nd ed)

Dweck added a chapter [src: dweck-mindset-2006.pdf p 148] on the misinterpretation of growth mindset. Common errors:
- Equating growth mindset with «being open-minded» (too vague)
- Telling students «you can do anything!» without strategy — empty self-esteem boost
- Praising effort that wasn't effective (effort + bad strategy = no progress; need strategy adjustment)

→ **Direct implication for Jetix:** Welcome-frame O-144 must not become empty cheerleading. Strategy specificity (Method V2 §A-§Q) is the antidote.

### §3.4 R12 paired-frame ⚠️

Dweck growth-mindset rhetoric has been **monetised** by adjacent industries (corporate trainings, coaching) sometimes in extractive forms. **Influence-ethics-expert R12 dispatch:**

- **Use mode:** Apply growth mindset to one's own development; respect Mass paradigm shift O-184 (all rise via strategy + work)
- **Abuse mode:** Growth-mindset rhetoric weaponised to blame victims of structural oppression («just need more grit») — false personal-responsibility transfer
- **Boundary:** Strategy + structure together. Pure individual-grit framing without structural critique = abuse pattern (cross-cite Freire §4.5)

### §3.5 Implications for Jetix

- O-180 «adequate intellect chooses developing methods» = growth mindset operationalised
- O-181 anti-cheating = growth-mindset discipline applied to AI use
- O-184 mass upliftment = growth-mindset at population scale
- Method V2 §A welcome explicit framing = growth-mindset induction (strategy-specific, not generic «you can!»)

## §4 Ericsson — *Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise* (2016)

### §4.1 Origin + thesis

Anders Ericsson — Florida State University. 30+ years studying expert performers (musicians, chess masters, athletes, surgeons, memory champions). **Deliberate practice** is the universal mechanism by which extraordinary performance is developed [src: ericsson-peak-2016.pdf p 491-548].

### §4.2 Three forms of practice (Ericsson taxonomy)

| Form | Description | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Naive practice** | Just doing the activity repeatedly | Plateaus once «good enough» reached |
| **Purposeful practice** | Defined goal + focus + feedback + getting out of comfort zone | Improves toward target |
| **Deliberate practice** | Purposeful PLUS in a field with established techniques + qualified teacher | Reaches expert performance levels |

[src: ericsson-peak-2016.pdf chapters 1-3 + 491-548]

### §4.3 Mental representations

Ericsson's most underappreciated contribution: **experts differ from novices PRIMARILY in the quality + organization of mental representations** of their domain (not raw IQ, not memory, not speed). Chess master ≠ generic intelligence + chess facts; chess master = different chunk structures of board patterns.

→ **Implication:** the goal of teaching = transmit / scaffold-into-being the **expert mental representation** of the domain. Method V2 §I + §J meta-method = explicit mental-representation transmission for «how to develop one's own life» as a domain.

### §4.4 The «10,000 hours» myth correction

Ericsson explicitly rejects the simplification «10,000 hours = expert» (popularised by Gladwell). The 10,000 number was a mean for elite violinists at music academies; it includes deliberate practice quality + qualified teacher. Hours of naive practice ≠ expertise [src: chapter on deliberate practice in everyday life].

### §4.5 Implications for Jetix

- Welcome-frame promises must be calibrated: Jetix Workshop / Method V2 = scaffolding into expert mental representation of «integrated self-development» — not «10,000 hours and you're a master»
- Cohort + qualified mentor (Workshop format) = deliberate practice conditions met
- O-180 adequate-intellect-chooses-developing-methods = deliberate practice discipline applied to method selection itself

## §5 Oakley — *A Mind for Numbers* (2014)

### §5.1 Origin

Barbara Oakley (Oakland University) + Terry Sejnowski — co-instructors of «Learning How to Learn» Coursera MOOC (one of largest MOOCs ever). Book consolidates neuroscience + practical learning techniques targeted at STEM but generalisable.

### §5.2 Focused vs diffuse mode

| Mode | When active | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Focused** | Conscious attention on problem | Tight beam — precise, analytical, narrow [src: oakley p 629-647] |
| **Diffuse** | Mind wandering / sleep / shower / walks | Broad pattern-recognition, novel-connection generation [src: oakley p 638-658] |

Insight: **you cannot be in both at once**. Toggling between them (e.g. Pomodoro) gives both depth + breadth.

→ **Implication for Jetix:** Method V2 design must accommodate diffuse-mode time — solo Notion-staring isn't enough; walks, sleep, exposure to varied content matter.

### §5.3 Chunking

Building **chunks** (consolidated mental representations of a skill or concept) requires:
1. Focused attention to encode
2. Understanding (not just memorization)
3. Practice + retrieval to consolidate
4. Context — knowing when chunk applies

«Illusion of competence» [src: oakley TOC entry «Chunking and Avoiding Illusions of Competence»] = thinking you've learned because you can re-read smoothly. Actual test: **retrieval without source**.

### §5.4 Procrastination — habit + emotion regulation

Procrastination = avoidance of discomfort associated with task initiation. Solutions [src: oakley chapters 5-9]:
- **Pomodoro** — 25-min focused chunks with breaks
- **Plant cues** — prepare environment night before
- **Reward** — bookend with small positive
- **Habit, not willpower** — willpower depletes; habit doesn't

### §5.5 Implications for Jetix

- Method V2 §F drill = chunking practice + retrieval (not re-reading)
- Workshop format = diffuse-mode unlock (varied content, peer dialogue)
- Procrastination patterns = Welcome-frame O-144 must address discomfort + initiation friction (template + first step very low-effort)

## §6 Cross-reference — Make It Stick (Brown / Roediger / McDaniel 2014)

Synthesized from cross-citations in Oakley + Hattie + canonical psych research.

### §6.1 7 evidence-based learning principles

| # | Principle | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Retrieval practice > re-reading** | Effortful retrieval consolidates more than passive review |
| 2 | **Spaced practice** | Forgetting curve interrupted at increasing intervals; long-term retention |
| 3 | **Interleaving** | Mix related topics in practice (instead of blocked) — improves discrimination + transfer |
| 4 | **Elaboration** | Explain in own words; connect to prior knowledge |
| 5 | **Generation** | Try to solve before being shown; failed attempts encode better |
| 6 | **Reflection** | Periodic review of what worked / didn't (anchor Hattie mindframe 1) |
| 7 | **Calibration** | Test one's own confidence vs actual performance (anti-«illusion of competence» Oakley) |

### §6.2 Surprising counter-intuitions

- **Massed practice feels easier but produces worse retention than spaced**
- **Forgetting is part of learning** — spaced retrieval forces re-encoding
- **«Desirable difficulties»** (Bjork) — easier ≠ better; engineered difficulty improves outcome

→ **Direct anchor for Phase 6 program design:** every multi-week variant must have **spaced retrieval cadence**, not just chronological content delivery.

## §7 Cross-reference — Sweller Cognitive Load Theory (1988+)

Synthesized via Willingham + canonical instructional design research.

### §7.1 Three load types

| Type | Source | Designer action |
|---|---|---|
| **Intrinsic** | Task complexity (irreducible) | Sequence simple-to-complex; don't change |
| **Extraneous** | Bad presentation (split attention, redundancy) | Eliminate — single-source, integrated |
| **Germane** | Schema construction work | Increase via well-designed practice |

### §7.2 Working memory limit

Miller (1956) «magic number 7 ± 2» — Cowan (2001) revised to **4 ± 1 chunks** for novel material. → No more than 4 novel chunks per teaching episode.

### §7.3 Implications for Jetix

- Method V2 chunks per §-section should respect 4 ± 1 limit when novel
- Multi-variant programs (Phase 6) — 2h overview must compress to ≤ 4 chunks
- Notion templates with too many properties → extraneous load (cross-cite O-158 Notion-MVP-bypass)

## §8 Cross-book synthesis — cognitive science

### §8.1 5 invariants (across Willingham + Hattie + Dweck + Ericsson + Oakley + Make It Stick)

1. **Effortful retrieval > passive exposure** (Willingham residue + Make It Stick #1 + Oakley illusion)
2. **Spaced > massed; interleaved > blocked** (Make It Stick #2-3 + Hattie d ≈ 0.60)
3. **Feedback timely + specific + actionable > grades alone** (Gagné event 7 + Hattie d ≈ 0.70 + mindframe 6)
4. **Mental representation quality > raw effort** (Ericsson + Willingham principle 6)
5. **Belief about changeability matters** (Dweck growth mindset + Hattie mindframe 4)

### §8.2 Failure-mode catalog (modern)

| Failure | Cause |
|---|---|
| Re-read but fail test | Illusion of competence; no retrieval practice |
| Forget within a week | No spacing; massed practice only |
| Can't transfer to new context | No interleaving; blocked-only practice |
| Plateau at «good enough» | Naive practice; no deliberate practice conditions |
| Avoid challenge | Fixed mindset induced via trait-praise |
| Information overload | Cognitive load — extraneous not minimised |
| «Lecture» but no impact | No event 6-7 (eliciting + feedback); just events 4-5 |
