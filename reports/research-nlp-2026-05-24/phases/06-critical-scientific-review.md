# Phase 6 — CRITICAL SCIENTIFIC REVIEW (MANDATORY)

> **Phase function:** Decide which NLP techniques pass through the empirical filter into Phase 7 IMPORT vs. fall to SKIP-list. NLP's commercial monetization (Phase 4) generates strong incentive for overclaim; this phase grounds the IMPORT/SKIP decisions in **independent empirical literature**.

> **Refutation condition:** If this phase is pro-forma (no substantive engagement with Witkowski + Sharpley + Heap + NRC report + meta-analyses), the entire research output is REFUTED per parent prompt §3.

---

## §6.0 Why NLP empirically tested at all — historical context

NLP emerged 1972-1975 from UC Santa Cruz. By late 1970s, it had been adopted as **therapy training framework** + **performance enhancement methodology** in clinical, educational, and (especially) U.S. military / corporate training contexts. By early 1980s the U.S. Army was researching whether NLP could be used to improve sniper performance, language learning, and stress management.

The **U.S. National Research Council** (NRC) was commissioned to evaluate these performance claims. The resulting 1988 report (Druckman & Swets) became the **first systematic, externally-credentialed empirical evaluation**. Its conclusions shaped subsequent academic engagement.

> **Constitutional note:** This is therapy + applied-psychology empirical territory. The standards applied = RCT replication + independent labs + pre-registered hypotheses. NLP's defenders sometimes argue these standards «inappropriate for systemic/experiential modeling». **Phase 6 holds the line:** any claim of behavioral / cognitive effect that is monetized = subject to standard empirical scrutiny. «Experiential» framing ≠ exemption from replication.

## §6.1 Sharpley (1984) — Preferred Representational System falsification

### §6.1.1 Source

Sharpley C.F. (1984). «Predicate matching in NLP: A review of research on the preferred representational system.» *Journal of Counseling Psychology* 31(2): 238-248.

### §6.1.2 Findings

Reviewed studies attempting to validate the NLP claim that:
1. Individuals have a stable **Preferred Representational System** (PRS)
2. PRS can be identified via predicate use in language
3. Counselor-client predicate matching produces measurable rapport increase

**Conclusions of Sharpley's review:**
- **No empirical support for stable PRS as cognitive trait**
- **No empirical support for predicate-based PRS identification accuracy**
- **Mixed results on predicate-matching → rapport** (some studies showed effect; methodology issues with several)

Sharpley's careful conclusion: «The data do not support the major claims of NLP regarding preferred representational systems.»

### §6.1.3 R12 implication

Promoting **VAK / PRS as fixed cognitive type** = R6 provenance violation (claim cited without empirical support). Indirect R12: extracts authority/fee from learner for false claim.

> **Phase 7 binding:** VAK fixed-type framing = HARD SKIP per Phase 1 §2.3 + §2.4 confirmed by Sharpley 1984. The weak claim («notice predicates, adapt language») is independently supported by accommodation theory (Giles 1973+) — that's the import path.

## §6.2 Sharpley (1987) — Follow-up empirical review

### §6.2.1 Source

Sharpley C.F. (1987). «Research findings on neurolinguistic programming: nonsupportive data or untestable theory?» *Journal of Counseling Psychology* 34(1): 103-107.

### §6.2.2 Findings

Follow-up after 3 more years of studies (1984-1987):
- **Eye accessing cues** (looking direction → internal representation system) — **failed to replicate** across studies
- **Predicate matching → counseling outcome** — no effect on measured outcomes
- **NLP techniques in academic learning** — no effect over control methods

Sharpley's stronger conclusion 1987: NLP is either «**nonsupportive data**» (when tested, doesn't work) or «**untestable theory**» (когда reformulated после falsification to evade refutation). Either way, **scientifically unsound**.

> NLP defenders responded by saying eye-accessing-cues require «calibration to individual» — but a science that requires individual calibration before each prediction is **untestable**, which was Sharpley's exact second branch.

### §6.2.3 R12 implication

Eye accessing cues = HARD SKIP per Phase 1 §2.4 confirmed by Sharpley 1987 + Wiseman 2012.

## §6.3 Druckman & Swets (1988) — U.S. National Research Council report

### §6.3.1 Source

Druckman D. & Swets J.A. (eds.) (1988). *Enhancing Human Performance: Issues, Theories, and Techniques.* Washington DC: National Academy Press. NRC report commissioned by U.S. Army Research Institute.

### §6.3.2 Findings

The NRC committee evaluated multiple performance-enhancement methods (including NLP, parapsychology, mental imagery, sleep learning, hemispheric integration, etc.). For NLP specifically:

> «**The committee found no evidence that NLP techniques as a system improve influence on others (e.g. negotiation, sales) or improve learning, performance, or therapeutic outcome beyond what could be achieved by control methods or by attention/expectancy effects.**»

The committee was particularly skeptical of:
- VAK preferred representation
- Eye accessing cues
- Mirroring/matching-as-influence-protocol
- Strategies elicitation via eye cues

The NRC report = **most authoritative single document concluding NLP doesn't work as marketed**. Cited continuously by skeptics since 1988.

### §6.3.3 R12 implication

NRC report = independent, government-commissioned, multi-discipline panel finding. R6 / empirical authority strong. Effect on this research: any NLP technique whose validation rests primarily on **NLP-affiliated proponents' studies** must be discounted; the NRC's finding sets the priors.

## §6.4 Heap (1988) — Hypnosis context interim verdict

### §6.4.1 Source

Heap M. (1988). «Neurolinguistic programming: An interim verdict». In Heap (ed.) *Hypnosis: Current Clinical, Experimental and Forensic Practices.* London: Croom Helm.

### §6.4.2 Findings

Heap surveyed NLP claims grounded in or adjacent to clinical hypnosis (Milton model, anchoring, swish, phobia cure):

- **«The interim verdict on NLP must, on the present evidence, be 'not proven'»**
- **Single-session phobia cure claim:** no replicated independent RCT evidence
- **Anchoring as durable behavioral conditioning:** generic conditioning is real; NLP single-trial claim weakly supported

Heap was professionally diplomatic; conclusion was essentially «no support for marketed claims».

### §6.4.3 R12 implication

«Cure phobia in single session» = R6 violation per Phase 1 §1.0.1 Andreas-foreword overclaim. Confirmed by Heap 1988.

## §6.5 Devilly (2005) — Power Therapies critique

### §6.5.1 Source

Devilly G.J. (2005). «Power therapies and possible threats to the science of psychology and psychiatry.» *Australian and New Zealand Journal of Psychiatry* 39(6): 437-445.

### §6.5.2 Findings

Devilly grouped NLP with other «power therapies» (Thought Field Therapy, Emotional Freedom Technique, Time Line Therapy) — interventions marketed as rapid + transformative but lacking RCT base.

Common pattern:
- Strong marketing claims («cure in single session»)
- Charismatic founders
- Workshop-monetized training pipelines
- Lack of replicated independent RCT support
- «Anomalies of efficacy» that don't generalize

> Devilly: «The power therapies share a common pattern: dramatic claims of efficacy + transmission through workshop-monetized certification + minimal independent empirical support.»

### §6.5.3 R12 implication

Devilly's critique applies the **R12-relevant structural lens**: tier-monetization + identity-binding + minimal empirical base = pattern across multiple modalities. Phase 4's structural critique of Robbins seminar industry = subset of this broader pattern.

## §6.6 Roderique-Davies (2009) — Education context

### §6.6.1 Source

Roderique-Davies G. (2009). «Neuro-linguistic programming: cargo cult psychology?» *Journal of Applied Research in Higher Education* 1(2): 58-63.

### §6.6.2 Findings

Reviewed NLP application in education contexts. Coined «**cargo cult psychology**» (after Feynman's cargo cult science framing). Findings:
- VAK learning styles claim in education = falsified by Pashler et al. (2009) — the major «Learning Styles» meta-review
- NLP-derived rapport claims in education = no advantage over control teaching
- Many «NLP in education» claims = circular (NLP-trained teachers report NLP helps; no independent measure)

### §6.6.3 R12 implication

«VAK learning styles» = **part of broader Learning Styles falsification** by Pashler+Bjork+McDaniel+Rohrer 2009 *Psychological Science in the Public Interest*. VAK as instructional principle = HARD SKIP, confirmed across multiple independent literatures.

## §6.7 Witkowski (2010) — 35-year empirical literature review

### §6.7.1 Source

Witkowski T. (2010). «Thirty-Five Years of Research on Neuro-Linguistic Programming. NLP Research Data Base. State of the Art or Pseudoscientific Decoration?» *Polish Psychological Bulletin* 41(2): 58-66.

### §6.7.2 Methodology

Witkowski analyzed the **NLP Research Data Base** — the largest collection of studies cited as evidence for NLP, maintained by NLP-affiliated researchers. He examined 33 studies meeting basic methodology standards.

### §6.7.3 Findings

- **18.2%** of studies supported NLP claims (6 of 33)
- **54.5%** of studies were neutral / mixed (18 of 33)
- **27.3%** of studies refuted NLP claims (9 of 33)

When restricted to studies with **proper experimental design** (control group + randomization + blinding):
- Studies supporting NLP claims **dropped to single-digit percentages**
- Most «supportive» studies in NLP database = methodologically weak (no control, no blinding, small sample, self-selection)

Witkowski's conclusion: **«NLP is in fact pseudoscientific»** — uses scientific-sounding vocabulary, claims empirical basis, but fails replication standards consistently.

### §6.7.4 35-year retrospective context

By 2010, NLP had been promoted as effective for:
- Therapy (anxiety, phobia, PTSD, depression)
- Education (learning, spelling, reading)
- Business (sales, management, negotiation)
- Performance (sports, public speaking)
- Personal development (habit change, belief change)

Witkowski: across all five domains, **no domain shows replicated independent RCT support** for the specific NLP techniques (as distinct from generic skills they overlap with — listening, goal-setting, etc.).

### §6.7.5 R12 implication

Witkowski 2010 is the **most-cited single critical paper** on NLP's empirical status. Combined with NRC 1988 + Sharpley + Heap, it establishes the empirical posture: **NLP-as-marketed is pseudoscience**. Generic communication / coaching skills NLP overlaps with retain empirical support; the NLP-specific extensions / framings do not.

> **Phase 6 binding finding:** Any NLP claim being imported to Phase 7 MUST have **independent (non-NLP-affiliated) empirical support** for the *functional* effect being claimed. The NLP framing itself does not carry empirical weight.

## §6.8 Wiseman et al. (2012) — Eye accessing cues direct test

### §6.8.1 Source

Wiseman R., Watt C., ten Brinke L., Porter S., Couper S.-L., Rankin C. (2012). «The eyes don't have it: Lie detection and Neuro-Linguistic Programming.» *PLOS ONE* 7(7): e40259.

### §6.8.2 Methodology + findings

Three pre-registered experiments testing whether eye-accessing-cues (lying = looking up-right; truth = looking up-left for right-handers) predict actual lying:
- **Experiment 1:** filmed lies vs truthful statements; eye movements coded blind; **no significant relationship**
- **Experiment 2:** police trained in eye-cues vs untrained — no difference in lie-detection accuracy
- **Experiment 3:** participants trained in eye-cues, tested on novel videos — **no improvement over untrained**

> Wiseman et al.: «The pattern of eye-movements associated with lying that is widely promoted in NLP is not supported by the data. Training people in the patterns does not improve lie-detection accuracy.»

### §6.8.3 R12 implication

Direct experimental falsification of one of NLP's most-cited claims. Eye accessing cues = **HARD SKIP — settled empirical question**. Continued promotion = R6 violation.

## §6.9 Sturt et al. (2012) — Health-context systematic review

### §6.9.1 Source

Sturt J., Ali S., Robertson W., Metcalfe D., Grove A., Bourne C., Bridle C. (2012). «Neurolinguistic programming: a systematic review of the effects on health outcomes.» *British Journal of General Practice* 62(604): e757-e764.

### §6.9.2 Findings

Systematic review of NLP applied to health outcomes (anxiety, weight loss, smoking cessation, etc.):
- **10 studies met inclusion criteria** (basic RCT-quality)
- **5 showed positive effect**
- **5 showed no effect**
- **Quality of studies generally low** (small samples, no blinding, NLP-practitioner-conducted)

Sturt et al. conclusion: «There is little evidence that NLP interventions improve health-related outcomes.» Where positive effects observed, they were within range of attention/expectancy effects (placebo-equivalent).

### §6.9.3 R12 implication

Clinical health claims (Andreas Heart of the Mind p ~4758, 8545 «AIDS support» — for example) = no replicated RCT support. Clinical NLP applications = SKIP for general Jetix scope (they're clinical-practitioner territory regardless).

## §6.10 Tosey + Mathison (2009) — More sympathetic academic engagement

### §6.10.1 Source

Tosey P. & Mathison J. (2009). *Neuro-Linguistic Programming: A Critical Appreciation for Managers and Developers.* Palgrave Macmillan.

### §6.10.2 Findings (a more sympathetic view)

Tosey + Mathison are academic researchers more sympathetic to NLP. Their **more careful conclusion:**

- NLP **as a coherent scientific theory** = unsupported
- NLP **as a set of practitioner heuristics** = some heuristics overlap with empirically-supported communication / coaching practice
- The **commercial / commodification** of NLP creates problems orthogonal to the techniques themselves
- Some NLP practitioners use techniques effectively; this **does not validate the theoretical claims**, only confirms that skilled practitioners get results from any reasonable framework

### §6.10.3 R12 implication

Tosey + Mathison's nuance = **operationally useful** for Jetix:
- Don't promote NLP theory
- Don't extract from learners on basis of «NLP works»
- Selected heuristics (well-formed outcomes, meta-model questioning) can be useful when grounded in independent empirical literature

This validates Phase 7's IMPORT discipline: take specific heuristics with independent validation, leave the brand + theoretical claims behind.

## §6.11 Grinder + Bostic St. Clair (2001) — Founder retrospective

### §6.11.1 Source

Grinder J. & Bostic St. Clair C. (2001). *Whispering in the Wind.* J & C Enterprises.

### §6.11.2 Founder retrospective

Grinder (one of the two original founders) wrote this book partly as **distancing** from how NLP had developed since the 1970s. Key points:

- **«New Code NLP»** = Grinder's proposed return to original modeling discipline, away from technique-cookbook approach
- Criticism of how NLP became dominated by «1st-generation patterns» (anchoring + reframing + Milton model) without preserving modeling rigor
- Acknowledgment that much of what's marketed as NLP today is **«technique application without modeling»** — i.e. cargo-cult use of the patterns without the discipline that produced them
- Recommendation that researchers/practitioners adopt **state-based modeling** rather than technique-collection approach

### §6.11.3 R12 implication

Co-founder's distancing from popularized NLP = useful triangulation. If even Grinder distances from much of what's marketed as NLP, Jetix should be careful adopting any technique без empirical validation OUTSIDE NLP-affiliated literature.

A technique that doesn't work tempts the practitioner to compensate via covert/manipulative deployment to extract value. The R12 paired-frame discipline + empirical filter act as orthogonal but mutually reinforcing screens.

## §6.18 Phase 6 binding rules for Phase 7

1. **Empirical filter rule**: any technique surviving to Phase 7 IMPORT must cite **non-NLP-affiliated empirical source** for the functional effect.
2. **Citation discipline rule**: never cite NLP as authority for an empirical claim; cite the independent literature.
3. **R12 + Empirical correlation rule**: techniques both empirically falsified AND R12-violating get HARD SKIP (no ambiguity).
4. **Adjacent-better rule**: when validated adjacent literature provides equivalent effect (Bolton vs NLP rapport; CBT vs NLP reframing; Atomic Habits vs NLP swish), prefer adjacent literature.
5. **Self-application rule**: for techniques that survive filter, prefer self-applied framing over other-applied (reduces R12 surface).
6. **Commercial-model audit rule**: never replicate Robbins-style escalating-tier monetization architecture, even with R12-compatible techniques (Phase 4 finding).
