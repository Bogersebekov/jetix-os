## §2.0 Context: Why DACH in 2026

The AI consulting market in 2026 exhibits the structural signature described in the
BIOS-moment research: over 35,000 AI consulting firms globally, 90–92% first-year
mortality, and a latent market for trustworthy, compliance-safe implementation that
no dominant standard has yet captured [src:decisions/STRATEGIC-INSIGHT-JETIX-AI-BIOS-MOMENT-2026-04-24.md#§0].
DACH Mittelstand is a specific pocket of this market where the latent demand is
acutely visible: businesses that have heard of AI, tried it, and hit a hard GDPR
wall. The blocking friction is not capability scepticism — it is compliance-and-trust
[src:decisions/LAYER-5-PRODUCT-DIRECTIONS-DEEP-DIVE.md#§3.3].

**R-5 timing context.** Risk R-5 in the Jetix risk register identifies a Mittelstand
AI-adoption window of 2026–2028. The window refers to the period during which
«AI-native» positioning is a genuine differentiator before competitive consolidation
makes it a commodity claim. Reported AI-adoption rates in DACH SMBs grew from ~7%
(2023) to ~37% (2025) — a 5× expansion in two years. The Phase-1 outreach window
is open; the Phase-2+ moat must be the methodology standard (USB-C / D20), not
the fact of AI usage [src:decisions/LAYER-7-BUSINESS-TRAJECTORY-DEEP-DIVE.md#R-5].

- F: F3 (sourced from STRATEGIC-INSIGHT-MA-DIRECTION R-5 reference; 7%→37% adoption
  figure cited from that document as external market reference; no primary survey
  data in-repo; qualitative extrapolation from visible consulting market trends)
- ClaimScope: DACH region Mittelstand (10–500 employees, €1M–€50M revenue);
  does NOT apply to Fortune-500 or startup ecosystems with different AI-adoption
  curves
- R: refuted if Q3 2026 Bitkom Mittelstand AI-report shows adoption rates
  stalling below 40%; accepted if adoption reaches 50%+ by Q4 2026 confirming
  window-closing acceleration

**ICP baseline.** Primary Phase-1 outreach archetypes per L6 ack: Operator-Founder-CEO
(DACH Mittelstand, €1M–€50M revenue) + Startupper (English-speaking digital founder,
$50K–$500K/yr) — both designated P1A active cold outreach. P1B (High-earner +
Миллионер) = opportunistic referral only. The seven vertical segments below are
analyzed against this ICP baseline to surface candidate hypotheses
[src:swarm/wiki/operations/quick-money/icp.md#Two-tier-Phase-1-discipline].

## §2.1 Vertical 1 — Manufacturing / Industrial Automation Mittelstand

**Description.** Companies in DACH manufacturing, Maschinenbau (mechanical engineering),
industrial automation, and Industrie 4.0 adjacent. Revenue range: €5M–€500M.
This is the backbone of the German economy — the «Mittelstand champion» category
that often operates as hidden global leaders (Weltmarktführer) in niche industrial
segments (e.g., precision cutting machines, hydraulics, specialty packaging
automation). Geography: Baden-Württemberg, Bavaria, NRW, Saxony.

**TAM / SAM / SOM estimate.**
- F: F2 (qualitative; no primary market research data in-repo; general knowledge synthesis)
- TAM (Germany alone): ~230,000 Mittelstand manufacturing firms (Statistisches
  Bundesamt 2024-class; qualitative extrapolation). AI consulting TAM for this
  segment globally estimated at €2–5B by 2026 (IDC AI services market extrapolation,
  not in-repo).
- SAM (DACH Mittelstand manufacturing, €5M–€100M revenue, AI-ready profile): ~8,000–15,000
  companies meeting revenue floor + digital-readiness signal.
- SOM (Phase-1 Jetix; solo-Ruslan; 6–12 months): 2–4 clients from this vertical is
  realistic; no meaningful SOM% at this scale.
- ClaimScope: estimates qualitative; investor-expert C-2 refines with arithmetic.
- R: refuted if first Mittelstand manufacturing outreach campaign (50 contacts) yields
  zero qualified discovery calls.

**AI-adoption maturity.** Early-to-mid. Large manufacturers (>€500M) are already
piloting SAP AI Copilot and Siemens Industrial AI tools; true Mittelstand (€5M–€100M)
is in early pilot or «heard about it» phase. Predictive maintenance, quality control
vision systems, and production planning optimization are the highest-interest use
cases. Documentation and knowledge management (a Jetix primary offering) is often
neglected but presents lower-risk entry.

**Pain points (specific to vertical).**
1. **GDPR + industrial IP dual fear.** Manufacturing secrets (machine specs, supplier
   contracts, process know-how) cannot enter cloud training data. Stronger fear than
   average Mittelstand because competitive moat = proprietary process knowledge.
2. **Knowledge loss at retirement.** German industrial Mittelstand faces severe
   «Fachkräftemangel» (skilled worker shortage) + aging workforce. When the
   Meister (master craftsman) retires, process knowledge walks out. AI knowledge
   capture is increasingly a succession-risk mitigation tool.
3. **Industrie 4.0 implementation fatigue.** Many firms paid large consulting fees
   for Industrie 4.0 projects (SAP, Siemens, Bosch Rexroth integrators) that
   delivered hardware but not sustainable knowledge systems. Scepticism of
   «consulting for consulting's sake» is high.
4. **ERP integration complexity.** AI solutions that don't connect to their existing
   SAP, DATEV, or custom ERP environments are dismissed immediately. Integration
   is a buying criterion, not a nice-to-have.
5. **Production downtime risk.** Any AI implementation that touches production
   processes must be tested in digital-twin mode first; live trials are unacceptable
   to production managers.

**Named competitors in this vertical.**
- McKinsey AI, BCG Gamma, Deloitte AI Studios: present at >€500M companies; rarely
  engage true Mittelstand (<€100M) due to minimum engagement size (typically
  €200K+/engagement).
- SAP partners (NTT, Accenture, Atos): dominate ERP-adjacent AI for SAP-running
  Mittelstand; expensive and vendor-locked.
- AI-first newcomers: Celonis (process mining), KONUX (predictive maintenance for
  railways/industry), Blackbird.AI (German AI advisory boutique). Dozens of
  German AI-Beratungsgesellschaften (SMB-scale) but mostly without methodology moat.
- F: F3 (competitor names from public market knowledge as of 2026-04 / not from
  in-repo primary research; Ruslan's separate market analyses may refine — flagged
  in §6 Open Questions)
- ClaimScope: competitor landscape changes rapidly in AI consulting; valid 2026-Q2.

**Pricing benchmarks.** Larger AI consultants (SAP partners, Deloitte) charge
€200–€500/hr for industrial AI advisory. Independent German AI consultants
(Freiberufler) charge €120–€180/hr. Jetix €150/hr sits at mid-market for
independent rates [src:decisions/LAYER-7-BUSINESS-TRAJECTORY-DEEP-DIVE.md#§2.1].
Productized packages for knowledge-management implementations in Mittelstand: market
references suggest €10K–€40K per implementation project (3–6 months scope),
well above Jetix's €5K–€10K Phase-1 floor, suggesting room on the upside.

**GDPR / regulatory profile.** HIGH sensitivity. Manufacturing IP + employee data
+ supplier contracts are all GDPR-sensitive. Path C (enterprise with GDPR-audit
demand) trigger probability: **high** for companies >€20M revenue; medium for
€5M–€20M. Local-first, client-private KB architecture (D13/D17 locks) is a
structural requirement, not a differentiator — it is the table stake
[src:decisions/STRATEGIC-INSIGHT-JETIX-AI-BIOS-MOMENT-2026-04-24.md#§3].

**Decision-maker accessibility.** Medium. Geschäftsführer (GF) / Inhaber (owner)
of manufacturing Mittelstand is reachable on LinkedIn but with high «noise floor»
(many pitches, low trust in cold DM). Referrals and Verband channels (VDMA,
Bitkom, IHK) are materially stronger. Second-degree warm intros through German
tech communities (CODE_n, Innovation Park Artificial Intelligence in Heilbronn)
may outperform cold LinkedIn in this vertical.

## §2.2 Vertical 2 — Professional Services Boutiques

**Description.** Law firms (Rechtsanwaltskanzleien), accounting/tax advisory
(Steuerberatung), management consulting boutiques, M&A advisory firms, and
engineering consultancies in DACH. Revenue: €1M–€30M. Typically 5–50 staff.
Heavily knowledge-intensive; client data is subject to professional secrecy
(Berufsgeheimnis) AND GDPR. This is the «high-earner solopreneur» extended to
boutique firm scale — and maps directly to the secondary USB-C ICP
[src:decisions/LAYER-5-PRODUCT-DIRECTIONS-DEEP-DIVE.md#§3.3].

**TAM / SAM / SOM estimate.**
- F: F2 (qualitative)
- Germany alone has ~160,000 registered law firms (Bundesrechtsanwaltskammer 2024-class)
  + ~93,000 tax advisory firms (Steuerberaterkammer). The sub-segment of boutique
  firms (>€1M revenue) that would consider AI implementation: estimated 5,000–20,000
  firms in DACH.
- SAM: boutiques with digital-readiness signal + pain about knowledge-management
  efficiency: ~2,000–5,000.
- SOM (Phase-1): 2–5 clients; no SOM% meaningful.
- ClaimScope: same as §2.1.

**AI-adoption maturity.** Mid. Larger law firms (KPMG Law, Freshfields, Allen &
Overy) already deploy Harvey AI and Luminance for contract review. Boutique firms
(5–50 staff) are at early adoption — many have heard of ChatGPT but face hard
professional secrecy barriers. Accounting firms are slightly ahead due to DATEV AI
Pilot push. The D27 fork-and-merge appeal (client forks Jetix methodology template
for their own use) is structurally attractive here — the boutique firm wants to
«own» the system, not depend on a vendor [src:decisions/LOCKS-D25-D28-ADDENDUM-2026-04-24.md#D27].

**Pain points.**
1. **Professional secrecy vs AI capability gap.** A lawyer cannot upload client case
   files to cloud LLMs. A tax advisor cannot upload client financial data to ChatGPT.
   This is legally mandatory, not a preference. The market for local-first, private
   AI is most acute here.
2. **Knowledge management and precedent search.** Law firms spend enormous time
   re-researching questions previously answered. A client-private KB that
   accumulates past advice, precedents, and deal structures is an immediate
   productivity multiplier.
3. **Candidate onboarding.** High staff turnover at associate level; onboarding
   new associates to firm methodology takes 3–6 months. An AI-augmented knowledge
   base dramatically compresses this.
4. **Non-billable overhead.** Boutique professional services: every hour spent on
   admin, formatting, internal search, or status reporting is unbillable. AI
   automation of these tasks has direct revenue impact.
5. **Regulatory compliance tracking.** Tax advisors must track continuous regulatory
   changes (GoBD, Tax reform 2025, EU Directive transpositions). AI-assisted
   regulatory monitoring is a compelling use case.

**Named competitors.** Harvey AI (law-specific LLM; US-first, GDPR gap for EU
clients), Luminance (contract review AI, UK-based), DATEV-AI (accounting-specific,
tightly scoped), local German legal tech firms (BRYTER, Clio German market entry).
Large consulting firms (McKinsey, Deloitte) rarely engage boutique professional
services as clients. Gap: no methodology-first, local-first AI consultant positioned
specifically for DACH professional services boutiques.

**Pricing benchmarks.** Professional services boutiques already pay €5K–€30K per
year for legal research databases (Wolters Kluwer, Beck Online). A comparable
price point for an AI knowledge system with equivalent value is plausible. Monthly
retainer for IT services: €2K–€8K/month for equivalent boutique tech infrastructure.
Jetix €5K–€10K productized engagement aligns well.

**GDPR / regulatory profile.** CRITICAL. Professional secrecy (§43a BRAO for lawyers,
§57 StBerG for tax advisors) creates obligations stronger than standard GDPR.
Client-private KB with local inference is the MINIMUM viable architecture for this
vertical. Path C trigger: **very high** for any law firm >€5M revenue. Path B
(client-hosted) is the natural fit. A Data Processing Agreement (DPA) must be in
place regardless.

**Decision-maker accessibility.** HIGH relative to manufacturing. Law firm partners
(Equity Partner / Namenspartner) and tax advisory firm owners are highly active
on LinkedIn and XING. They are accustomed to B2B services purchasing. A well-
crafted cold DM with a specific pain hook («Ihre Mandantenakten in ChatGPT?
Natürlich nicht — aber wie nutzen Sie KI dann?») has realistic conversion potential.

## §2.3 Vertical 3 — B2B SaaS / IT Services Mittelstand

**Description.** German and DACH SaaS companies (€1M–€50M ARR), IT service
providers, MSPs (Managed Service Providers), and technology consulting firms.
Typically 10–200 employees. Already digitally sophisticated; AI-curious to AI-active.
This vertical partially overlaps with the Startupper archetype for founders at the
smaller end ($1M–$10M ARR).

**TAM / SAM / SOM estimate.**
- F: F2 (qualitative)
- Germany has ~5,000–10,000 B2B SaaS / IT services Mittelstand companies by rough
  estimate (BITKOM ICT sector data, extrapolated). AI-interested sub-segment: high
  density (~70–80% have some AI experimentation).
- SAM: companies actively seeking external AI consulting or methodology: ~500–2,000
  (majority attempt in-house).
- SOM (Phase-1): 2–4 clients.

**AI-adoption maturity.** Late (highest of all verticals analyzed). These companies
are already building AI features, using Claude/OpenAI APIs, and in many cases selling
AI-adjacent products. They are less likely to need a Jetix «what is AI» orientation
and more likely to need AI-augmented knowledge management for internal operations,
or strategic advisory on AI positioning within their own products.

**Pain points.**
1. **Internal knowledge management at scale.** SaaS companies scale documentation
   debt rapidly — engineers, PMs, and support teams all produce knowledge that
   becomes fragmented. A Jetix-style KB architecture directly addresses this.
2. **AI-leverage their own product.** A B2B SaaS founder at €3M ARR who uses
   Jetix methodology to implement AI in their own product (not just internal ops)
   would generate a demonstrable case study.
3. **Sales knowledge management.** SaaS sales teams lose institutional memory when
   SDRs churn. AI-assisted CRM enrichment and sales playbook maintenance.
4. **Competitive market and easier sale.** These buyers understand the value
   proposition immediately; they do not need basic AI education.
5. **Harder to charge premium.** The same AI literacy that makes these buyers easy
   to engage also makes them more likely to say «we can build this ourselves.»

**Named competitors.** Most direct AI consulting competitors Jetix would face. AI-
first newcomers targeting this segment: Obvious (AI strategy consultancy),
numerous DE tech consultancies that pivot toward AI. McKinsey Digital and BCG
Gamma target larger SaaS ($100M+ ARR). The competitive density is highest in
this vertical.

**Pricing benchmarks.** SaaS companies pay €150–€250/hr for tech advisory. External
consulting engagements at €5K–€20K are normal. However, they are also the most
likely to price-compare or attempt internalization.

**GDPR / regulatory profile.** MEDIUM. SaaS companies are already GDPR-compliant
(it is a product requirement for EU-market sales). GDPR is less of a buying
barrier here; the value prop must shift to methodology and compounding knowledge.
Path C trigger: low for SaaS (unless handling sensitive client data as part of
their product).

**Decision-maker accessibility.** VERY HIGH. Founders/CTO/CPO of B2B SaaS are
extremely LinkedIn-active, attend conferences (SaaStr Europe, Berlin SaaS meetups),
and are comfortable with cold outreach. Highest accessibility of all seven verticals.

## §2.4 Vertical 4 — Healthcare / Medizintechnik

**Description.** DACH medical device companies (Medizintechnik, MDR-regulated),
healthcare clinics and hospital networks, digital health SaaS, and pharmaceutical
support services. Revenue: €5M–€200M. Heavily regulated.

**TAM / SAM / SOM estimate.**
- F: F2 (qualitative)
- Germany has ~1,400 Medizintechnik companies; digital health sector adds significant
  volume. Medical device AI consulting is a growing sub-segment.
- SAM: companies specifically interested in AI knowledge management (not AI diagnostics
  which is MDR-product): ~200–500.
- SOM (Phase-1): 1–2 clients only, given regulatory barrier.

**AI-adoption maturity.** Early, heavily restricted by regulation. EU Medical Device
Regulation (MDR 2024 full enforcement) + EU AI Act (high-risk AI classification
for medical AI systems) + GDPR Art. 9 (special category health data) create a
trifecta of regulatory barriers. Companies are highly interested but legally cautious.
The use cases most likely to be pursued in Phase-1 are NON-clinical: internal
knowledge management, R&D documentation, regulatory submission drafting assistance
— not diagnostic AI.

**Pain points.**
1. **MDR documentation burden.** Creating and maintaining the Technical File for
   MDR compliance is enormously documentation-heavy. AI assistance in drafting
   and maintaining compliance documentation is a real use case.
2. **Regulatory watch.** Tracking FDA, EMA, MDR, EU AI Act changes relevant to
   specific device classes.
3. **Clinical trial data sovereignty.** Phase II/III trial data must remain under
   controlled custody — cloud AI tools are categorically unsuitable. Local-first
   is mandatory, not optional.
4. **IP protection of R&D.** Device IP is the company's core asset; cloud training
   data risk is existential, not theoretical.
5. **Long sales cycles.** Healthcare procurement is slow; 6–12 month decision cycles
   are normal.

**Named competitors.** McKinsey Healthcare (consultancy, not AI-specific), Deloitte
Life Sciences, specialized Medtech AI consultants (few; Siemens Healthineers
consulting arm, Philips consulting). No dominant AI-methodology consultancy for
DACH Medizintechnik Mittelstand identified.

**GDPR / regulatory profile.** EXTREME. Art. 9 GDPR (health data as special category)
+ MDR + potentially EU AI Act Art. 6 high-risk designation. Any client-data-touching
AI implementation requires Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA). This vertical
is Path C default — enterprise override — not Path B. Compliance overhead makes
Phase-1 engagement complex for solo delivery.

**Decision-maker accessibility.** LOW-MEDIUM. Medical device company Geschäftsführer
is less LinkedIn-active than SaaS founders. Verband channels (BVMed, SPECTARIS)
and healthcare innovation events (Medica Düsseldorf, Arab Health for DACH firms)

## §4.2 Vertical-Channel Fit Matrix

Scoring legend: **High** = decision-maker habitually uses this channel; cultural + professional
norm fit; likely conversion if well-executed. **Medium** = reachable but lower cultural fit

## §4.3 Phase-1 Bandwidth Allocation Hypotheses

**Constraint:** 5-10 hrs/week total outreach Phase-1 per L5 §3.1
[src:decisions/LAYER-5-PRODUCT-DIRECTIONS-DEEP-DIVE.md#§3.1]. Total weekly capacity ceiling:
40 hrs (20-30 delivery + 5-10 systems/admin + 5-10 outreach). Every outreach hour is
opportunity cost of €150 consulting delivery
[src:decisions/LAYER-7-BUSINESS-TRAJECTORY-DEEP-DIVE.md#§7.2.1].

**Ashby requisite variety lens:** The controller (Ruslan's outreach system) must have at least
as much variety as the system it is trying to influence (DACH Mittelstand + Startupper
acquisition landscape, with 5 distinct hypothesis verticals × 8 channels = 40 possible
channel-vertical combinations). Concentrated allocation reduces variety (Allocation A);
maximal allocation covers variety but risks reaching no critical mass in any sub-system
(Allocation C). The optimal allocation from a variety perspective depends on which verticals
Ruslan wants to learn about fastest — the allocation is a hypothesis about where signal will
arrive soonest, not a commitment to efficiency.

---

### Allocation A: Concentrated — LinkedIn DM Primary

**Distribution:** 8 hrs/week LinkedIn DM only (10 outreach/day × 5 days = 50 contacts/week
at the L6 cap [src:decisions/LAYER-6-COMMUNITY-DEEP-DIVE.md#§7.1]).

| Channel | Hours/week | Contacts/week |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn pre-researched DM | 8 hrs | ~50 outreach units |

**Feedback loop dynamics:** Maximum leverage on the single fastest-converting direct channel.
At 50 contacts/week, 5-15% response = 2-7 responses/week, 30% proceed to call = 0.6-2
discovery calls/week, 20% close = 0.1-0.4 contracts/week. Expected time to first contract:
3-8 weeks. The reinforcing loop (DM → response → LinkedIn algorithm visibility → inbound)
reaches critical mass fastest with concentrated volume.

**Phase-1 traction signal:** First discovery call within 2 weeks of launch; first contract
within 6 weeks. If 50 contacts/week over 3 weeks (150 total) yield zero responses, template
failure or ICP mismatch is confirmed — consolidate and pivot.

**When to consolidate:** If response rate consistently >5% → continue; if <2% after 100
contacts → pause, revise templates, test new segment.

**Risk from systems lens:** High template-fatigue risk in small verticals. If Ruslan targets
DACH Mittelstand Manufacturing specifically, the addressable universe at Phase-1 (1000-3000
companies with right ICP fit) + 50/week cap = full vertical reached in 20-60 weeks. No
variety hedge. If LinkedIn algorithm changes or account gets soft-limited, entire channel
collapses with no fallback. Ashby: controller variety = 1 channel; system variety = much
higher. Fragile to single channel disruption.

---

### Allocation B: Mixed-3 — Balanced Direct + Warm + Content Parallel

**Distribution:** 4 hrs LinkedIn + 2 hrs warm-intro asks + 2 hrs content (weekly video +
lead magnet follow-up via T7.2.16).

| Channel | Hours/week | Contacts/week |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn pre-researched DM | 4 hrs | ~25-30 outreach units |
| Warm intros / referral asks | 2 hrs | 2-4 intro asks |
| Content (video + engagement) | 2 hrs | 1 video/2 weeks or consistent LinkedIn posts |

**Feedback loop dynamics:** Lower LinkedIn volume (25-30/week vs 50) reduces immediate
response count but adds two parallel loops: warm intros (30-60% response when targeted)
and content (compound inbound seeding). The warm-intro loop is structurally different —
it converts faster per contact but has harder volume ceiling. The content loop is slowest
to convert but builds durable inbound that Allocation A does not create. Three loops running
simultaneously: different time constants; different conversion ceilings; some redundancy
(if one loop underperforms, others continue).

**Phase-1 traction signal:** Warm-intro → discovery call within 3 weeks (high response rate
justifies 2 hrs investment); LinkedIn DM → response within 4 weeks at lower volume; first
content-driven inbound inquiry within 8-12 weeks. Portfolio of signals across channels.

**When to consolidate:** After 8 weeks, compare CPL (cost-per-lead in Ruslan-hours) across
three channels. Double down on the channel with lowest CPL; reduce hours on highest-CPL
channel. Data-driven reallocation rather than a-priori concentration.

**Risk from systems lens:** Mixed allocation risks reaching sub-critical mass in each channel.
25-30 LinkedIn DMs/week is below threshold where LinkedIn algorithm begins reinforcing
visibility (rough threshold ~50 new connections/week for measurable algorithm boost). Content
at 1 video/2 weeks is below the 1/week cadence JETIX-PLAN §3.4 specifies. Warm intros at
2-4/week may exhaust existing network within 4-6 weeks. Template fatigue is lower; single-
channel dependency is eliminated; but none of the three loops reaches peak efficiency Phase-1.
Meadows: under-leverage on all three levers simultaneously.

---

### Allocation C: Mixed-5 — Maximum Variety (Ashby Dispersed)

**Distribution:** 2 hrs LinkedIn + 1 hr Email Mittelstand + 2 hrs warm-intro + 1 hr podcast
outreach + 2 hrs content.

| Channel | Hours/week | Contacts/week |
|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn pre-researched DM | 2 hrs | ~15-20 outreach units |
| Email cold (Mittelstand) | 1 hr | 3-6 highly-personalised emails |
| Warm intros / referral asks | 2 hrs | 2-4 intro asks |
| Podcast booking outreach | 1 hr | 1-2 host pitches |
| Content (video + engagement) | 2 hrs | LinkedIn presence + 1 video/2 weeks |

**Feedback loop dynamics:** Highest channel variety; maximum information collection about
which vertical-channel combinations actually work Phase-1 DACH. Podcast outreach begins
building the long-cycle authority loop. Email cold tests DACH Mittelstand response to EN
written pitch. LinkedIn and warm-intro run at lower but non-zero volume. Information value
from this allocation is highest — Ruslan learns in 6-8 weeks which of 5 channels produces
signal vs noise in DACH context specifically. However, LinkedIn (20/week) is well below
critical mass; email (5/week) is a sample, not a campaign; podcast pitches (2/week) will
produce appearances 4-8 weeks out at earliest.

**Phase-1 traction signal:** Data across 5 channels after 6-8 weeks. This allocation produces
a portfolio of data points rather than depth on any single channel. Appropriate IF the goal
of Phase-1 outreach is to learn which channels work DACH-specific (hypothesis testing) rather
than to maximise Q2 €50K gate probability.

**When to consolidate:** At 6-8 weeks: identify top-2 channels by response rate → shift to
Allocation B with the two best-performing channels + content. Allocation C is explicitly
a transitional learning allocation, not a steady-state.

**Risk from systems lens:** Maximum risk of «nothing reaches critical mass». Senge law:
«the harder you push, the harder the system pushes back» — dispersed effort in B2B often
produces linear-zero results per channel (each channel has a minimum viable effort threshold
below which it produces noise, not signal). At 20 LinkedIn DMs/week, the reinforcing loop
may not activate; at 5 emails/week, sample is too small to distinguish good template from
bad vertical. Ashby paradox: highest variety allocation may produce lowest requisite variety
in the controller because each sub-channel lacks the critical mass to receive feedback from
the environment. The allocation that theoretically maximises learning may practically produce
insufficient signal from each channel to learn from.

### Cross-Allocation Consideration: BOSC-A-T-X gate perspective (Phase-1 scalability lens)

The €50K G1 gate first trigger fires **C+S (Composition + Scale)** — the swarm installs
measurement substrate, transitioning from open-loop to closed-loop
[src:decisions/LAYER-6-COMMUNITY-DEEP-DIVE.md#§11.1 G1 gate]. This means Phase-1 outreach
allocation is not merely a sales choice — it is the composition change that generates the
first feedback signals. Whichever allocation Ruslan tests, the critical deliverable is not
the contacts made but the measurement harness: response rate per channel per vertical per
template. Without this instrumentation, all three allocation hypotheses are blind.

**Leverage point for all three allocations (Meadows L9 — delays in feedback loops):** The
current Phase-1 outreach system has no systematic feedback loop connecting channel activity
→ response data → allocation adjustment. Adding measurement (even a simple spreadsheet:
channel / target / date / response / outcome) converts this from open-loop to closed-loop
and is the single highest-leverage intervention across all three allocation hypotheses.
This is independent of which allocation Ruslan selects.

## §5.2 Phase-1 Client-Private KB Delivery Shape

Per L5 §3.1, STRATEGIC-INSIGHT §3/§6, D17, D25, D27, D28.

### What «setup scripts» ship (Path B standard package)

The delivery package is a versioned git repository (`jetix-client-methodology-vX.Y.Z`)
that the client's IT installs on their own infrastructure. Contents:

1. **`jetix-setup.sh`** — idempotent bootstrap script. Creates the 9-entity-type wiki
   directory structure (`concepts/`, `entities/`, `sources/`, `topics/`, `ideas/`,
   `experiments/`, `claims/`, `summaries/`, `foundations/`) + agent config stubs +
   `.claude/config/*.yaml` per D25 Company-as-Code. Idempotency: re-running the script
   is safe (IDEM invariant per optimizer §4.1 — does not create duplicates or overwrite
   existing client KB content).
   - F: F4 (current Jetix swarm uses identical structure; client delivery version is
     not yet validated with a paying client — this is the gap between Jetix internal
     proof-of-concept and client-facing production readiness per STRATEGIC-INSIGHT §6)
   - ClaimScope: holds for client installs on Linux or macOS server; Windows-on-prem
     would require PowerShell equivalent (not yet built; open question)
   - R: refuted if first 3 client installs require >2 hours of debugging `jetix-setup.sh`;
     accepted if first 3 installs complete script execution cleanly in <30 minutes

2. **`wiki/_templates/`** — sector-specific wiki page templates (one set per hypothesis
   vertical). Templates are the Apache 2.0-licensed open surface per D13/D27; client
   KB entries populated by client data are the closed core (client's property, never
   sent upstream to Jetix).

3. **`agents/`** — client-facing agent config stubs: system prompts for KB-assistant
   role (reads client wiki, answers questions, drafts entries). LLM-agnostic: each
   agent config stub has an `LLM_PROVIDER` environment variable that the client sets
   to `claude-api`, `ollama-llama3`, `ollama-mistral`, or `deepseek-distilled`. No
